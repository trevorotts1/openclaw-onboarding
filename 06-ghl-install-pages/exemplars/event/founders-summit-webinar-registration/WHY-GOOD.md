# WHY-GOOD.md — event / founders-summit-webinar-registration

**Register.** Urgent-but-substantive. An EVENT registration page has one job
the other two categories don't share: convince the reader a SPECIFIC TIME
SLOT is worth defending on their calendar, not just that the topic is
interesting. The register leans harder into concreteness of date/time/seat
count than either the lead or buyer exemplar, because the conversion event
is "shows up live," not "clicks a button" — a vague promise does not survive
contact with a calendar decision three days from now.

**Structure.** The hero states the date, time, and format in the same
breath as the promise — never buried below the fold. "Why live, not a
recording" pre-answers the reader's own silent objection ("I'll just catch
the replay") before it can form, which is the single highest-leverage move
on an event page: replay-deferral is the default failure mode of every
webinar registration, and this exemplar's whole "why live" section exists
to name and defuse it directly, not to ignore it and hope. The bullet stack
promises WHAT HAPPENS ON SCREEN during the session (a metric named live, a
sequence built live, a decision made live) rather than what the reader will
"learn" in the abstract — concreteness here is what makes "worth clearing
my calendar for" feel true instead of asserted.

**Qualification.** "Who should show up" filters for people with a draft
plan already in hand — this keeps the room's live-workshop format
functional (nobody wants to watch someone start from zero on stage) and
keeps post-event lead quality high, mirroring the lead exemplar's
qualification move but tuned to what an EVENT specifically needs from its
attendees.

**The close.** The registration line states the real mechanic (300 seats,
48-hour replay window ONLY for live attendees) — this is a genuine
incentive to actually show up, stated as a fact about how the event works,
never as manufactured drama. The closing line explicitly disowns the
"webinar as disguised pitch" pattern, which is the single most common trust
failure in this deliverable category — naming it and refusing it is a
stronger trust signal than simply not doing it silently.

**What a copy-through of this exemplar would look like (avoid this):**
reusing "The Founders Summit," "300 live spots," the exact hero line, or
the three-bullet framing on a real client page. The MECHANISM to copy is:
put the date/time/format in the hero, pre-answer the replay-deferral
objection by name, promise concrete on-screen moments instead of abstract
learning, qualify for the room's actual format, and state the real
incentive mechanic instead of performing urgency — built fresh from THIS
client's own event and audience, never from this illustrative text.
