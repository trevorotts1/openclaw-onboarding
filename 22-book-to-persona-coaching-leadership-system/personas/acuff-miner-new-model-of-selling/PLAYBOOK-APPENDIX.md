> **Orientation for copy specialists (Skill 6 funnel/website copy, Skill 35 social):** This is a *sales* book, not a copywriting book — but its reusable assets are verbal openers, hooks, question funnels, objection rebuttals and close language. In direct-response copy these map almost one-to-one: a verbal "opener" is an *ad hook / subject line / above-the-fold headline*; the NEPQ question funnel is a *VSL / sales-page narrative spine*; objection scripts are *FAQ / risk-reversal blocks*; committing questions are *CTA copy*. Where the source genuinely does NOT cover a web-native asset (opt-in form wireframes, cart-abandon email automations), it is marked **ABSENT IN SOURCE** in Section H — never fabricated. The governing voice rule for everything below: **neutral languaging** (soft/possibility words, zero pressure, curiosity over claims). "We are most persuasive when we let people persuade themselves."

## A — Headline, Hook & Subject-Line Formula Bank

### A1. The Personalized Introduction Hook (Problem + Solution + Question)
- **When to use:** Ad hook, cold-DM opener, above-the-fold headline, "what we do" section. The single highest-leverage opener in the book.
- **Pattern:** `You know how [target audience] sometimes [2–3 generic problems your solution actually solves]? Well, what we do is help [audience] like that [solve those problems], so they can [emotional payoff]. → [flip to a question: "Does that resonate with you, or is it something you might be experiencing?"]`
- **Worked example:** "Well, you know how a lot of companies sometimes get frustrated by losing sales to low-cost competitors? They're concerned about their sales teams inconsistently hitting sales targets and are just really worried about the lack of results from their salespeople. What we do is we help companies like that by training their teams on how to sell using specific questions and techniques that work with human behavior, so they are viewed as the trusted authority in the market, which allows them to charge more and hit their sales targets at the same time. What do you guys do to train your salespeople?"
- **Source:** Ch. on Developing Interest / Personalized Introduction (Part B).

### A2. The Curiosity-Factoid Cold Open
- **When to use:** First line of an ad, email, or video to break the scroll/pattern before any pitch.
- **Pattern:** `[Surprising true factoid that opens an information gap related to your theme]… [pivot into the prospect's world].`
- **Worked example:** "Only 12% of people dream in black and white…" (opens the gap, earns attention, then transitions into the conversation).
- **Source:** Five Principles to Develop Interest (A10); Curiosity/Information-Gap mechanics (F3).

### A3. The Information-Gap Hook (open the loop, then close it)
- **When to use:** Subject lines, video first-3-seconds, ad headlines where you want the click/keep-watching.
- **Pattern:** `[State that there is a knowable thing the reader does NOT yet know but should] → [imply the cost of not knowing it] → [promise to close the gap].`
- **Worked example:** "The biggest problem in sales is the one you don't know you have." (Loewenstein's information-gap: curiosity is an itch created by a gap in knowledge — open it first, scratch it later.)
- **Source:** F3 Curiosity/Mystery/Surprise (Heath, Loewenstein).

### A4. The Disarming Safe-Environment Hook
- **When to use:** Opt-in headline, ad subhead, or opener where skepticism is high; lowers resistance before any claim.
- **Pattern:** `[What we do] doesn't necessarily mean we're right for you. Before we go over how we might help, can I ask a few questions around [topic]?`
- **Worked example:** "This first part is more for us to find out really what you're looking for, just to see if we could actually help you — as there are just some people out there that there's not much we can do for them."
- **Source:** A10 Develop Interest; B6 Connecting Questions disarming frame.

### A5. The "Help Me Out For a Moment" No-Resistance Opener
- **When to use:** Cold outreach (call, DM, voicemail), live-chat opener.
- **Pattern:** `Hey [name], this is just [your name]. I was wondering if you could possibly help me out for a moment?`
- **Worked example:** "Hey Larry, this is just Justin Scott. I was wondering if you could possibly help me out for a moment?"
- **Source:** B3 Cold-Call Script. Note the load-bearing word "just" — it disarms.

### A6. The "Would You Be Opposed To…" Negative-Phrasing Hook
- **When to use:** Ad CTA, cold opener, subject line — removes pressure by inverting the ask.
- **Pattern:** `Would your company be opposed to looking at any possible hidden [gaps/issues] around [area] that could be [costing/causing] you [pain]?`
- **Worked example:** "I called your office to see if your company would be opposed to looking at any possible hidden gaps or issues around your offline marketing that could be causing you to overspend on your ads each month?"
- **Source:** B3 Cold-Call Script.

### A7. The "I'm Not Sure You're the Right Person" Pull-In Hook
- **When to use:** Cold email/voicemail opener; the deliberate uncertainty triggers the reader's brain to pull you in.
- **Pattern:** `I'm not quite sure you're the person I should be talking to, but [reason you reached out + compelling problem].`
- **Worked example:** "I'm not sure whether you're the right person or not, but I am trying to reach the person who's responsible for looking after [problem] to see whether any department in your company might be losing revenue due to vendors overcharging you."
- **Source:** B3, B4 Voicemail Scripts. "Disarming = telling the prospect you're not sure you can even help them, which pulls them in."

### A8. The Inbound-Lead Speed-to-Lead Callback Hook
- **When to use:** Subject line / first line of any follow-up to an opt-in, ad response, or form fill.
- **Pattern:** `Hi [name], you responded to our [ad/offer] [specific time] ago about [specific thing]. You would have seen [recognizable brand cue], and I was [calling/writing] to see whether we could possibly help you.`
- **Worked example:** "Hi Mary, you responded to our online ad two hours ago about possibly starting your own franchise. You would have seen our logo for XYZ Company, and I was calling you back to see whether we could possibly help you."
- **Source:** B4 Inbound-Lead Voicemail.

### A9. The "If There Was A Way…" Conditional-Benefit Hook (replaces "If I could show you")
- **When to use:** Sales-page promise line, ad benefit hook, offer headline — makes the benefit the reader's idea instead of your claim.
- **Pattern:** `If there was a way that [neutral third-party] could help you [get the result they want] without [the thing they dread], is that something you might be looking for?`
- **Worked example:** "If there was an investment that could get you the returns you're looking for, would that be of interest to you?" (vs. the killed version "If I could show you the best investment…").
- **Source:** F1 Neutral Soft Words; B10 Pretend Rule 2.

### A10. The "I'm Going to Make You a Promise" Pattern-Interrupt Hook
- **When to use:** Opener when the reader expects to be pitched; resets the frame and earns a few seconds of attention.
- **Pattern:** `I'm going to make you a promise. [pause] …[the promise that you are different and not there to push].`
- **Worked example:** "I'm going to make you a promise… that I am not like other salespeople you've dealt with. When I tell you I'm going to do something, I'm going to do it. I'm not trying to push you to buy anything. I'm trying to see whether we can create value for you. And if we can't, I'll be the first person to say we can't."
- **Source:** F5 Consistent/Persistent-Action Device.

### A11. The Eight-Second / First-3-Seconds Video Hook
- **When to use:** Short-form social video (Skill 35), VSL cold open, reel hook.
- **Pattern:** `[Pattern-break or surprise in the first 8 seconds] → [open a curiosity loop] → [keep it open with mystery].` "The most basic way to get attention: break a pattern."
- **Worked example:** Open on a counter-intuitive claim — "There is no 'unsellable generation'; there's only an incompatible generation" — then promise to show why everything you were taught about closing now backfires.
- **Source:** F3 (Heath "break a pattern"); H Eight-second rule (Matthew Luhn "You have eight seconds…").

### A12. The Reframe Headline ("The sale is lost at Hello")
- **When to use:** Authority/thought-leadership headline, carousel cover, ad that challenges a belief.
- **Pattern:** `[Provocative reframe that overturns conventional sales/marketing wisdom in <8 words].`
- **Worked example:** "The sale is lost at Hello." / "Telling is not selling." / "Stop acting like a seller and start thinking like a buyer."
- **Source:** G Brand Voice catchphrases.

### A13. The "Does That Resonate?" Engagement Close on an Opener
- **When to use:** End of any hook/ad/opener to convert attention into a reply (DM, comment, reply-to-email).
- **Pattern:** `[Problem statement]… Does that resonate with you, or is it something your [company/family/situation] might be experiencing?`
- **Worked example:** "…so they can keep more of their money rather than wasting it on ad campaigns that just don't work. Does that resonate with you or is it something your company might be experiencing?"
- **Source:** A1 advertising/lead-gen worked example.

### A14. The Subject-Line "Hidden-Gap" Formula
- **When to use:** Cold email subject lines and ad headlines built around an unseen cost.
- **Pattern:** `Possible hidden [gaps/issues] in your [area] that could be [costing you X] each [period]`
- **Worked example:** "Hidden gaps in your offline marketing that could be causing you to overspend on your advertising each month."
- **Source:** B4 Voicemail V2.

### A15. The "There's Money Being Left on the Table" Loss-Frame Hook
- **When to use:** B2B ad/email hook where the lever is wasted spend or lost revenue.
- **Pattern:** `[Audience] is losing [quantified revenue] because [hidden cause] — and most don't even know it's happening.`
- **Worked example:** "…whether any department in your company might be losing revenue due to vendors overcharging you." / "…losing sales to low-cost competitors."
- **Source:** A1, B4. (People buy emotionally; loss is the strongest emotion.)

## B — Funnel & Page Recipes (page-by-page)

> The book's "funnels" are *conversation* flows. Each recipe below is given block-by-block (the conversation page) plus the **web page it powers** so a Skill 6 copy specialist can lay the same logic onto a sales page / VSL / opt-in flow.

### B1. The Master NEPQ Conversation Funnel (the whole sales journey / VSL spine)
- **When to use:** The narrative spine of a long-form sales page, VSL, or webinar. This is the book's central funnel.
- **Pattern (block order, each block = its conversion job):**
  1. **Connecting** — take focus off you, onto the reader; build first-impression trust. *(Page job: above-the-fold empathy hook.)*
  2. **Situation** — establish their current state (max 3–4 beats, no interrogation). *(Page job: "you currently…" framing.)*
  3. **Problem Awareness** — name the problem, its cause, and how it's affecting them emotionally. *(Page job: agitate the problem.)*
  4. **Solution Awareness** — let them articulate the fix and the future "objective state." *(Page job: paint the desired outcome.)*
  5. **Consequence** — what happens if they do nothing for the next 6/12 months → the GAP = urgency. *(Page job: cost-of-inaction block.)*
  6. **Qualifying** — confirm importance, decision process, funding. *(Page job: "is this right for you" qualifier.)*
  7. **Transition** — bridge naturally into the offer. *(Page job: "here's what we do" pivot.)*
  8. **Commitment** — move to purchase with zero pressure. *(Page job: CTA / checkout.)*
- **Worked example:** Time allocation that proves the structure — **New Model: 85% Engagement (steps 1–6) · 10% Presenting (step 7) · 5% Committing (step 8).** "The sale is already made in the prospect's mind during the engagement stage." (Old Model wastes it: 10% trust / 10% needs / 50% pitch / 30% close+objections.)
- **Source:** A2 Time-Allocation Chart; A4 NEPQ Sequence.

### B2. The 3-Pillar Presentation Page (sales-page body / offer section)
- **When to use:** The "how it works" / offer body of a sales page or VSL. Build 3–4 pillars; never more info than needed.
- **Pattern (per pillar, repeat 3–4×):**
  - **Problem block:** "Remember how you said you were having [problem]?" OR "One of the biggest problems people have when they come to us is they [problem + consequence]…"
  - **Solution block:** "So the way we solve that for clients like you is we [how you fix it]…"
  - **Meaning block:** "And what that means to you is [advantage / future state]…" → **checking-for-agreement question** ("Are you with me on that?").
- **Worked example (real-estate investing training pillar):** "Remember when you mentioned you want your first investment property but you're limited with capital, so you'd have to go really slow? One of the biggest problems people have is they don't understand their financing options and how to get into a property with little to no money down… The way we solve that is we teach eight strategies to profit on any deal — Wholesale, Traditional Rentals, Lease Options, Wraps, Sub Tails, Group Homes, Airbnbs, Short-Term Rentals. What this means to you is you're not letting any deal fall through the cracks, not using your own money, so you can scale your portfolio quickly. Are you with me on that?"
- **Source:** C3 Three-Step Presentation Formula. Rules: tailor every time (no cookie-cutter), use case studies showing problem→solution→quantified result, present ONLY the issues they raised, ask 5–10 checking-for-agreement questions.

### B3. The 3-Option Proposal / Pricing Page
- **When to use:** Pricing page, proposal, checkout offer stack. Multiple-choice beats fill-in-the-blank.
- **Pattern (block order):**
  1. **Restate their 2–3 problems** (make them re-live the pain).
  2. **Restate their 2–3 objectives** (the objective state).
  3. **Restate the value** at ≥ **10× the cost** ("a $250,000 solution seems small against a problem costing $15M/year").
  4. **Option 1 — basic:** lower-priced but still profitable.
  5. **Option 2 — core:** the middle offering where most land (the target).
  6. **Option 3 — premium:** high-priced anchor that makes Option 2 look like a bargain.
  7. **Make it a signable agreement** (call it "agreement," never "contract"). Fewer steps = fewer chances to fall apart.
- **Worked example:** "I'd be open to putting together a proposal. To be frank, I'm not quite sure we could even help you yet. Could I ask a few questions about your situation… Would that be appropriate?" → then deliver the 3 options anchored by the premium tier.
- **Source:** C2 Proposal Recipe; 10X Value Rule; Always-Three-Options anchoring.

### B4. The Transition / Offer-Pivot Block (problem-recap → offer)
- **When to use:** The seam on a sales page where you stop agitating and start presenting; the "here's what we do" pivot.
- **Pattern:** `Based on what you told me, [Name], what we do at [Company] might actually work for you. Because you know how you said [their want] and right now [their pain metric], and because of that it's making you feel — I think you mentioned a little bit of [stress/worry]? … This is what we do…`
- **Worked example + DOWNPLAY technique:** Always *understate* their emotion ("a little bit of stress," "a little bit frustrated sometimes"). They fight back and amplify their own pain ("You have no idea the stress, I can't sleep at night") → then: "What's that doing to you?" → "So it's important for you to do something then?" → "Well, with your permission, what we do at [Company] is…"
- **Source:** C1 Transition Statement + Downplay Technique.

### B5. The Commitment / Checkout Block (the 5% close)
- **When to use:** CTA section, checkout copy, calendar-booking step. Never says "close" or "buy."
- **Pattern (block order):**
  1. **Q1:** "Do you feel like this could be the answer for you?" → on yes: "Why, exactly, do you feel it is though?" (self-persuasion).
  2. **Q2:** "Do you feel like this is something you can [use/have] that will get you where you're wanting to go?" → "Why do you feel like it is?"
  3. **Q3 (logistics):** "I don't have anything else to go over — it looks like we covered what you're looking for. Really the next step would be to make some arrangement for your [solution]… Would that be appropriate? How would you like to proceed?"
  4. **Calendar commitment:** "In order to do this for you, would it be appropriate for us to get out our calendars and schedule the next steps?"
- **Worked example:** "Well, I don't have anything else to go over with you. It looks like we covered what you're looking for. Really the next step would be to make some type of arrangement for your [solution] — you can do wire or card… Would that be appropriate? How would you like to proceed from here?"
- **Source:** E3 Three Committing Questions; E4 Calendar Commitment. Vocabulary: "authorize the agreement," never "sign the contract."

### B6. The DELTA Journey Map (top-of-funnel → close, the over-arching wireframe)
- **When to use:** Mapping an entire funnel's stages end to end before writing any page.
- **Pattern:** `DEVELOP interest → ENGAGE in dialogue → LEARN the situation/problem → TELL your story (only after confirming fit) → ASK for commitment (only when appropriate).`
- **Worked example:** Develop = curiosity-factoid ad (A2). Engage = connecting questions / interactive opt-in. Learn = discovery/quiz. Tell = 3-pillar sales page (B2). Ask = commitment block (B5).
- **Source:** A3 DELTA Model.

### B7. Opt-in / squeeze page wireframe, webinar registration page, cart-abandon / upsell-downsell page recipes
- **Status:** **ABSENT IN SOURCE — adapt from B1/B4.** The book teaches no web-native opt-in/checkout/upsell wireframes. Build them by porting the Connecting + Disarming hooks (A4, A5) onto the form, and the Commitment block (B5) onto checkout. Marked ABSENT in the Coverage Map — not fabricated.

## C — Script Bank (sales / objection / follow-up / discovery / close)

### C1. Cold-Call / Cold-Outreach Script
- **When to use:** Cold call, cold DM, live-chat open.
- **Pattern:** `Hi, is [name] there? → Hey [name], this is just [your name]. I was wondering if you could possibly help me out for a moment? → I'm not quite sure you're the person I should be talking to, but I called to see if [company] would be opposed to looking at any possible hidden gaps around [area] that could be [pain]?`
- **Worked example (handling "we already have an agency"):** "Yeah, that's pretty normal, and to be frank, I am not sure we can even help you yet. I'd have to know a little bit more about what you're doing to see whether we can even help you. And if we can't, we can just end the call, or I might be able to suggest someone who could better help you. Would that be appropriate?"
- **Source:** B3.

### C2. Voicemail Scripts (designed to earn a callback)
- **When to use:** Any voicemail or async voice note.
- **Pattern:** `Hi [name], this is just [name]… I was wondering whether you could help me out for a moment? I'm not sure whether you're the right person, but I'm trying to reach whoever looks after [problem] to see whether [pain]. If that resonates, you're welcome to call me back at [#]. I should be available for a little while today.`
- **Worked example (V1):** "Hi Alex, this is just Stefanie Smith. I was wondering whether you could help me out for a moment? I'm not sure whether you're the right person or not, but I am trying to reach the person who's responsible for looking after [problem] to see whether any department in your company might be losing revenue due to vendors overcharging you. If that resonates, you're welcome to call me back. My number is 573-578-9872, and I should be available here for a little while today."
- **Source:** B4. Mechanism: urgency + "I have other options" + "I'm busy, not waiting all day."

### C3. Gatekeeper Script
- **When to use:** Reaching a decision-maker through an assistant/front desk; "treat the gatekeeper as the most important person."
- **Pattern:** `Hi, I'm wondering whether you could help me out for a second? I'm trying to get a hold of [name] and got their voicemail. Would you happen to know if they're in a meeting, possibly at lunch, or even on vacation by any chance?` (You OFFER the answers = zero pressure.)
- **Worked example (if "I don't know"):** "That's not a problem. By any chance, would you happen to know anyone close to their desk who works in their area that would know where they are or when they'd be available?" Plus relationship questions: "Do you mind telling me a little about yourself?" / "How long have you been with the company?"
- **Source:** B2. "Everyone has an invisible tattoo that says 'Make me feel important.'"

### C4. Connecting-Questions Discovery Opener (3 questions)
- **When to use:** First minute of a discovery call or inbound-lead reply; produces the prospect's first self-persuasion.
- **Pattern:** `Q1: When you went through [the ad/offer], what was it about it that attracted your attention? → Q2: Was there anything else that attracted your attention? → Q3: Do you know what you're looking for? (or: What were you hoping to get out of the call today, just so I understand better?)`
- **Worked example:** "Oh, for sure I can go through all the details — but I was just curious, when you went through the ad, what was it about it that attracted your attention?" Plus protective qualifier: "Have you found what you're looking for, or are you still looking for [benefit]?" (99% are still looking.)
- **Source:** B6.

### C5. Situation-Questions Set (max 3–4; "the DNA")
- **When to use:** Discovery; establish present state without interrogating.
- **Pattern:** `What do you [have/use/do] now? → How long have you [had/been doing] that? → What caused you to choose that [originally]?`
- **Worked example (life insurance):** "Who do you use for your life insurance now? → What type of policy? → What caused you to choose that company? → What do you have in place now to financially protect your family when you pass away?"
- **Source:** B7. Frame first: "Our product is not for everyone. The question is who might benefit. Does it make sense to continue to see if what we do can actually help you?"

### C6. Problem-Awareness + "Two Truths" Script
- **When to use:** When the prospect says they love their current provider/solution.
- **Pattern:** `Do you like the [current solution] you have now? → (if yes) That's not a problem. Just curious, what do you like about them? → Anything else you like? → So it sounds like things are going fairly well. Is there anything you would change about [provider]… if you could?`
- **Worked example:** 99% give a *different, contradicting* answer to the last question = the real truth. First answer = "protected history"; second = what they actually want to happen. B2B variant: "Back when you chose to work with that company, what were your selection criteria? In what ways has that changed as you look at your needs today though?"
- **Source:** B8.

### C7. Probing & Clarifying Question Bank (peel the onion)
- **When to use:** Any time you need the emotion under a factual answer; "every rep heard the FACTS; only you heard the FEELINGS."
- **Pattern (probing — surface emotion):** "How long has that been going on?" · "Has that had an impact on you?" · "In what way?" · "What bothers you the most about this?" · "How tough a position did that put you in?"
- **Pattern (clarifying — go deeper):** "Can I ask why you said that?" · "What do you mean by that exactly?" · "How do you feel about that?" · "Why now though?" · "Is there anything else I should know about that?" · "So what you're saying is…?"
- **Worked example (resort dialogue):** problem (cold dinner food) → "How do you mean, exactly?" → "Can you tell me how this affects you when it happens?" → "How did that make you feel?" → "Devastated… I have a family… I can't lose my job." → "So this is important to you to get right?" → "Oh, you have no idea."
- **Source:** B9.

### C8. Solution-Awareness & "Pretend" Script (let them build the solution)
- **When to use:** Get the prospect to articulate the future "objective state" so they emotionally own it.
- **Pattern:** `What have you done about changing this, if anything? → What would you do if you could? → (pretend) How do you see your life being different than it is now? → How would that make you feel?`
- **Worked example + 3 Pretend Rules:** (1) Conditional language — "If you were to go ahead with this, when would you do that?" (2) Remove the salesperson (drop "I/my") — "If there was a way that we could help you get a much higher return…" (3) Remove company/product name — "If you were to start a new internet service program…" (not "If you go with our XYZ Program…").
- **Source:** B10.

### C9. Consequence-Questions Script (build the gap / urgency)
- **When to use:** After solution-awareness, to make inaction feel costly.
- **Pattern:** `What happens if you don't do anything about this and keep doing the same thing for the next 3, 6, or even 12 months? (add "5, 10, 15+ years" for big-ticket).`
- **Worked example bank:** "What would it do to you personally if you didn't solve this?" · "Does that cause you to be concerned?" · "What if it didn't work out for you?" · (existing client leaving) "Have you considered what could happen if switching to a different company doesn't get you the results you said you want?"
- **Source:** B11. Ethics note: empower, don't bully — show them they have the power to change.

### C10. Qualifying & Transition Script
- **When to use:** Confirm importance, funding and decision process before presenting.
- **Pattern:** `If you were able to find something that could get you what you're looking for, what type of funding would you have to put into it? → How important is it for you to solve this? → Why now though? → Time for a change possibly?`
- **Worked example (travel/demo qualifier):** "Let's suppose I do come out for a day, you pull the decision makers together, I do a demo, and everyone finds we can solve this problem — what do you see happening next?" Only travel if the answer is "We'd do business with you for sure."
- **Source:** B12.

### C11. Referral-Engine Script (full ask)
- **When to use:** Right after delivering value to an existing client.
- **Pattern:** `In your mind, how do you feel I've helped you the most? → With that in mind, who do you know that might be struggling with [the problem you solved]? → Tell me a little about this person and why you feel I could help them. → How do you think it'd be best to approach them? Do you feel you should reach out first? → What do you think you should say?`
- **Worked example (referral call):** "Hi, is this John? This is just Jeremy Miner. A mutual associate of yours, Amy, suggested I call you, as I recently helped her with X that was causing her Y, and she mentioned you might be experiencing the same challenges. Is this an appropriate time to talk?"
- **Source:** B5. Referral strength ladder: name only (weak) → referrer makes the intro (strong) → referrer arranges a 3-way meeting (strongest).

### C12. Objection: "This is too expensive / I can't afford it"
- **When to use:** Price objection on any channel (also a sales-page FAQ block).
- **Pattern:** `What do you mean by "too expensive"? / In what way? → (find the cause) Can I ask how you arrived at that figure? → Tell me, if you did have the money, would this be something that would work for you? → Why do you feel it would though? → How do you think you can find the money so you can [their want]? → What other avenues do you have to find the funding?`
- **Worked example:** "A lot of our clients just use their credit card or get some type of loan… Would that help you?" / "…use funds from their 401(k) or other investments… Would that work for you?" Principle: "There is always money" — it's allocated to priorities; make your solution the priority.
- **Source:** D1, D4.

### C13. Objection: "I need to think it over"
- **When to use:** Stall/indecision close-out.
- **Pattern:** `That's not a problem. What's your timeframe on getting back to me in the next day or two, just to see if I'd be available for you? → (calendar commitment) If you have your calendar handy, pull up mine and book a specific time, that way you don't have to chase me down and vice versa. Would that be appropriate? → Before I go, what were you wanting to go over in your mind, just so I know what questions you might have when we talk tomorrow?`
- **Worked example:** The last line surfaces the REAL concern. Root cause: "think it over" usually means your questions failed to build the gap between current state and objective state.
- **Source:** D5.

### C14. Objection: "Send me references / more info" + "I'm too busy"
- **When to use:** Information-request stalls.
- **Pattern (references):** "That's not a problem. What would you like to ask them when you call? → When do you plan on calling so I can let them know? → Let's pretend they say good things about how we solved the same problem — where do you think we go from there?"
- **Worked example ("too busy"):** "That's not a problem. What I can do is give you my number and you'll have to call me back later today to see whether I'd be available. Would that help? What's your timeframe on getting back to me today?" (Positions you as busy/valuable, not needy.)
- **Source:** D6, D7, D8.

## D — Email & Sequence Bank

> **Honesty note:** the book is phone/verbal-first and explicitly warns against emailing proposals ("you're not there to clarify = DOA"). It contains NO marketing-automation email sequences (welcome / cart-abandon / launch). What it DOES contain is a genuine **multi-touch outreach & follow-up cadence**, reproduced below in order, with each touch adaptable to email. Pure email-marketing sequences are marked ABSENT in Section H — not fabricated.

### D1. The Outreach & Follow-Up Cadence (complete, every touch in order)
- **When to use:** Cold-to-booked outbound sequence; adapt each touch to call, voicemail, email or DM.
- **Sequence:**
  - **Touch 1 — Opener (Day 0).** Role: earn a reply. Subject pattern: "Possible hidden [gap] in your [area]". Body skeleton: A5 + A6 ("this is just [name]… would you be opposed to looking at [hidden gap] that could be [pain]?"). Timing: first contact.
  - **Touch 2 — Voicemail/Email (Day 0, same day).** Role: plant urgency + optionality. Subject: "I'm not sure you're the right person…". Body: C2 voicemail script, end with "I should be available for a little while today." Timing: immediately after Touch 1 if no answer.
  - **Touch 3 — The Disarming Re-send (Day 1–2).** Role: lower resistance. Body: A4 ("…that doesn't necessarily mean we're right for you. Before we go over how we might help, can I ask a few questions around [topic]?"). Timing: 24–48h.
  - **Touch 4 — Inbound-trigger speed reply (within minutes of any response).** Role: capitalize on intent. Body: A8 ("you responded… [time] ago about [thing]… I was [writing] to see whether we could possibly help you"). Timing: < 5 min from any reply.
  - **Touch 5 — "Too busy" parking touch.** Role: keep the lead warm without chasing. Body: C14 ("That's not a problem… call me back later today to see whether I'd be available… What's your timeframe?"). If they don't reply at the stated time, wait two minutes, then YOU reach out.
  - **Touch 6 — Calendar commitment.** Role: convert reply to booked time. Body: B5 calendar-commitment ("pull up your calendar and book a specific time, that way you don't have to chase me down and vice versa. Would that be appropriate?").
- **Worked example (Touch 2 verbatim):** "Hi Alex, this is just Stefanie Smith. I was wondering whether you could help me out for a moment? I'm not sure whether you're the right person, but I'm trying to reach whoever looks after [problem]… If that resonates, you're welcome to call me back at 573-578-9872. I should be available here for a little while today."
- **Source:** B3, B4, C14, B5 (assembled cadence).

### D2. Marketing-automation email sequences (welcome / nurture / cart-abandon / launch / re-engagement)
- **Status:** **ABSENT IN SOURCE.** Not taught. Use the NEPQ funnel (B1) to *structure* a nurture sequence and the objection scripts (C12–C14) as the bodies of re-engagement emails — but the book provides no ready sequences. Recorded ABSENT in Coverage Map.

## E — Frameworks, Models & Templates (with the steps)

### E1. The Definition-of-Selling Reframe
- **When to use:** Resetting a founder's/team's mental model before any copy or call.
- **Pattern:** Kill the wrong definition → install the right one. WRONG: "a profession where the goal is to close so you make cash." RIGHT: **"Your primary goal is to interact with a potential customer and DISCOVER WHETHER THERE'S A SALE TO BE MADE OR NOT."** Detach from making the sale.
- **Worked example:** "Selling is the art of finding and solving problems by asking skilled questions and listening for the answers."
- **Source:** A1.

### E2. Old-Model vs New-Model Time Allocation
- **When to use:** Diagnosing why a funnel/call over-pitches; the signature chart.
- **Pattern:** OLD = 10% trust / 10% needs / 50% pitch / 30% close+objections. NEW = **85% Engagement / 10% Presenting / 5% Committing.** Rule: front-load engagement, starve the pitch.
- **Worked example:** "The sale is already made in the prospect's mind during the engagement stage."
- **Source:** A2.

### E3. DELTA Model (great-sales-process spine)
- **When to use:** End-to-end process design. **D**evelop interest → **E**ngage in dialogue → **L**earn the situation → **T**ell your story (only after confirming fit) → **A**sk for commitment (only when appropriate).
- **Worked example:** See B6 funnel mapping.
- **Source:** A3.

### E4. The NEPQ 7(+1)-Stage Question Sequence
- **When to use:** The master question funnel for any conversation or VSL.
- **Pattern:** 1 Connecting → 2 Situation (≤3–4) → 3 Problem Awareness → 4 Solution Awareness → 5 Consequence → 6 Qualifying → 7 Transition → 8 Commitment. Nested types: Probing, Clarifying, Pretend, Checking-for-Agreement, Suppose.
- **Worked example:** Full scripts in Section C (one per stage).
- **Source:** A4.

### E5. The Five Key Principles of the New Model
- **When to use:** Governing checklist for any NEPQ deliverable. (1) Purpose = find & help solve problems (detach). (2) Timing is everything. (3) Listen to what they MEAN, not just what they say. (4) Use the **Commitment Formula** to close. (5) Eliminate sales pressure/resistance. Hub: NEPQ.
- **Worked example:** Commitment Formula = summarize their problems + emotional pain → briefly describe what you do (benefits that satisfy the emotional problem) → ask a qualifying question → ask "why" to make them clarify → toss out a committing question so they close themselves.
- **Source:** A5.

### E6. The Three Destinations (qualifying outcomes)
- **When to use:** Deciding whether to pursue a lead. D1 = no need · D2 = need but no desire to change · D3 = need AND real desire to change (the only one worth pursuing).
- **Worked example:** "The answers they give you are your GPS navigation."
- **Source:** A6.

### E7. The Eight Laws of Sales Intent (mindset checklist)
- **When to use:** Pre-call/pre-campaign mindset. I intend to: (1) have empathy; (2) focus on them not me; (3) find people who truly want what I offer; (4) be seen as different/unique; (5) master the knowledge to be the expert; (6) prepare for every call (for them); (7) use language that resonates; (8) hold an internal locus of control.
- **Worked example:** Pairs with Michael Porter's "be different, not better."
- **Source:** A7.

### E8. The Five Rules of Buying
- **When to use:** Sales-page and offer framing. (1) You sell more thinking like a buyer than acting like a seller. (2) Quality of business ∝ prospect's desire to talk to you. (3) Size of business ∝ your ability to ask questions that provoke thought. (4) High pressure → little exchange. (5) Low pressure → greater exchange + receptivity.
- **Worked example:** "Stop acting like a seller and start thinking like a buyer."
- **Source:** A8.

### E9. Question Architecture: Intent / Content / Condition
- **When to use:** Writing or QC-ing any question (or interactive copy/quiz). INTENT = why you ask (always "to seek understanding," never leading). CONTENT = exactly what you want to know (write it down). CONDITION = how it will LAND (say it aloud: "If I were the customer, would this put me on the defensive / confuse me / feel like a setup?" If yes, rework).
- **Worked example:** Rework "Don't you think you should…?" (leading) → "What are your thoughts on…?" (neutral).
- **Source:** A9.

### E10. Five Principles to Develop Interest
- **When to use:** Designing an opener/hook. (1) Research interesting ways to open (factoid). (2) Use openings that create SAFE environments. (3) Bring value before the sales conversation. (4) Make connections that help the customer. (5) Be crystal clear on what you need to know.
- **Worked example:** "…that doesn't necessarily mean we're right for you. Before we go over how we might help, can I ask a few questions around XYZ?"
- **Source:** A10.

### E11. The CEO Listening Framework (Clues / Essence / Opportunities)
- **When to use:** Discovery calls and mining testimonials for copy. **CLUES** = vague adjectives/adverbs + nonverbal cues that trigger a follow-up ("You said it's *critical* — why that word?"). **ESSENCE** = true meaning behind the words; clarify three ways: Question, Paraphrase, Summarize ("Don't assume, ask"). **OPPORTUNITIES** = hidden openings to build trust at near-zero cost.
- **Worked example:** Prospect mentions a Boston trip → you later name-drop a Boston restaurant. Stat anchors: 95% think reps talk too much; only 14% ask the right questions; reps spend ~70% of the day listening but are ~25% effective.
- **Source:** A11.

### E12. The 3-Step Presentation Formula (Problem → Solution → Meaning)
- **When to use:** Sales-page body / demo / VSL offer section. Build 3–4 pillars, each = Problem ("Remember how you said…") → Solution ("the way we solve that is…") → Meaning ("what that means to you is…") + checking-for-agreement question.
- **Worked example:** See B2 (real-estate) and the marriage-counseling client who went 30%→61% close in 60 days.
- **Source:** C3.

### E13. The 3-Option Proposal + 10X Value Rule
- **When to use:** Pricing/proposal. Restate problems + objectives → state value at ≥10× cost → present Option 1 basic / Option 2 core / Option 3 premium anchor → signable "agreement."
- **Worked example:** See B3.
- **Source:** C2.

### E14. The Downplay Technique
- **When to use:** Any emotional-agitation copy or transition. Always UNDERSTATE their pain ("a little bit of stress") so they fight back and amplify it themselves.
- **Worked example:** "I think you mentioned a little bit of stress?" → "You have no idea the stress, I can't sleep at night."
- **Source:** C1.

### E15. The Six Principles of Asking for Commitment
- **When to use:** CTA/close design. (1) People change more if asked. (2) Questions must be comfortable for both (if you squirm, so do they). (3) Great commitments start with pre-call planning. (4) Make them easy to ask. (5) Commitments are the natural end of a conversation. (6) Asking the *seriousness* of a commitment after a yes lifts sales.
- **Worked example:** The Three Committing Questions (E16) operationalize this.
- **Source:** E1 (Part E).

### E16. The Three Committing Questions (the top-1% close)
- **When to use:** CTA copy / close. Q1 "Do you feel like this could be the answer for you? → Why exactly do you feel it is though?" Q2 "Do you feel this is something you can use that will get you where you're wanting to go? → Why do you feel like it is?" Q3 (logistics) "The next step would be to make some arrangement… Would that be appropriate?"
- **Worked example:** Advanced "black-belt" version: "Where should we go from here?" (they answer "What do I need to do to purchase?").
- **Source:** E3.

### E17. The "Suppose" Question (uncover hidden concerns)
- **When to use:** Objection-handling / FAQ; isolate the real blocker.
- **Pattern:** "Let's suppose we were able to resolve that issue WITH you. I know it's not resolved right now, but let's just pretend we could. Are there any other issues you'd want to see resolved?"
- **Worked example:** "with you" = teamwork; "I know it's not resolved" = respect; isolates concern #1 from the rest.
- **Source:** D2.

### E18. Handling Demands You Can't Meet (3 steps)
- **When to use:** Negotiation / scope pushback. (1) Repeat what they want ("let me make sure I understand"). (2) Include their objection/interest. (3) Decline with explanation (neutral no) + invite co-solving ("Would you like to go over what I think might work for you?").
- **Worked example (restart a lost deal):** "How would I be able to communicate to you that you might be making the wrong decision without you getting upset with me?"
- **Source:** D9.

### E19. Storytelling — Six Principles
- **When to use:** Case-study copy, founder story, testimonial framing. (1) Base it on your unassailable position (questions establish it). (2) Combine facts+features+benefits+questions+anecdotes. (3) Contain BOTH logic AND emotion. (4) Base it on satisfying the customer's needs (not bashing competitors). (5) Engage with body language/tone/eye contact. (6) Have them SEE THEMSELVES in the story.
- **Worked example:** "Stories create 'sticky' memories by attaching emotions."
- **Source:** F4.

### E20. The Counter-Acronyms (what to kill, what to install)
- **When to use:** Re-training a team off legacy tactics. KILL: ABC (Always Be Closing), assumptive sale, feel-felt-found, trial close, AIDA, "it's a numbers game," "be relentless." INSTALL: **ABD = Always Be Disarming**; **DEF = Don't Ever Follow.**
- **Worked example:** "Saying 'trust me' is the death knell of modern-day sales. If you have to say it, your customer is likely to immediately do the opposite."
- **Source:** H Vocabulary; G Brand Voice.

## F — Brand Voice & Brand-Building Language Patterns

### F1. Neutral "soft words" (USE these)
- **Pattern/Example:** "might," "possibly," "perhaps," "it seems like," "I'm not sure, but…," "this might possibly work for you," "this looks like it might be a decent fit." Ex: "This might possibly be a fit for what you're looking for."

### F2. Hard words (NEVER use)
- **Pattern/Example:** Kill "You should," "You have to," "You must," "This is perfect for you," "If I could show you," "sign the contract," "close," "informed decision." Ex: replace "You should buy this" → "This might be worth a look — what are your thoughts?"

### F3. The "just" disarmer
- **Pattern/Example:** Drop "just" before your name/ask to shrink perceived threat. Ex: "this is just [name]" / "I was just curious."

### F4. The "mighty might"
- **Pattern/Example:** Soften any claim or ask with "might." Ex: "what you might be looking for," "this might take some pressure off you."

### F5. "Would that be appropriate?" (the permission close)
- **Pattern/Example:** End asks with a low-pressure permission check. Ex: "Can I ask a few questions first? Would that be appropriate?"

### F6. The conditional "If there was a way…"
- **Pattern/Example:** Make benefits the reader's idea. Ex: "If there was a way to get you [result] without [dread], is that something you might be looking for?"

### F7. Downplay / understatement language
- **Pattern/Example:** "a little bit of stress," "a little bit frustrated sometimes," "a little bit worried." Ex: "Sounds like maybe a little bit of frustration there?"

### F8. Magic diffusers
- **Pattern/Example:** "That's not a problem." · "I apologize, I didn't mean to offend you." · "I'm not sure we can even help you yet." Ex: open a rebuttal with "That's not a problem…" to drop the temperature.

### F9. Negative-selling credibility
- **Pattern/Example:** Lower your own claim to raise trust (Cialdini: "Whenever you say something negative about your product, your credibility goes up"). Ex: "To be frank, I'm not sure we can even help you."

### F10. Vocabulary swaps (reframe the loaded word)
- **Pattern/Example:** "close" → "committing"; "sign the contract" → "authorize the agreement"; "contract" → "agreement"; "tax" → "revenue" (IRS analogy). Ex: "Just authorize the agreement here."

### F11. "There is always money" framing
- **Pattern/Example:** Treat budget as priority, not existence. Ex: "Do you feel the budget you've been given is sufficient to solve this problem?"

### F12. Verbal/engagement cues (presence markers)
- **Pattern/Example:** "Right!" · "Tell me more…" · "Uh-huh." · "Oh, I see." · "Hmmm…" · "Is that right?" Ex: sprinkle into copy/scripts to signal active listening.

### F13. The playful/accommodator tonality (Chris Voss)
- **Pattern/Example:** Likable, charming, relaxed, truthful; inflect DOWNWARD, slow, with pauses. Ex: copy should read calm and curious, never firm or assertive. "No word was ever as effective as a rightly timed pause."

### F14. Curiosity / information-gap language
- **Pattern/Example:** Open a loop, then close it. Ex: "The biggest problem in sales is the one you don't know you have."

### F15. Naming patterns (proprietary mechanisms & terms)
- **Pattern/Example:** Coin memorable named mechanisms: "NEPQ (Neuro-Emotional Persuasion Questions)," "the objective state," "two truths," "the mighty might," "Hopium," "post-trust era." Ex: name your method so it becomes ownable IP.

### F16. Catchphrase / thesis-line patterns
- **Pattern/Example:** Short, repeatable, reframing one-liners. Ex: "problem finder and problem solver, NOT a product pusher"; "Telling is not selling"; "Silence equals sales."

### F17. Pop-culture / analogy voice
- **Pattern/Example:** Rapid, irreverent, meme-soaked analogies (Marvel, Jeopardy!, Betamax/Walkman, "Bye, Felicia," "is water wet?"). Ex: use a familiar cultural anchor to make a sales point sticky and human.

### F18. Borrowed-authority quoting pattern
- **Pattern/Example:** Anchor claims to named authorities. Ex: "People buy emotionally and defend their decisions logically" (Carnegie — 85% emotional / 15% logical); Steve Jobs, Gitomer, Cialdini, Covey for credibility framing.

### F19. CTA / urgency / guarantee language (non-pushy)
- **Pattern/Example:** Urgency via optionality + scarcity-of-your-time, never pressure. Ex: "I should be available for a little while today." · "you'll have to call me back later today to see whether I'd be available." Guarantee framing: "And if we can't help you, I'll be the first to say so."
- **Source (F1–F19):** Parts F, G, H; D1, D4.
