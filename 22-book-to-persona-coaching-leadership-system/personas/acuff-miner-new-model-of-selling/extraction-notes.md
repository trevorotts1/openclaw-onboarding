## LENS A - COACHING METHODOLOGY (items 1-11)

**1. Author authority & origin story.** Jerry Acuff (CEO, Delta Point; top-10 sales expert by Global Gurus 6 years; fired from his first two sales jobs incl. Lipton Tea; 30+ yrs) + Jeremy Miner (founder, 7th Level; NEPQ; trained 143,000+ across 37 countries; behavioral-science/neuroscience at Utah Valley University; 12 years building a "book of phrases and questions"; host of *Closers are Losers*).

**2. Central thesis.** "Selling is the art of finding and solving problems by asking skilled questions and listening for the answers." There is no "unsellable generation," only an "incompatible" one. We are least persuasive when we tell/push and most persuasive when others persuade themselves.

**3. The post-trust era.** Term from Michael Maslansky. Trust at an all-time low; "Google U" buyers are skeptical of experts. Higher burden of credibility, less time to build it. The sale is now "lost at Hello" because a pitch fires a resistance *schema*.

**4. Definition reset.** Selling is NOT convincing/persuading/manipulating/closing. Primary goal: "interact with a potential customer and discover whether there's a sale to be made or not." Beliefs drive behavior; the wrong definition produces the wrong skills.

**5. Problem finder / problem solver, not product pusher.** The New Model salesperson is a "sales therapist": discover the problem, its root cause, and its emotional impact. Detach from making the sale.

**6. The Eight Laws of Sales Intent.** Intent is everything (felt by the prospect): (1) empathy/see their POV, (2) focus on them not you, (3) find people who truly want it, (4) be seen as different/professional, (5) master your knowledge, (6) prepare for every call, (7) use resonant language, (8) internal locus of control.

**7. Self-persuasion mechanism.** When prospects answer skilled questions, they tell *themselves* the problem, cause, cost, and urgency, and challenge their own beliefs ("Why do I keep putting this off?"). Their own words are more persuasive than the seller's.

**8. Tonality & delivery.** Calm, relaxed, neutral, empathetic, slow pace. "Playful voice" (Chris Voss). Memorize questions but deliver naturally like an actor (Clooney/Johansson). Robotic tonality = the prospect feels interrogated. Nonverbals/nodding matter even on Zoom.

**9. Words Matter / "languaging."** Jeremy's coined verb for engineering word choice. Mark Twain: lightning-bug vs lightning. Turn statements into questions ("What if," "Do you think," "If you could"). Neutral words: "authorize the agreement" not "sign the contract"; "revenue" not "tax" (IRS example); never "just sign here."

**10. The six myths.** (1) numbers game, (2) rejection is part of sales, (3) be enthusiastic, (4) sale is lost at the end [it's lost at Hello], (5) assume the sale, (6) Always Be Closing. Replace ABC with **ABD - Always Be Disarming.** You cause your own objections.

**11. Business development / retention.** Five things: maintain base, grow within it, leverage for referrals, create new, "train to retain." Strong referrals = the referrer makes the intro/sets the meeting. "Is training something you did, or something you do?" (Brad Lea).

## LENS B - AGENT GOVERNANCE FRAMEWORKS (items 12-20)

**12. The NEPQ question sequence (the operating system).** In order: Connecting -> Situation -> Problem Awareness -> Probing -> Clarifying -> Solution Awareness (Pretend) -> Consequence -> Qualifying -> Transition -> Commitment. This is the deployable script spine for any sales agent.

**13. Old-vs-New time allocation (the measurable standard).** OLD: 10% trust / 10% needs / 50% present / 30% close+objections. NEW: 85% engagement / 10% presentation / 5% commitment. Governs how an agent should budget a conversation.

**14. Connecting & Situation question rules.** Connecting questions take focus off the seller (e.g., "When you went through the ad, what attracted your attention?"). Situation questions map the present state but are "boring" -> cap at 2-4 in a row to avoid interrogation.

**15. Problem-surfacing trio: Problem Awareness + Probing + Clarifying.** Start with "Do you like the [X] you have now?" (neutral, both sides). Probe for emotion ("What bothers you most?"; non-question probe: "I sense you may be frustrated by this."). Clarify past surface answers ("What do you mean by that?"; "How did that make you feel?") -- surface answers are not the real answer.

**16. The Three Pretend Rules (solution-awareness governance).** (1) conditional language ("If you were to go ahead..."); (2) remove the salesperson (no "I/my"); (3) remove the company/product name (stay neutral) -- so future-pacing questions carry zero pressure/assumption.

**17. Consequence + Qualifying.** Five-star consequence question: "What if you don't do anything and keep doing the same thing for the next 3, 6, or 12 months?" Creates urgency WITHOUT bullying (empower, don't force). Qualifying confirms importance + funding ("How important is it to change?"; "Why now though?") before any presentation.

**18. Transition + Presentation rules.** Transition: "Based on what you told me... You know how you said... and because of that it's making you feel... This is what we do..." with the "downplay" technique ("a little bit of stress") so the prospect re-owns and amplifies the pain. Presentation = only 10%; mirror back ONLY the 2-3 stated problems; three options (basic/core/premium); value ~10x cost; never email a proposal before presenting it live.

**19. Commitment governance (the 5%).** Never "close," always "commit." Six commitment principles. Committing questions: "Do you feel like this could be the answer for you?" -> "Why, exactly, do you feel it is though?" (self-persuasion) -> Calendar Commitment ("In order to do this for you, would it be appropriate to get out our calendars?"). Conditional decisions = decisions; use "agreement," never "contract"; zero pressure.

**20. Objection prevention (vs handling).** Objections are *caused* by assumptive closing and premature presenting. Eliminate at the source in engagement; treat a concern as something to understand with a question, not an objection to overcome. Neutral, two-sided questions defuse resistance ("This is a simple way to defuse the objection of already having a company").
