## A — Headline, Hook, USP & Offer Formula Bank

The governing law of every formula below: **message-to-market match.** Copy must speak to a *specific niche's specific problem*, make a *compelling, risk-lowered offer*, and ask for *one measurable action* — never "we're the best, contact us." Vague, company-centric, everyone-targeting claims kill response; specific, customer-centric, risk-reversed claims win.

### A1 — The Unique Selling Proposition (USP) formula
- **When to use:** The core differentiator on every message, ad, and landing page; replaces "we're the best / highest quality."
- **Pattern:** `For [specific niche] who [specific problem/desire], we are the only [category] that [concrete differentiated promise] — unlike [the generic alternative].`
- **Worked example (illustrative):** "For busy dual-income families, we're the only meal service that delivers a week of allergy-safe dinners cooked and ready in under 6 minutes — unlike supermarket meal kits that still take 45 minutes to cook."
- **Source:** BEFORE-2 (Crafting Your Message)

### A2 — The Compelling-Offer formula (the irresistible offer)
- **When to use:** Whenever you ask the market to act; the offer, not the product, drives response.
- **Pattern:** `[Specific desirable result] + [risk reversal / guarantee] + [reason to act now] — [single clear call to action].`
- **Worked example (illustrative):** "Get your first month of bookkeeping done for free — if we don't save you more than our fee, you pay nothing and keep the work. Spots close Friday. Book your free setup call."
- **Source:** BEFORE-2

### A3 — The "Why Choose You" message-to-market-match headline
- **When to use:** The landing-page or ad headline; answers "why you, over a cheaper competitor."
- **Pattern:** `The [niche] way to [achieve the result] without [the pain/risk the niche fears].`
- **Worked example (illustrative):** "The boutique-law way to settle your divorce without the courtroom war and the $40k legal bill."
- **Source:** BEFORE-2

### A4 — The Specific-Niche call-out hook
- **When to use:** Top of any ad/page; makes the right person feel "this is for me" and filters everyone else out.
- **Pattern:** `Attention [hyper-specific niche]: [the exact problem you solve, in their words].`
- **Worked example (illustrative):** "Attention independent dental practices losing patients to corporate chains: here's how to fill your chairs without discounting."
- **Source:** BEFORE-1 / BEFORE-2

### A5 — The Risk-Reversal / Guarantee line
- **When to use:** Inside any offer to remove the buyer's fear and shift risk onto the seller.
- **Pattern:** `[Try/Buy] with zero risk: if [the promised result] doesn't happen by [timeframe], [stronger-than-money-back remedy].`
- **Worked example (illustrative):** "Try us risk-free for 30 days. If your response rate doesn't beat your current campaign, we'll refund every cent and pay for your next month's ad spend."
- **Source:** DURING-6 (Sales Conversion)

### A6 — The Lead-Magnet / "Ethical Bribe" title formula
- **When to use:** Naming the free asset that captures the not-yet-ready into your database.
- **Pattern:** `The [Number]-[Point/Step/Minute] [Guide/Checklist/Toolkit] to [specific result] for [niche] — free.`
- **Worked example (illustrative):** "The 7-Point Checklist to Cut Your Restaurant's Food Waste by 20% — free download."
- **Source:** DURING-4 (Capturing Leads)

### A7 — The Problem-Agitate-Solve (PAS) body-copy opener
- **When to use:** Opening body copy for ads, emails, and pages; pulls the reader into their pain before the offer.
- **Pattern:** `[Name the problem they live with] → [agitate the cost of leaving it unsolved] → [present your offer as the relief].`
- **Worked example (illustrative):** "You're losing leads the moment they leave your site. Every untracked visitor is a customer your competitor captures instead. Our lead-capture system grabs them before they're gone."
- **Source:** BEFORE-2 / DURING-4 (direct-response copy structure the book endorses)

### A8 — The "Results in Advance" proof hook
- **When to use:** When you can give a taste of the result before purchase to build trust.
- **Pattern:** `Here's [a real, usable result] — for free, before you ever pay us. Imagine what happens when you go all in.`
- **Worked example (illustrative):** "Here's a free audit showing exactly where your funnel leaks. Fix these three and you'll see it yourself — that's a fraction of what we do as clients."
- **Source:** DURING-5/6 (Nurture / Sales Conversion)

### A9 — The Single-Call-to-Action line (direct response)
- **When to use:** The close of every asset; one action, measurable, unambiguous.
- **Pattern:** `[Imperative verb] [the one action] [now/by deadline] to [get the specific result].`
- **Worked example (illustrative):** "Click below to claim your free strategy session before this week's three spots fill."
- **Source:** BEFORE-2 / DURING-6 (direct-response discipline)

### A10 — The Specificity Upgrade (vague → specific) rewrite rule
- **When to use:** Any time a claim is round, generic, or company-centric.
- **Pattern:** `Replace [vague claim] with [specific number + specific outcome + specific niche].`
- **Worked example (illustrative):** NOT "we help businesses grow" → INSTEAD "we help solo accountants add 10 retainer clients in 90 days without cold calling."
- **Source:** BEFORE-2 (specificity beats generality)

## B — Campaign & Page Recipes (square-by-square)

The book teaches ONE master plan — the nine-square grid across three lifecycle phases — and every campaign is a build of one or more squares. Below is the full recipe set: the master grid, each of the nine squares as a build recipe, and high-value variants.

### B0 — MASTER RECIPE: The 1-Page Marketing Plan (the whole 3×3 grid)
- **When to use:** Any small-business marketing build. This is the spine all other recipes hang on.
- **Pattern (the nine squares, in order, with each square's one job):**
  - **BEFORE — turn a stranger into a lead:**
    1. `TARGET MARKET` — *job: choose a specific niche to dominate.*
    2. `MESSAGE` — *job: USP + compelling offer matched to that niche.*
    3. `MEDIA` — *job: reach that niche measurably.*
  - **DURING — turn a lead into a customer:**
    4. `CAPTURE LEADS` — *job: collect the not-yet-ready into a database.*
    5. `NURTURE LEADS` — *job: build know-like-trust over time.*
    6. `SALES CONVERSION` — *job: lower risk so buying is the natural next step.*
  - **AFTER — turn a customer into a raving fan:**
    7. `WORLD-CLASS EXPERIENCE` — *job: exceed expectations, become a category of one.*
    8. `INCREASE LIFETIME VALUE` — *job: grow value from existing customers.*
    9. `ORCHESTRATE REFERRALS` — *job: engineer referrals deliberately.*
- **Worked example (illustrative):** A local HVAC firm niches to "older homes with ageing heating," runs a "$49 winter safety inspection" offer via local direct mail + Facebook, captures non-buyers with a "5 signs your furnace is about to fail" guide, nurtures with seasonal emails, converts with a no-risk guarantee, delivers a white-glove install, upsells maintenance plans (LTV), and runs a "refer a neighbour" system.
- **Source:** Whole book (the 3×3 grid)

### B1 — TARGET MARKET recipe (Square 1)
- **When to use:** Step 1; every plan starts here.
- **Pattern (required ingredients):** pick a *specific* niche (`the riches are in the niches`) → score candidates on the **PVP index** (see §E2) → narrow with the **Bullseye** (see §E3) → describe the ideal customer concretely (demographics + the specific problem they're trying to solve). Avoid "everyone."
- **Worked example (illustrative):** Instead of "homeowners," choose "owners of pre-1970 homes with original wiring in [metro] worried about electrical fire risk."
- **Source:** BEFORE-1

### B2 — MESSAGE recipe (Square 2)
- **When to use:** Step 2; what you say to the niche.
- **Pattern (block order):**
  1. `USP` — the one differentiated promise (see §A1). *Job: why you, not a cheaper rival.*
  2. `COMPELLING OFFER` — result + risk reversal + reason to act (see §A2/§A5). *Job: make yes easy.*
  3. `MESSAGE-TO-MARKET MATCH CHECK` — copy is about the customer's problem, not the company. *Job: relevance.*
  4. `SINGLE CALL TO ACTION` — one measurable next step (see §A9). *Job: direct response.*
- **Worked example (illustrative):** USP + a "free 30-day risk-free trial" offer + PAS body copy (§A7) + one CTA.
- **Source:** BEFORE-2

### B3 — MEDIA recipe (Square 3)
- **When to use:** Step 3; how you reach the niche.
- **Pattern:** choose media by *where the specific niche actually is* and by *measurability* → start with one or two channels, track cost-per-lead, scale winners → favour direct-response channels (direct mail, email, paid search/social with tracking, content, joint ventures, referrals) over untrackable brand awareness. (Decision logic in §E5.)
- **Worked example (illustrative):** A B2B niche reached via LinkedIn + targeted email + a partner's list, each tracked to cost-per-lead.
- **Source:** BEFORE-3

### B4 — CAPTURE LEADS recipe (Square 4)
- **When to use:** Step 4; collect the 97% not ready to buy today.
- **Pattern (block order):**
  1. `LEAD MAGNET / ETHICAL BRIBE` — a free, specific, valuable asset (see §A6). *Job: earn the opt-in.*
  2. `CAPTURE FORM` — minimal fields (name + email/phone). *Job: the exchange.*
  3. `DATABASE / CRM` — every lead stored and tagged. *Job: own the asset, enable nurture.*
  4. `SINGLE CTA` — "get the free [magnet]." *Job: one clear action.*
- **Worked example (illustrative):** "5 signs your furnace is about to fail" PDF behind a name+email form, piped into a CRM.
- **Source:** DURING-4

### B5 — NURTURE LEADS recipe (Square 5)
- **When to use:** Step 5; turn captured leads warm before selling. (Full sequence in **Section D**.)
- **Pattern:** deliver ongoing value across channels (email core + SMS + content) → educate on the problem and your unique solution → build know-like-trust → stay top-of-mind until they're ready. `Don't propose on the first date.`
- **Worked example (illustrative):** A weekly value email + occasional helpful SMS that positions you as the obvious choice when the buying trigger hits.
- **Source:** DURING-5

### B6 — SALES CONVERSION recipe (Square 6)
- **When to use:** Step 6; make buying the natural next step. (Scripts in **Section C**.)
- **Pattern (required elements):**
  1. `TRUST / POSITIONING` — position as the trusted expert, not a commodity. *Job: take price off the table.*
  2. `RISK REVERSAL` — strong guarantee shifts risk to you (see §A5). *Job: remove fear.*
  3. `RESULTS IN ADVANCE` — give a taste of the result first (see §A8). *Job: prove it works.*
  4. `CLEAR OFFER + SINGLE CTA` — one path to yes (see §A9). *Job: convert.*
  - Sell without being sleazy: serve, don't pressure.
- **Worked example (illustrative):** A free audit (results in advance) → a no-risk pilot (risk reversal) → a single "start your pilot" CTA.
- **Source:** DURING-6

### B7 — WORLD-CLASS EXPERIENCE recipe (Square 7)
- **When to use:** Step 7; the first After-phase square, post-purchase.
- **Pattern:** systematize delivery (SOPs) → exceed expectations / engineer "wow" moments → use technology to remove friction → become a "category of one." *Job: retention + word-of-mouth.* (Levers in §E7.)
- **Worked example (illustrative):** A new-customer onboarding sequence with a surprise welcome gift and a single point of contact.
- **Source:** AFTER-7

### B8 — INCREASE LIFETIME VALUE recipe (Square 8)
- **When to use:** Step 8; grow profit from existing customers. (Levers + math in §E8.)
- **Pattern (the LTV levers):** raise prices → upsell / cross-sell → ascend up a value ladder → increase purchase frequency → reactivate lapsed customers → reduce attrition/churn. Pull the strongest one or two levers first.
- **Worked example (illustrative):** Add a monthly maintenance plan (frequency + ascension) and a win-back campaign for lapsed customers (reactivation).
- **Source:** AFTER-8

### B9 — ORCHESTRATE REFERRALS recipe (Square 9)
- **When to use:** Step 9; engineer referrals instead of waiting for them. (Scripts in §C8; system in §E9.)
- **Pattern:** make a deliberate ask at the moment of peak satisfaction → give a reason/reward → make referring easy (templates, links) → systematize it as a repeatable campaign, not a hope.
- **Worked example (illustrative):** A post-delivery "who else do you know with this problem?" ask plus a two-sided reward.
- **Source:** AFTER-9

### B10 — VARIANT: The Single-Square Sprint
- **When to use:** When a business has a working plan but one weak square.
- **Pattern:** isolate the failing square (e.g., no nurture) → build only that square to standard → re-measure that square's metric → leave the rest untouched.
- **Worked example (illustrative):** Plug the "no nurture" gap with the Section D sequence; measure lead-to-customer conversion lift.
- **Source:** Whole book (the plan is modular by square)

## C — Script Bank (sales / objection / follow-up / discovery / close)

*The 1-Page Marketing Plan* is a strategy book; its "scripts" are short say-this lines for offers, conversion, referrals, plus the founder-coaching objection rebuttals. All captured below; longer verbatim call scripts are not in the source (noted in H).

### C1 — The Discovery / qualification opener
- **When to use:** First conversation with a captured lead; qualify and surface the real problem.
- **Pattern:** `What's the single biggest [problem this niche has] you're trying to solve right now — and what's it costing you to leave it unsolved?`
- **Worked example (illustrative):** "What's the biggest reason patients aren't rebooking — and what's that costing the practice each month?"
- **Source:** DURING-6 (discovery)

### C2 — The Results-in-Advance offer line
- **When to use:** To earn trust before asking for the sale.
- **Pattern:** `Before you decide anything, let me give you [a real usable result] for free. If it helps, we'll talk about doing the rest together.`
- **Worked example (illustrative):** "Let me run your free marketing audit first — no obligation. If the gaps I find are worth fixing, we'll talk."
- **Source:** DURING-5/6

### C3 — The Risk-Reversal close
- **When to use:** At the point of decision, to remove the buyer's fear.
- **Pattern:** `Here's how we take the risk off you: [strong guarantee]. So the only question is whether you want [the result] — yes or no?`
- **Worked example (illustrative):** "If we don't beat your current results in 30 days, you pay nothing and keep the work. So — do you want the extra customers?"
- **Source:** DURING-6

### C4 — The "sell without being sleazy" reframe
- **When to use:** When the owner or agent fears selling feels pushy.
- **Pattern:** `If you genuinely solve their problem, not offering the solution is the disservice. Selling here is service.`
- **Worked example (illustrative):** "You know this fixes their cash-flow problem — staying quiet to seem polite just leaves them stuck."
- **Source:** DURING-6 (the book's selling philosophy)

### C5 — The Nurture value-touch line
- **When to use:** Inside nurture; deliver value with no ask to build trust.
- **Pattern:** `No pitch today — just [one genuinely useful thing] for [niche]. Use it, and reach out if you want a hand.`
- **Worked example (illustrative):** "No pitch — here's a one-page checklist to cut your no-show rate this week."
- **Source:** DURING-5

### C6 — The Reactivation / win-back line
- **When to use:** LTV square; re-engage a lapsed customer.
- **Pattern:** `We've missed you. Here's [a reason to come back / a specific offer]. Is there anything that stopped you we should fix?`
- **Worked example (illustrative):** "It's been a while — here's a free tune-up on your next service, and tell us if something went wrong last time."
- **Source:** AFTER-8 (reactivation lever)

### C7 — The Upsell / ascension line
- **When to use:** LTV square; raise average value at the moment of satisfaction.
- **Pattern:** `Now that [result is achieved], the natural next step to [bigger result] is [next offer]. Want me to set it up?`
- **Worked example (illustrative):** "Now that your books are clean, the next step is monthly management so they stay that way — want me to start it?"
- **Source:** AFTER-8

### C8 — The Referral ask (orchestrated)
- **When to use:** Referral square; at peak satisfaction.
- **Pattern:** `I'm glad this worked for you. Who else do you know with [the same specific problem]? I'd love to help them too — and here's [an easy way / a reward] to introduce us.`
- **Worked example (illustrative):** "Who else do you know with an ageing furnace? Send them this link and you both get $50 off."
- **Source:** AFTER-9

### C9 — The "category of one" positioning line
- **When to use:** Conversion/experience; refuse the commodity frame.
- **Pattern:** `We're not the cheapest and we're not trying to be — we're the only [category] that [unique promise], which is why [niche] choose us.`
- **Worked example (illustrative):** "We're not the cheapest agency — we're the only one that guarantees a measurable lift, which is why founders pick us."
- **Source:** DURING-6 / AFTER-7

### C10 — Objection rebuttal: "My product is for everyone."
- **When to use:** Founder resists niching.
- **Pattern:** `If you talk to everyone you reach no one — pick one niche and own it. The riches are in the niches.`
- **Worked example:** "If you talk to everyone you reach no one — niche down and dominate."
- **Source:** BEFORE-1 (coaching)

### C11 — Objection rebuttal: "Niching will shrink my market."
- **When to use:** Scarcity fear about narrowing.
- **Pattern:** `A specific message to a specific market out-pulls a generic message to everyone — you'll win more, not less.`
- **Worked example:** "A sharp message to the right niche beats a bland one to everybody."
- **Source:** BEFORE-1 (coaching)

### C12 — Objection rebuttal: "Our USP is that we're the best."
- **When to use:** No real differentiation.
- **Pattern:** `Everyone claims 'best.' What do you actually do that the customer cares about and competitors won't claim?`
- **Worked example:** "'Best' is invisible — give me a concrete thing only you do."
- **Source:** BEFORE-2 (coaching)

### C13 — Objection rebuttal: "I only want buyers ready now."
- **When to use:** Owner ignores the not-yet-ready.
- **Pattern:** `97% aren't ready today but will be — capture them now or hand them to a competitor later.`
- **Worked example:** "Capture the not-ready, or your competitor will when they're ready."
- **Source:** DURING-4 (coaching)

### C14 — Close / standing reframe: "Marketing is the multiplier."
- **When to use:** Reframing why marketing strategy matters at all.
- **Pattern:** `Your product getting better helps a little; your marketing getting better multiplies everything. Fix the marketing system first.`
- **Worked example:** "A better product adds; better marketing multiplies."
- **Source:** Intro / whole book

## D — Email & Sequence Bank

The book teaches lead nurture as an ongoing, multi-channel value sequence (Square 5) feeding into conversion (Square 6). It gives the *architecture and intent*, not long verbatim drip copy. Captured faithfully below as one complete sequence; body skeletons are patternized from the book's stated structure (fidelity = MODERATE; see H).

### D — The Nurture-to-Conversion Sequence (complete, in order)

**Message 1 — Deliver the Lead Magnet + Indoctrinate (T+0, Email)**
- **Role:** Deliver the promised free asset, set the relationship tone, introduce who you are and what you stand for.
- **Subject pattern:** `Your [lead magnet] is inside ✅` / `Here's your [magnet] — plus a quick note`
- **Body skeleton:** `[Warm thanks] → [Download link, repeated] → [1-sentence "best way to use it"] → [Who we are + who we help (niche) + our USP in a line] → [soft "reply if you want a hand"].`
- **Timing:** Immediately on opt-in.

**Message 2 — Pure Value, No Pitch (T+2 days, Email)**
- **Role:** Build know-like-trust by helping with zero ask.
- **Body skeleton:** `[One genuinely useful tip/resource for the niche] → [why it matters] → [no CTA beyond "hope this helps"].` (Mirrors §C5.)
- **Timing:** 2 days after Message 1.

**Message 3 — Problem + Story + Soft Bridge (T+5 days, Email)**
- **Role:** Agitate the core problem with a relatable story, then bridge to how you solve it.
- **Subject pattern:** `[Curiosity/problem hook from §A]`
- **Body skeleton:** `[Short relatable story of someone in the niche with the problem] → [the cost of leaving it unsolved (PAS, §A7)] → [your unique approach] → [soft CTA: "want me to show you how?"].`
- **Timing:** 3 days after Message 2.

**Message 4 — Higher-Open Channel Nudge (T+7 days, SMS / Text)**
- **Role:** Re-engage on a higher-open channel; one idea, one link.
- **Opener pattern:** `[First name] — quick one: did the [magnet] help? If you want the shortcut to [result], reply YES.`
- **Timing:** 2 days after Message 3.

**Message 5 — Results in Advance + The Offer (T+10 days, Email)**
- **Role:** Give a taste of the result, then make the compelling, risk-reversed offer (move to Square 6).
- **Body skeleton:** `[Deliver/offer a free taste of the result (§A8)] → [the compelling offer + risk reversal (§A2/§A5)] → [reason to act now] → [single clear CTA (§A9)].`
- **Timing:** 3 days after Message 4.

**Messages 6…N — Ongoing Value + Periodic Offers (cadence: as long as you keep delivering value)**
- **Role:** Stay top-of-mind until the buying trigger hits; alternate value touches with periodic offers.
- **Pattern:** `[New value content] + [occasional soft ascension to the next value-ladder rung]`, repeated across email/SMS/content. Each lead may take weeks or months to be ready — keep nurturing.
- **Timing:** Recurring.

**Broadcasts — Time-Specific (layered over the sequence)**
- **Role:** Event-driven promotion over the evergreen nurture (seasonal offer, webinar, launch, deadline).
- **Pattern:** `[Specific offer + deadline]` to the segment, with one CTA.
- **Timing:** As events occur.

## E — Frameworks, Models & Templates (with the steps)

Every named model in the book, reproduced with its internal steps + a worked example. Numbers in examples are illustrative unless they are part of a named formula.

### E1 — The 1-Page Marketing Plan (the 3×3 / nine-square grid)
- **Steps:** BEFORE (1 Target Market → 2 Message → 3 Media) → DURING (4 Capture Leads → 5 Nurture Leads → 6 Sales Conversion) → AFTER (7 World-Class Experience → 8 Increase Lifetime Value → 9 Orchestrate Referrals). Each square has one job; fill all nine on a single page. (Full block-level recipe of each square in **Section B**.)
- **Worked example:** See B0 (HVAC firm walked through all nine squares).
- **Source:** Whole book

### E2 — The PVP Index (niche selection)
- **Steps:** For each candidate niche, score 0–10 on **P**ersonal fulfilment (do you enjoy serving them?), **V**alue to the marketplace (how much do they value the result?), and **P**rofitability (how profitable are they?). Sum the three; choose the highest-scoring niche to commit to.
- **Worked example (illustrative):** Niche A scores P8/V6/P5 = 19; Niche B scores P6/V9/P9 = 24 → commit to Niche B.
- **Source:** BEFORE-1

### E3 — The Bullseye (narrowing the target market)
- **Steps:** Picture concentric rings; your *ideal* customer sits at the dead center. Work from a broad market inward to the precise bullseye — the specific segment you can dominate — then aim all message and media at that center rather than the whole board.
- **Worked example (illustrative):** "homeowners" (outer) → "older homes" → "pre-1970 homes with original wiring worried about fire risk" (bullseye).
- **Source:** BEFORE-1

### E4 — The USP + Compelling Offer
- **Steps:** (1) Identify the niche's specific problem. (2) Write a differentiated USP (only-we promise) — see §A1. (3) Build a compelling offer = desirable result + risk reversal + reason to act — see §A2/§A5. (4) Attach one clear CTA. Avoid "best/quality" claims and "contact us."
- **Worked example (illustrative):** See A1 + A2 (meal-service USP + free-first-month risk-reversed offer).
- **Source:** BEFORE-2

### E5 — Direct Response vs Brand/Mass Marketing (the media filter)
- **Steps:** For a small business, choose **direct-response** over brand awareness: marketing must be (1) trackable, (2) measurable, (3) aimed at a specific niche, (4) carry a compelling offer, (5) have a single call to action, (6) capture leads, and (7) follow up. Pick media by where the niche is and by measurability; scale by cost-per-lead and return.
- **Worked example (illustrative):** Choose a trackable email/paid channel over an untrackable billboard for a small budget.
- **Source:** BEFORE-3 / Intro ("hammer to put in a screw")

### E6 — Lead Capture & the "Ethical Bribe" (lead magnet)
- **Steps:** Recognize ~97% of the market isn't ready to buy now. Offer a specific, valuable free asset (the ethical bribe / lead magnet — see §A6) in exchange for contact details; store every lead in a database/CRM; this converts fleeting attention into an owned, nurturable asset.
- **Worked example (illustrative):** A "free 7-point checklist" gating name+email into the CRM.
- **Source:** DURING-4

### E7 — World-Class Experience Levers (becoming a "category of one")
- **Steps:** (1) Systematize delivery with SOPs so quality is repeatable. (2) Exceed expectations / engineer "wow" moments. (3) Use technology to remove friction. (4) Differentiate so completely you're a "category of one" with no real comparison.
- **Worked example (illustrative):** Standardized onboarding + a surprise welcome gift + a frictionless self-serve portal.
- **Source:** AFTER-7

### E8 — Increasing Lifetime Value (the LTV levers + Jay Abraham's three ways)
- **Steps:** Grow value from existing customers via: **raise prices**, **upsell/cross-sell**, **ascend** up a value ladder, **increase frequency**, **reactivate** lapsed customers, **reduce attrition/churn**. This maps to the three ways to grow any business: (1) increase the number of customers, (2) increase average transaction value, (3) increase frequency of transactions. LTV = average transaction value × frequency × customer lifespan.
- **Worked example (illustrative):** Raising price 10% + adding a monthly plan (frequency) + a win-back campaign (reactivation) compounds revenue without new leads.
- **Source:** AFTER-8

### E9 — Orchestrating & Stimulating Referrals (the referral system)
- **Steps:** (1) Don't wait — ask deliberately at peak satisfaction. (2) Give a reason or two-sided reward. (3) Make referring effortless (templates, links, introductions). (4) Build it as a repeatable system, not a one-off hope. (5) Stimulate word-of-mouth via remarkable experience (ties to E7).
- **Worked example (illustrative):** See B9 + C8 (two-sided "$50 off" referral link after delivery).
- **Source:** AFTER-9

### E10 — The Value Ladder / Ascension Model
- **Steps:** Map offers from free (lead magnet) → low-ticket → core → high-ticket; many customers enter low and ascend over time. Each rung raises LTV and deepens the relationship.
- **Worked example (illustrative):** Free checklist → $49 inspection → core service → annual maintenance plan.
- **Source:** AFTER-8 / DURING-5 (ascension across the lifecycle)

### E11 — "Ready, Fire, Aim" (execution doctrine)
- **Steps:** Fill the one-page plan in pencil and act before it's perfect; launch, measure the response, then adjust ("aim") based on real data. Done beats perfect; iteration beats analysis paralysis.
- **Worked example (illustrative):** Ship the first offer to a small segment, read the response rate, refine the offer, scale.
- **Source:** Whole book (execution philosophy)

### E12 — Marketing Metrics (what to measure)
- **Steps:** Track the numbers that make marketing accountable: **cost per lead**, **conversion rate** (lead → customer), **average transaction value**, **purchase frequency**, **customer lifetime value**, **return on marketing investment**, and **referral rate**. Decisions follow the numbers, not opinion.
- **Worked example (illustrative):** If cost-per-lead is $20 and lead→customer is 10% at $600 LTV, each customer costs $200 in leads against $600 LTV — scale it.
- **Source:** BEFORE-3 / DURING / AFTER (direct-response measurement throughout)

> **Section E: full framework set captured — 12 named frameworks/models, each with internal steps.** Source richness: **RICH** — the strategy frameworks are the book's core and most reusable assets.

## F — Brand Voice & Brand-Building Language Patterns

Allan Dib's reusable voice machinery, as ready-to-use lists with example lines. This is what lets a copy specialist write ON-brand and richly instead of thinly.

### F1 — The sharp analogy (assumption-breaker)
- `Using big-brand marketing in a small business is like using a hammer to put in a screw.` / `Selling to a cold lead is like proposing marriage on a first date.`

### F2 — The "riches are in the niches" specificity drumbeat
- `The riches are in the niches.` / `If you're talking to everyone, you're talking to no one.`

### F3 — Customer-problem framing (not company-praise)
- Open with the customer's problem, never the company: `You're losing leads the moment they leave your site…` not `We are a leading provider of…`.

### F4 — The direct-response checklist cadence
- List what makes marketing accountable: `trackable, measurable, niche-targeted, offer-driven, single call to action, captures leads, follows up.`

### F5 — "Ready, fire, aim" permission-to-ship language
- `Done beats perfect.` / `Fill it in pencil and go — you'll aim once you see real data.`

### F6 — Power-word bank (use)
- Specific · Measurable · Niche · Compelling offer · Risk-free · Guaranteed · Free · Results in advance · Category of one · Raving fans · Lifetime value · Direct response · Trackable.
- Example: `a compelling, risk-free offer for a specific niche`.

### F7 — Words/phrases to AVOID
- "Everyone / anyone who needs us," "we're the best / highest quality / world-class" (as the *only* claim), "build brand awareness," "get our name out there," vague "great service," "contact us for a quote" (as the only CTA).

### F8 — Risk-reversal / guarantee language
- `Zero risk.` / `If it doesn't [result] by [date], you pay nothing — and we'll [stronger remedy].`

### F9 — The "category of one" positioning line
- `We're not the cheapest and we're not trying to be — we're the only [category] that [unique promise].`

### F10 — Specificity-over-vagueness rewrite tic
- Always swap round/vague for exact: `"help businesses grow"` → `"add 10 retainer clients in 90 days for solo accountants."`

### F11 — The "97% aren't ready" capture frame
- `Most of your market isn't ready to buy today — so capture them, don't chase them.`

### F12 — The lifecycle / three-phase frame
- `Before, During, After: stranger → lead → customer → raving fan.`

### F13 — Single-call-to-action discipline
- `One asset, one action.` Every piece ends with exactly one measurable CTA.

### F14 — Naming patterns (offers/lead magnets)
- `The [N]-[Point/Step/Minute] [Guide/Checklist/Toolkit] to [result] for [niche]` · `The [niche] [Blueprint/Playbook] to [result]`.
- Example: "The 7-Point Checklist to Cut Food Waste."

### F15 — Anti-fluff, blunt operator tone
- Talk to the time-starved owner: short sentences, no agency jargon, no buzzwords. `Skip the branding theatre — what's your offer, and can you measure it?`

### F16 — The "marketing is the multiplier" mantra
- `A better product adds; better marketing multiplies.` / `Marketing is the single biggest lever you have.`

### F17 — "Sell without being sleazy" service framing
- `If you genuinely solve their problem, not offering the solution is the disservice. Selling is service.`

### F18 — The After-phase / "money is in the After" emphasis
- `The first sale is the start, not the finish — the profit is in experience, lifetime value, and referrals.`

> **Section F count: 18 brand-building language patterns, each with an example line** (floor was 15). Source richness: **RICH** — Dib's voice (blunt, analogy-driven, specificity-obsessed) is distinct and well-evidenced across the book.
