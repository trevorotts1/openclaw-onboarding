## LENS A — COACHING METHODOLOGY (items 1–11)

### 1. Core definition / north star
"Sales copywriting is anything intended to persuade the right reader, viewer, or listener to take a specific action." (Secret #1) Copywriting is not literary writing — it is selling in print. Its only scorecard is action taken: a click, an opt-in, a call, a purchase. Edwards' frame: "In copywriting, there's no prize for second place. Your copy either works or you starve." (Secret #1)

### 2. The mindset shift Edwards coaches first
"People love to buy; they hate to be sold." Copy doesn't trick people into doing what they don't want — it helps people buy what they already want to buy. The owner's job is to be "a great communicator about why they need what you have." Anyone can learn this; nobody is born knowing it ("Nobody is born writing sales copy"). Path to mastery: "try → bad → good → great." Be a *student* of copywriting, never an "expert" (the moment you call yourself an expert you stop asking the questions that keep you selling). (Secrets #1, #2, "Everything Else")

### 3. Ten Reasons People Buy (Secret #3) — the master "why" lens
People don't buy without a reason why. The ten: (1) Make money (2) Save money (3) Save time (4) Avoid effort (5) Escape mental or physical pain (6) Get more comfort (7) Cleanliness/hygiene → better health (8) Gain praise (9) Feel more loved (10) Increase popularity / social status. The first five are the strongest "pegs." Tie *multiple* reasons to one product — like tying down a tarp in a windstorm at 2–5 corners instead of one. Don't default to only "make money / save money."

### 4. FRED — the avatar (Secret #8)
FRED = Fears, Results, Expectations, Desires. Give your avatar a name and a face. Drill niche → sub-niche → micro-niche (dartboard: rim → inner rim → bullseye). Use **psychographics first, demographics to refine**. "People buy what they WANT, not what they NEED" — sell the want, include the need in delivery. PQR2 = Problems, Questions, Roadblocks, Results — the four things inside Fred's head. The chasm/bridge image: Fred stands on one cliff (problems/questions/roadblocks), the results he wants are on the far cliff; **your sales copy is the bridge** (not the product). "Enter the conversation already going on inside his head."

### 5. It's all about THEM, never about you (Secrets #4, #13)
"Nobody cares about you in your sales copy." The hack: hunt for "I, me, my, we, ours" and convert to "you, your, yours." Lead with the customer's problem/future/desire; introduce your product only after you've talked about them. Prospects want to be the hero of the message.

### 6. Emotion is what really sells — "Meaning creates emotion" (Secret #10)
People buy on emotion and justify with logic (Carnegie/Bonaparte epigraphs). After every feature/benefit, ask "Why does that matter? What does that mean?" Use the two magic words **"which means…"** to reach the felt outcome. Tie product to core drivers: love, hate, fear, vanity, pride, longing, greed, freedom. The emotional connection turns lookers→buyers→raving fans→customers for life.

### 7. Headlines are the #1 skill (Secret #6)
8 of 10 read the headline, 2 of 10 read the rest. A great headline + mediocre copy beats excellent copy + a weak headline ("gets you up to bat more often"). Headline connects emotionally around a **fear or a desire** and targets the *right* people (and repels the wrong ones). Edwards spends up to **50% of total project time on the headline**, then **TESTS** it (his own "infinite increase" story: bankrupt-trailer-trash headline → "How To Gain A Positively Unfair Advantage In Business And In Life" = saved the whole business).

### 8. Hot / Warm / Cold traffic — never one-size-fits-all (Secret #7)
Eugene Schwartz awareness levels. Hot = knows you & product → talk product. Warm = has the desire, doesn't know you → talk the solution. Cold = only feels the problem → talk the problem first, then need, then solution. Mismatched message = selling 1% instead of 20%. Cold market is the largest = the door to big sales.

### 9. The three "never-fail" sales formulas (Secret #15)
(1) **Problem / Agitate / Solve** — agitating ("make it hurt, a LOT worse") is the secret sauce; best for cold traffic. (2) **"If you want [3X benefit], then do this"** — positive/benefit-led; best for hot & warm. (3) **Before / After / Bridge** — uses NLP *future pacing*; works on all temperatures. "They don't buy if it don't hurt."

### 10. Hooks & persona (Secrets #29, #21)
A hook = a one-sentence story that creates *intense curiosity* (≠ a USP). Formulas: "unlikely character + timing + results"; "result + timing − pain." "Love me; hate me — there's no money in the middle": take a stand, build a recognizable persona/character; people buy from a person, not a logo.

### 11. Craft discipline & momentum
The "fill the page" brainstorm: the magic answers come in the *back third* of the page (the 5th/6th/10th bullet beats the 1st). Write 10–12 bullets, keep the best 5–6. Keep a **swipe file** (Secret #30) to warm up before writing. Write the offer/copy **before** you build the product — "the copy becomes the blueprint" ("Everything Else"). Practice daily and put eyeballs on it — measure real action, not compliments.

## LENS B — AGENT-GOVERNANCE FRAMEWORKS (items 12–20)

### 12. The Ultimate Bullet Formula (Secret #9) — a hard execution template
Feature + Benefit + Meaning → **"It ______ so you can ______ which means ______."** Feature = what it *is*; Benefit = what it *does for you*; Meaning = what it *means to the buyer*. This is the single most enforceable governance rule for any AI writing bullets/value props: a bullet that stops at the feature, or even at the benefit, is non-compliant. "Five amazing bullets outperform 30 good ones."

### 13. The 13-Part Sales Letter (Secret #17) — the canonical execution checklist
In order, each part's job is to "pass the bucket" to the next: (1) Headline package (pre-head / head / sub-head + "From the desk of / Re:") (2) Shocking Statement ("Did you know…?") (3) Define the Problem ("Here's the problem you face: ___") (4) Agitate ("which means you'll ___") (5) Present the Solution ("Luckily for you, there's now a solution. Let me introduce you to ___") (6) Bullets to arouse curiosity (6–10 best) (7) Credibility — "Why you?" (8) Proof — "Why should I believe you?" (9) Sum up the offer + price (what/how/when/how much) (10) Bonuses & pot-sweeteners (turn a competitor's USP into a free bonus; "sell dollars for dimes" — stack to ~10x perceived value) (11) Guarantee (remove risk) (12) Call to Action (13) P.S. (restate benefits + act now). Miss a stone, the reader "gets wet" and never buys.

### 14. The only purpose of an online ad (Secret #28)
"Get the RIGHT people to click and the WRONG people to keep on scrolling." Three required elements: emotional grabber + curiosity driver + clear call to action. Branding-as-goal is rejected. It's a **numbers game**: expect 10–50 ad tests to find a winner; "fail fast" on losers, scale winners; winners decay, so the pipeline never ends.

### 15. Three sales-asset format rule (Secret #16)
Default to a **Video Sales Letter** first (fastest to test); use **long-form** when price is high or the sale is technical/needs more info; use **short-form** for cheap/self-evident products (over-selling a $15 item trips the BS detector). Decision is "as much art as science" → **TEST**. Beware "absolute experts" (the red-vs-blue-headline cautionary tale).

### 16. Awareness-segmentation routing logic (Secret #7) — runtime decision rule
A per-task routing rule for any sales/marketing agent: classify the audience temperature (hot/warm/cold) BEFORE choosing the lead. Cold → lead with problem (fear-based converts best for cold). Warm → lead with solution. Hot → lead with product/offer + urgency (takeaway / FOMO close). Wrong temperature = wasted spend.

### 17. The "good way vs bad way" to go negative (Secret #24) — ethics guardrail
GOOD: use the negative in the prospect's head to get in sync; attack a *behavior*, a *result*, or a *method*. BAD: never attack a person or company **by name** ("Some of our competitors will tell you ___, but here are the facts" — never name names). Eugene Schwartz: copy channels existing desire, it does not manufacture it. This is the core do/don't boundary for AI-generated competitive copy.

### 18. Integrity guardrails on scarcity & claims (Secrets #23, #17, #31)
If you promise a time deadline or limited quantity, **honor it** — re-opening a "closed" cart or magically restocking "limited" units destroys integrity. Prefer FOMO / future-pacing over fake deadlines. Every claim needs proof you "could defend in a court of law" — never flip before/after photos; FTC-sensitive niches (money, weight loss, health) require rock-solid substantiation + disclaimers. Always tell the truth and back it up.

### 19. QC / polish protocol (Secret #31) — a pre-delivery checklist
Grammar/spelling/punctuation/formatting check (sloppy copy signals a sloppy product). Cross-browser + cross-device render check. **Secondary reading path**: can a skimmer get the whole pitch from headline + sub-heads + bold + images + P.S. alone? Watch the VSL **with sound off** (add subtitles; Facebook autoplays muted). Get a **second pair of eyes**. **Slippery-slide test**: no friction, no jarring section breaks — each section flows into the next "splashing down into the money pool."

### 20. Diagnostic "Lipstick on the Pig" checklist (Secret #23) — failure-mode triage
When copy underperforms, audit in order: Is there a headline? Is it about them (not you)? Is there a bold benefit/promise? Is the offer crystal clear (what/how/when/how much)? Is there a clear reason to buy NOW? Is there an emotional driver (people buy from dissatisfaction 9/10)? Do the bullets carry curiosity (Feature+Benefit+Meaning) or just features? Is the price/value right (too-low reads as cheap; aim "dollars for dimes")? Right graphics/colors? Sufficient proof for every claim? #1 thing to test = the headline.
