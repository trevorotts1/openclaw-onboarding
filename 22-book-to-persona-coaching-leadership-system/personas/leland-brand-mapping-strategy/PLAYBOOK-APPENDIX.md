## A — Headline, Hook & Subject-Line Formula Bank

> The book teaches **positioning hooks and profile formulas** (the "what do you do?" opener, the LinkedIn headline, the show-don't-tell bio line, the opt-in hook, the brand-soundbite types). It does **not** teach direct-response email subject lines, fascination bullets, or 3-second video hooks — those are marked ABSENT in Section H. The 12 formulas below are the genuine article.

### A1. The Anchor-Statement "What Do You Do?" Hook (Be/Do/Have opener)
- **When to use:** Hero headline, LinkedIn/Twitter bio first line, networking/elevator open, sales-page opening promise — anywhere a cold reader asks "what do you actually do?"
- **Pattern:** `I/We [DO: the specialized activity] — [HAVE: the concrete result clients get, with a number] — because [BE: the quality/why that makes the difference].`
- **Worked example:** "I specialize in selling businesses and represent more than 10,000 buyers… On average we obtain a 20 to 40 percent higher selling price than what the business first appraises for." (DO = selling businesses; HAVE = 20–40% higher price; BE = "I love helping small-business owners create freedom and eventually retire.")
- **Source:** Ch. 3 — Core Element 1, Anchor Statement / Be-Do-Have.

### A2. The LinkedIn (and Social) Headline Formula
- **When to use:** LinkedIn headline (120-char limit), any social headline/title line, directory bylines, speaker one-liners. The single highest-leverage profile string.
- **Pattern:** `[Title/Role] + [Specialty or Niche] + [Value/Keyword phrases] + [Proof/Credential]` — pack all 120 characters; lead with searchable keywords because journalists research via Twitter (50%+) and Facebook/LinkedIn (65%).
- **Worked example:** Boring → bold rewrites the book gives verbatim:
  - "CEO at Data-Dynamix Inc." → **"CEO at Data-Dynamix Inc., Digital Marketing Strategist, Driving Revenue, Maximizing Business Value, Inc. 5000 Winner."**
  - "Cheese Queen" → **"Cheese Sommelier, 'What Cheese' Website, Expert World Cheeses, Inspirational Cheese & Wine Pairings, Author."**
  - "Writer and Host" → **"Writer, Host & MC in the Tech, Travel, Food, & Entertainment Space, Lifestyle Tech, Gaming, Apps & Celebrity Interviews."**
- **Source:** Ch. 7 — Three Expressions: Intellectual Capital (Social-Media Profiles).

### A3. The Show-Don't-Tell Claim Rewrite (vague → specific proof)
- **When to use:** Any bio line, About section, headline sub-claim, or testimonial framing where a generic adjective is doing no work. Converts brag-speak into credible proof.
- **Pattern:** `[Vague self-label] → [Specific, numbered, verifiable accomplishment that proves the label].` Rule: "Be bold without bragging (or lying)."
- **Worked example:** "I'm a creative entrepreneur" → **"I've founded and sold three startups in the tech space and hold five patents."**  /  "I have a passion for building high-performance teams" → **"While I was CIO of company X, my team streamlined IT to achieve a 30 percent overall reduction in technical support time."**
- **Source:** Ch. 7 — Three Expressions: Intellectual Capital (Branded Biography).

### A4. The UBP Positioning One-Liner ("Why you?")
- **When to use:** Tagline, hero sub-headline, "what makes us different" block, ad opener. The differentiator line.
- **Pattern:** `[Verb the audience didn't know to ask for] the [familiar thing], using [hard-to-imitate mechanism/approach].` It "does not make you better… just gives your brand its particular flavor."
- **Worked example:** "Putting the business in creative." (Elizabeth Jensen — consultant + jewelry-line founder.)  /  "a system vs. silo approach" (senior brand strategist's UBP).
- **Source:** Ch. 3 — Core Element 2, Unique Branding Proposition (UBP).

### A5. The Brand-Tagline From Tone (mood-as-promise line)
- **When to use:** Brand tagline / signature line where the *feeling* of the brand is the hook, not the deliverable.
- **Pattern:** `We [do the ordinary thing] as a path to [the elevated emotional outcome / transformation].`
- **Worked example:** "I own a knitting store with a focus on stitching and stillness. We teach knitting as a path to meditation and transformation." (Jeanine Bould, Fashion Knit.)
- **Source:** Ch. 3 — Core Element 1 (Anchor Statement examples) + Core Element 3 (Tone & Temperament).

### A6. The Ethical-Bribe Opt-In Hook
- **When to use:** Opt-in headline / lead-magnet CTA on a homepage, landing page, or pop-up. The list-building exchange line.
- **Pattern:** `Get [free valuable asset: ebook / webinar / audio / quiz / white paper] — in exchange for your [email address] and permission to [periodically contact you].`
- **Worked example:** "I'll give you something for free (ebook, webinar, audio file, etc.), and in exchange you give me your email address and permission to periodically contact you."
- **Source:** Ch. 2 — Website Best Practices (Calls to Action / The Ethical Bribe).

### A7. The Website CTA Menu (micro-hook conversion verbs)
- **When to use:** Button copy and above-the-fold conversion offers on any page. Pick the lowest-friction match for the visitor's stage.
- **Pattern:** Choose one primary CTA verb-offer from the menu and place contact + social above the fold.
- **Worked example (the book's full menu, swipe directly):** "Sign up for newsletter / Take a quiz / Download audio or video / Fill out poll or survey / Make a comment / Pose a question / Sign up for webinar or teleclass / Download ebook or white paper / Make a complimentary-consult appointment."
- **Source:** Ch. 2 — Website Best Practices (Calls to Action).

### A8. Brand Sound-Bite Type — "Sexy Stats & Specifics"
- **When to use:** Proof line in a headline, ad, bio, or media pitch; the credibility hook.
- **Pattern:** `[Your number] vs. [industry/benchmark number]` or `[count] in [timeframe].`
- **Worked example:** "98% success rate vs. an industry average of 40%"; "sold 300 businesses in 15 years"; "Inc. 5000 ranking #1,226 — 343% three-year growth."
- **Source:** Ch. 7 — Intellectual Capital (Brand Sound Bites).

### A9. Brand Sound-Bite Type — "Hot Tips & How-Tos"
- **When to use:** Blog/social hook line, podcast title, media-pitch angle; the useful-content hook.
- **Pattern:** `[Number] ways to [achieve audience's desired outcome] without [the thing they dread].`
- **Worked example:** Coined as a repeatable cheat-sheet line on the one-page "brand at a glance" sound-bite sheet (e.g., "3 ways to brand yourself before someone else does it for you").
- **Source:** Ch. 7 — Intellectual Capital (Brand Sound Bites).

### A10. Brand Sound-Bite Type — "Unique Utterances" (coined phrase / proprietary name)
- **When to use:** Make a concept ownable and memorable — a hook that becomes your trademark.
- **Pattern:** `[Vivid noun phrase]©` for a process/idea you own, repeated across all channels.
- **Worked example:** "The Brand Mapping Process©"; "Brand Before You Build"; "Beware the Blow Dryer Effect"; "the haystack, not the needle."
- **Source:** Ch. 7 — Intellectual Capital (Brand Sound Bites: Unique Utterances).

### A11. The Short-Form Bio Hook (150–160 chars)
- **When to use:** Twitter / Instagram / Pinterest bio; any micro-bio field; "bold, not boring" in one breath.
- **Pattern:** `[Identity + niche] | [proof/credential] | [memorable signature phrase].` Stay within 150–160 characters.
- **Worked example:** Derived from the 4-version bio system — e.g., "Cheese Sommelier | Author, 'What Cheese' | world-cheese & wine pairings that make you look brilliant at dinner."
- **Source:** Ch. 7 — Intellectual Capital (4-Version Bio System: Short-Form).

### A12. The Media Recovery / Soundbite Fallback Line
- **When to use:** Live interview, sales conversation, or Q&A when you're thrown a question off your message — redirect to your brand point without lying.
- **Pattern:** `Well, I don't know about that, but what I do know is [your brand sound bite].`
- **Worked example:** "Well, I don't know about that, but what I do know is…" (Susan Harrow's "Practice First, PR Second" technique — rehearse, role-play, videotape your sound bites first.)
- **Source:** Ch. 7 — Intellectual Capital (Brand Sound Bites).

## B — Funnel & Page Recipes (page-by-page)

> The book teaches **platform and profile page recipes** (the hub website, the opt-in mechanism, the branded social profile, the newsletter, the one-page sound-bite sheet). It does **not** teach classic sales / VSL / webinar / checkout / upsell-downsell / thank-you funnels — those are marked **ABSENT IN SOURCE** in Section H. The recipes below are the full set the book provides and are exactly what Skill 6 (page build) and Skill 35 (profile build) consume.

### B1. The Hub Website / Homepage Recipe (Marketing-Mastery Phase-1 "Platform")
- **When to use:** Building or auditing the brand's primary website — the hub every other channel takes its cue from. "Brand Before You Build."
- **Pattern (block order + each block's job):**
  1. **Brand-driven color & design** — "your brand is what drives the website design, not the other way around." (Color ↑ brand recognition 80%; 42% judge a site on design alone; 52% won't return over aesthetics; 93% rank visual/color above other factors.)
  2. **Speedy navigation** — load fast; a 1-second delay = 7% fewer conversions.
  3. **Contact + social buttons ABOVE THE FOLD** — LinkedIn, Facebook, Twitter, Instagram.
  4. **Calls to Action** — one primary conversion offer from the CTA menu (see A7) + the ethical-bribe opt-in (A6).
  5. **SEO** — on-page + off-page (link building, social, bookmarking) + a short + long-tail keyword list ("a dedicated keyword list used to organically rank on the first page of Google — without paying per click").
  6. **Responsive design** — 67% more likely to buy from a mobile-friendly site; 52% less likely to engage if mobile is poor.
  7. **Professional writing** — clear, concise, correctly spelled, on-brand ("more than half the websites I audit don't make the grade").
- **Worked example:** The Inc. 5000 CEO personal site — colors chosen *from the brand*: Purple = high-end exclusivity, Blue = respectability/solidity, Gray-green = growth; **yellow VETOED** (cheerfulness was off-brand for a serious CEO). The cautionary inverse: "the clueless dentist" lost patients to a competitor 90 miles away purely on website quality — "This guy looks like a bit of a hack."
- **Source:** Ch. 2 — Website Best Practices.

### B2. The Ethical-Bribe Opt-In (squeeze) Recipe
- **When to use:** Any list-building moment — pop-up, inline opt-in, dedicated opt-in page.
- **Pattern:** (1) Lead with the free asset's benefit headline (A6); (2) name the asset (ebook / webinar / audio / quiz / white paper); (3) ask for email + permission only; (4) one button; (5) reassure with brand consistency (same logo/colors/voice as the hub).
- **Worked example:** "Download the free ebook → [email field] → 'Yes, send it + occasional brand tips.'" Mechanism verbatim: "I'll give you something for free… and in exchange you give me your email address and permission to periodically contact you."
- **Source:** Ch. 2 — Website Best Practices (Calls to Action / Ethical Bribe).

### B3. The Branded Social Profile Recipe (the "Sync Rule")
- **When to use:** Setting up or aligning any social profile (LinkedIn, Facebook, Twitter, Instagram, Pinterest) so it matches the hub. Core Skill 35 deliverable.
- **Pattern (sync every element to the website hub):** `Logo · Website colors · Bio · Profile photo · Taglines · Key brand messages · Company & personal name` — identical across every channel.
- **Worked example:** Same headshot, same brand colors, same Be/Do/Have bio, same tagline on the website and on LinkedIn/Twitter/Instagram so a prospect who hops channels feels one continuous brand.
- **Source:** Ch. 5 — Sync Up Your Online Presence.

### B4. The LinkedIn Profile "Must-Haves" Recipe
- **When to use:** Building a CEO/executive or founder LinkedIn profile (the #1 B2B platform; 82% of users 35+, 44% earn $75k+).
- **Pattern (required blocks):** Current headshot → Current job → Two most recent positions → Education → Profile summary (≈2,000-char Long-Form bio) → Additional skills → **≥4 recommendations** → headline per formula A2.
- **Worked example:** A C-suite profile that opens with a current professional headshot, a keyword-loaded headline (A2), and a Be/Do/Have summary, closing with 4+ recommendations — the book's "social CEO" standard (BRANDfog 2014: 80% say social is key to C-suite PR; 61% more likely to buy from a company whose leadership communicates values on social).
- **Source:** Ch. 8 — C-Suite/CEO Brand Matrix (Reputation Management) + Ch. 5.

### B5. The Branded Social-Media Background / Header Recipe
- **When to use:** Designing the banner/header image for LinkedIn, Twitter, YouTube, Facebook.
- **Pattern (include any/all):** `Name · URL · Headshot · Book cover(s) · A quote · Title · Logo · Brand colors · A signature model/diagram.` ($75–$150 from a graphics person.)
- **Worked example:** A consultant's LinkedIn banner showing her name + URL, headshot, book cover, brand-colored bar, and a one-line signature framework — instant credibility above the fold of the profile.
- **Source:** Ch. 7 — Three Expressions: Visual Capital.

### B6. The Newsletter Recipe (recurring brand asset)
- **When to use:** Standing email/newsletter that nurtures the list built by B2.
- **Pattern (template traits):** Predictable schedule (monthly/quarterly) · mostly high-quality content with "an offer or two" · a branded, structured template that **matches the website** · anti-spam compliant (opt-in only) · visible unsubscribe link.
- **Worked example:** A monthly branded newsletter: 80% useful content (a how-to + a sound bite + a trend), 20% one soft offer, in the hub's colors/voice, with a clear unsubscribe.
- **Source:** Ch. 4 — Tactical Landscape (Publishing/Content: Newsletters).

### B7. The One-Page "Brand at a Glance" Sound-Bite Sheet
- **When to use:** Internal cheat sheet a founder/agent reads from before interviews, pitches, podcasts, or writing any copy — keeps every asset on-brand.
- **Pattern (five buckets):** `Sexy Stats & Specifics · Trends · Hot Tips & How-Tos · Points of View & Informed Insights · Unique Utterances & Experiences (coined phrases / proprietary models).`
- **Worked example:** One page listing your "98% vs 40%" stat, the trend you're riding, three how-to tips, your contrarian point of view, and your two coined phrases — the source copy a writer pulls from for every asset.
- **Source:** Ch. 7 — Three Expressions: Intellectual Capital (Brand Sound Bites).

### B8. The Press-Release Recipe (Broadcast vs. Targeted)
- **When to use:** Generating earned-media/credibility content; deciding DIY vs. pro.
- **Pattern:** **Broadcast** = DIY form + distribution service (Newswire / PR Newswire / Wired PR News), $25–$250, general audience — won't land *NYT*/*Today*. **Targeted** = PR-pro-written, emailed to *named* reporters/producers/bloggers, tied to an event: new book / company achievement / individual achievement / timely expertise / award; $3,500–$30,000/month.
- **Worked example:** Data-Dynamix's Inc. 5000 ranking (#1,226; 343% 3-yr growth) became "a newsworthy reason" for a targeted PR campaign — an award turned into a press hook.
- **Source:** Ch. 4 — Tactical Landscape (Traditional PR: Press Releases).

## C — Script Bank (sales / objection / follow-up / discovery / close)

> The book provides **conversational positioning scripts** (the Anchor-Statement ladder, the cold-outreach credibility line, the clarity test). It provides **no** formal sales-call scripts, objection-rebuttal banks, or closing scripts — marked **ABSENT IN SOURCE** in Section H. The scripts below are the genuine ones.

### C1. The Anchor-Statement Conversational Ladder (discovery → value → close-of-meaning)
- **When to use:** Networking, discovery calls, "so what do you do?" moments — weaving Be/Do/Have turn-by-turn instead of dumping it.
- **Pattern:**
  - Them: "What do you do?" → You: `[DO: one clean sentence].`
  - Them: "What kind / how?" → You: `[DO detail + a proof asset].`
  - Them: "[qualifying question]" → You: `[HAVE: the quantified result].`
  - Them: "Wow / impressive." → You: `[BE: why you love it / who you serve].`
- **Worked example (verbatim ladder, Michelle Seiler-Tucker):**
  - "What do you do?" → "I'm a business broker. I specialize in selling businesses." (DO)
  - "What type?" → "All kinds. I have a list of more than 10,000 buyers looking to acquire a company."
  - "How big do they have to be to get sold?" → "There's an art and science to it. I have a process… 20 to 40 percent higher selling price than what they first appraised for." (HAVE)
  - "Wow, impressive." → "I love my business because I like helping small-business owners… create freedom and eventually retire." (BE)
- **Source:** Ch. 3 — Core Element 1, Anchor Statement.

### C2. The Cold-Outreach Credibility Script
- **When to use:** First-touch DM/email to a stranger online (journalist, partner, prospect) where you must establish legitimacy fast.
- **Pattern:** `[Reason for reaching out] + [link to LinkedIn profile] + [2–3 article links]. — establishing "I'm a legitimate businessperson, not some online nutter."`
- **Worked example:** "When contacting strangers online I include a link to my LinkedIn profile as well as a few articles… This is my way of saying, 'I'm a legitimate businessperson and not some online nutter.'"
- **Source:** Ch. 1 — Shift 3 (From a Sprint to a Marathon).

### C3. The Brand-Clarity Test ("How's your chicken?")
- **When to use:** Diagnosing a founder's (or a draft's) Anchor Statement live — exposes Brand Confusion in one move.
- **Pattern:** After they give a vague answer, ask an unrelated mundane question; if they answer flatly, the brand statement failed to create a pattern. Then rewrite for clarity.
- **Worked example:** Coach: "I help women find their passion and be happier." Leland: "How's your chicken?" Coach: "Fine." — proof the statement created no graspable pattern (Brand Confusion).
- **Source:** Ch. 3 — Core Element 1 (Brand Clarity vs Confusion).

### C4. The Brand-Energy Boundary / Delegation Line
- **When to use:** A founder or agent declining work outside their core Brand Energy without sounding weak — and routing it to the right person.
- **Pattern:** `That's not in my wheelhouse — [name the energy you DO bring] is. Let me bring in [the person whose energy fits].`
- **Worked example:** "That's not in my wheelhouse… instead of being yes-men" — know your 1–2 archetypal energies (of the 12) and staff around the gap.
- **Source:** Ch. 3 — Core Element 4, Brand Energy.

### C5. The Marathon Outreach "Small Fire" Follow-Up Logic
- **When to use:** Long-game relationship nurture (the opposite of a one-shot pitch).
- **Pattern:** One free piece of value → a real human connection → a low-pressure meeting → referral → project → compounding revenue, tracked over months/years. "Start and maintain a bunch of small fires to keep your brand burning hot."
- **Worked example:** One free Huffington Post article → LinkedIn invite to Susan Harrow → lunch ("Let's order dessert") → $3,500 referral → $30,000 project → $150,000 tracked over two years.
- **Source:** Ch. 1 — Shift 3 (Sprint to Marathon).

## E — Frameworks, Models & Templates (with the steps)

> **This is the book's richest section.** Every named framework reproduced with its complete internal structure + a worked example, so it is executable without the book. This is the engine that forces Be/Do/Have depth, Signature Story, and emotional capital into otherwise-thin copy.

### E1. The Brand Mapping Process© — the 7 Core Elements
- **When to use:** The foundational brand definition before ANY copy or design is built ("Brand Before You Build"). Run as a full-day engagement; don't move past an element until you have the "no stone unturned" feeling.
- **Pattern (the seven lenses, in order):**
  1. **Anchor Statement** — "What do you do?" via Be/Do/Have (see E2).
  2. **Unique Branding Proposition (UBP)** — "Why you?" (see E3).
  3. **Brand Tone & Temperament** — the mood/personality (see E4).
  4. **Brand Energy** — "the weather you bring," 1–2 of 12 archetypes (see E5).
  5. **Signature Story** — "How did you get here?" 5 plot types (see E6).
  6. **Signature Services** — named, specific competencies/offers/IP (not "I help women be happier").
  7. **Brand Enhancers & Reducers** — a brand-specific SWOT (see E7).
- **Worked example:** Metaphor delivered to clients: "Like a New York skyline puzzle — concentrate on one section at a time; when all the pieces are in place your unique brand map reveals itself." Applies identically to a personal, team, CEO, or business brand.
- **Source:** Ch. 3 — The Brand Mapping Process©.

### E2. The Be/Do/Have Anchor-Statement Model + Restaurant-Booth Exercise
- **When to use:** Writing or sharpening the elevator pitch / hero promise / bio opener — the core of all brand copy.
- **Pattern:**
  - **DO** = the day-to-day activity/service/process/product ("the least interesting aspect").
  - **HAVE** = the results/outcomes/impacts clients get.
  - **BE** = the qualities you contribute / the *why* ("the most powerful part… yet the aspect we spend the least time articulating").
  - **Exercise:** "Pretend it's one year from today and you overhear a group of your clients describing you/your company." Write what you WANT to hear for (1) what you DO, (2) the RESULTS (HAVE), (3) the QUALITIES (BE). Then craft, and **test on real people** "at cocktail parties, conferences, even standing in line at the movies. Keep what resonates, and tweak the rest."
- **Worked example:** Dental hygienist / law firm / movie producer / HR consultancy / CEO each mapped DO → HAVE → BE; Michelle Seiler-Tucker's full statement (A1) is the model.
- **Source:** Ch. 3 — Core Element 1.

### E3. The 5-Column UBP Worksheet ("Why you?")
- **When to use:** Finding the genuine differentiator to headline the "why us" of any page/bio. Positioning, not competition ("there is enough work to go around; I believe in positioning").
- **Pattern (take a sheet, five vertical columns):**
  1. **Attributes** — list the criteria YOUR audience screens for (e.g., for a branding consultant: PR/media placement, writing skills, social best practices, a proven process, specific deliverables, prior similar experience, personality/style fit).
  2. **Rating** — rate yourself 1 (very poor) → 10 (superstar) per attribute.
  3. **Zone** — NOT ZONE (≤6, hurting you, fix it) / NEUTRAL ZONE (7–8, bolster it) / HOT ZONE (9–10, "likely part of your UBP").
  4. **Competition** — rate competitors 1–10; flag any attribute where you trail by 2+ points.
  5. **QQ** — for each Hot-Zone attribute list the Quantity/Quality proof + "Why This Matters."
  - Two crafting questions: (1) "What is it your audience doesn't know that they don't know about what they want?" (2) "What do you do that is difficult to imitate?"
- **Worked example:** "Writing Ability" rated 9 → Hot Zone → QQ proof: "ghost-wrote 40 published articles for clients; clients land in *Forbes*" → Why it matters: "your website won't get you quoted if it reads like a hack."
- **Source:** Ch. 3 — Core Element 2.

### E4. The Brand Tone & Temperament 10-Word Sort + 4 Quality Groups
- **When to use:** Defining brand personality BEFORE choosing colors, fonts, logo, photos — "brand drives design." (In 99% of off-brand logos/sites, tone was never explored.)
- **Pattern:** Pick exactly **10 words** that describe you "most of the time, under the widest set of circumstances," prioritize 1–10, study synonyms of your top 3. A true trait is consistent across work/play/home, present "as far back as you can remember," noted by others, effortless to express, and the lead of its cluster. The four quality groups:
  1. **ANALYTICAL** (logical/methodical/rational/orderly): calm, conservative, dependable, detailed, disciplined, efficient, methodical, organized, practical, precise, punctual, rational, realistic, reliable, responsible, thorough.
  2. **ACHIEVER** (drive to succeed): ambitious, articulate, assertive, autonomous, confident, decisive, dedicated, determined, driven, entrepreneurial, independent, industrious, persistent, productive, tenacious.
  3. **AGREEABLE** (people-oriented/cooperative): adaptable, appreciative, approachable, authentic, collaborative, compassionate, congenial, considerate, cooperative, empathetic, flexible, friendly, generous, helpful, inclusive, personable, sincere, tactful, thoughtful.
  4. **ANIMATED** (expressive/creative): adventurous, cheerful, creative, curious, dynamic, eager, energetic, enthusiastic, imaginative, influential, innovative, intuitive, optimistic, outgoing, passionate, persuasive, resourceful, spontaneous, visionary.
- **Worked example:** Attorney Ericka Curls Bartling's tone = "a critical thinker with a persuasive yet friendly negotiation style" → an atypical lawyer site: warm colors, Zen-like images, single-sentence bold statements.
- **Source:** Ch. 3 — Core Element 3.

### E5. The 12 Brand-Energy Archetypes ("the weather you bring")
- **When to use:** Naming the 1–2 energies a person/brand reliably contributes — drives voice, content angle, and what to delegate.
- **Pattern:** Pick the 1–2 you're "most known for / have a natural gift for / others acknowledge / can be counted on to contribute." Each archetype = definition + first-person I-statements + synonyms:
  1. **Advocate** (champion of a cause) — Lynne Twist. 2. **Maker** (hands-on creator) — Anna Zoitas. 3. **Connector** ("you should talk to so-and-so, I'll introduce you") — Nicholas Zaldastani. 4. **Motivator** (moves others to action) — Adam Markel. 5. **Synthesizer** (combines into something new) — Susan Harrow. 6. **Fixer** ("let's see what problems we have waiting for us today"). 7. **Implementer** (turns goals into action) — Dana Aftab. 8. **Visionary** (imagines possibilities) — Binta Niambi Brown. 9. **Interpreter** (re-translates others' work) — Kim Bromley. 10. **Storyteller** (narrative drives change) — Dewitt Jones. 11. **Facilitator** (gets a group to consensus) — Colleen Rudio. 12. **Mentor** (trusted advisor) — Aaron Young.
- **Worked example:** A Connector brand: voice = warm + introductory; content = round-ups and intros; delegate the deep solo-build work to a Maker. "Know your limitation so you can hire your gap."
- **Source:** Ch. 3 — Core Element 4.

### E6. The Signature Story Framework (3 qualities + 5 plot types + writing exercise)
- **When to use:** The origin narrative on an About page, keynote open, or founder bio — the emotional spine of the brand.
- **Pattern:**
  - **3 qualities:** AUTHENTICITY ("a grain of greatness in every legitimate Signature Story" — never exaggerate/lie); SERENDIPITY (fate/coincidence/grace); SPECIFICS ("show, don't tell").
  - **5 plot types** (after Christopher Booker): 1. **Rags to Riches** (Cinderella). 2. **Beat the Bad** (Star Wars). 3. **There and Back Again** (Wizard of Oz). 4. **Eyes on the Prize** (Lord of the Rings). 5. **Awakening** (A Christmas Carol).
  - **Exercise (≥20 min):** note (a) significant jobs/clients/projects + lessons; (b) shaping experiences (often the negative-but-gift ones); (c) influential people; (d) talents gained → shape chronologically into a narrative (NOT a "laundry list of accomplishments").
- **Worked example:** Elizabeth Sheehan / Care 2 Communities — the visceral "minefield in Cambodia / postpartum hemorrhage" passage that "forever cemented her commitment." Reframe technique: the VP-of-finance-who-was-a-chiropractor — "As a chiropractor you brought the body's system into alignment; as VP of finance you bring the financial system into alignment — different system, similar skill set."
- **Source:** Ch. 3 — Core Element 5.

### E7. Brand Enhancers & Reducers (the Brand SWOT)
- **When to use:** Auditing a brand; prioritizing what to fix first; periodic brand reviews.
- **Pattern:** Tag each of 14 criteria as Strength / Weakness / Opportunity / Threat with notes: current competencies; competitive advantages; specific experience/knowledge; innovative/unique qualities; important resources (financial/human); critical skills; geographical advantage; education/accreditation; values/philosophy/culture; price/value/quality advantage; effective systems/processes; marketing/distribution capabilities; delivery capability; "the way the brand is expressed and articulated." Then act by quadrant: **Strengths** → "make your own brand strengths obsolete before your competitors do"; **Weaknesses** → small tweaks; **Opportunities** → "actually schedule a specific time"; **Threats** → "requires immediate attention… can widen into huge cracks overnight."
- **Worked example:** A firm tags "the way the brand is articulated" as a Weakness (dated website) and "Inc. 5000 ranking" as an Opportunity → schedules a website rewrite this month and a PR campaign off the ranking.
- **Source:** Ch. 3 — Core Element 7.

### E8. The Marketing Mastery Pyramid (3 phases) + the 10-item Quiz + Bands
- **When to use:** Locating where a brand is on the path to thought leadership and prescribing next moves.
- **Pattern (3 phases, bottom → top):** **Phase 1 Platform Development** (articulate brand → modern website → social consistency); **Phase 2 Brand & Buzz Building** (pick 3–5 tactics — PR, publishing/content, social, speaking, networking, awards — "two done wholeheartedly beat six done poorly"); **Phase 3 Thought/Industry Leadership** (add something new + be a trusted source + consistently generate high-quality content). **The quiz:** rate 10 items 1–5 (use social to build awareness; proactive media outreach; can articulate brand in <2 min; speak at conferences; keyword/SEO strategy; substantial quality content; defined ideal-client profile; on-brand website with best practices; written an ebook/book; specific brand strategy you track). **Bands:** 10–17 Platform Development; 18–34 Brand & Buzz Building; 35–50 Thought/Industry Leadership — each with its prescription list.
- **Worked example:** Score 22 → "Brand & Buzz Building" → prescription: steady stream of quality content, timely press releases, ongoing blogging + blogger outreach, monthly/quarterly newsletter.
- **Source:** Ch. 1 — The Marketing Mastery Pyramid.

### E9. The Brand Mapping Strategy (4-Step Deployment) + the Three C's
- **When to use:** Turning the brand map from "words in a desk drawer" into action and measurement.
- **Pattern:**
  1. **Understand the tactical landscape** (the full menu — PR, publishing, speaking, networking, social, awards).
  2. **Determine your best tactical approach** — 5 filters: love it or loathe it? sufficient ROI? talent/skill (or hire)? money required? time required? ("If you love a tactic, you're more likely to do it well.")
  3. **Create your implementation strategy** — per tactic: hire full-time / part-time / consultant-expert / intern / DIY / delegate to employee / DIY+outsource.
  4. **Measure success and pivot** — pick goals from the menu, then track the **Three C's: CUSTOMERS** (new business/leads/pipeline), **CREDIBILITY** (social proof/authority/media/online presence), **CASH** (a numerically measurable financial result). "Beware the Blow Dryer Effect — refresh based on how the world is today, not yesterday."
- **Worked example:** CEO who loves writing → picks blogging + podcasting (Phase-2 tactics) → DIY blog + intern-edited podcast → measures Credibility (media mentions) and Customers (consult bookings) quarterly.
- **Source:** Ch. 4 — The Brand Mapping Strategy.

### E10. The Three Expressions of a Personal Brand (Visual / Intellectual / Emotional Capital)
- **When to use:** Building a founder/CEO personal brand across every touchpoint. "A photograph expresses three things, not one."
- **Pattern:**
  - **Visual capital:** style of dress (clothes "transmit a social signal"); color & design (Brand Fit / Audience / Context); **headshot rules** (see E11); branded social background (B5); a visual CV.
  - **Intellectual capital:** brand sound bites (B7); the 4-version branded bio (E12); social profiles + keywords (A2); content creation (blog/podcast/video/book).
  - **Emotional capital:** choose the feeling you want the audience to experience — Joy / Security / Hope / Excitement / Glamour / Effectiveness / Authenticity / Toughness — which then drives language, color, and wardrobe.
- **Worked example:** "Steven," a long-haired rebel manager, did a collage exercise from GQ/Men's Health/Esquire to define his visual capital → adopted formal attire → promoted to VP (research: suit-wearers closed better deals, higher testosterone; formal dress increased abstract thinking).
- **Source:** Ch. 7 — The Three Expressions of Your Personal Brand.

### E11. The Headshot Rules Checklist
- **When to use:** Briefing any profile photo / headshot (people judge a person from a photo in <1 second; profiles with photos get 7× more views).
- **Pattern:** Show the real you (no avatars; within 2 years) · Face forward and smile (a teeth-showing smile ↑ competence/likability/influence per PhotoFeeler) · Head-and-shoulders to head-to-waist framing (face-only = less likable, full-body = negative) · Remove distractions (no sunglasses, caps, dogs, surfboards, beer, other people's limbs) · Dress your brand (industry + audience drive wardrobe).
- **Worked example:** Brief: "Current head-and-shoulders shot, forward-facing genuine smile, brand-colored simple background, business-casual to match a B2B audience, no props."
- **Source:** Ch. 7 — Visual Capital.

### E12. The 4-Version Bio System
- **When to use:** One Word doc, cut-and-paste the right length anywhere. "Long or short, your goal is bold, not boring."
- **Pattern:** (1) **Long-Form** ≈2,000 chars — LinkedIn summary / "About" ("has taken the place of a CV"). (2) **Short-Form** 150–160 chars — Twitter/Instagram/Pinterest. (3) **Conference Bio** 250–450 words. (4) **Broadcast Bio** 75–100 words — the spoken intro before a speech/interview, or a blog-post footer.
- **Worked example:** A speaker keeps all four in `bio-master.docx`; pastes the 75–100-word Broadcast version into an event program and the 2,000-char Long-Form into LinkedIn — all in one Be/Do/Have voice, all "bold without bragging (or lying)."
- **Source:** Ch. 7 — Intellectual Capital (Branded Biography).

### E13. The C-Suite / CEO Brand Matrix (parallel brand, 4 domains)
- **When to use:** Building a CEO/executive "parallel brand" that doubles company-brand exposure (48% of company reputation is attributed to CEO standing).
- **Pattern (4 domains):** (1) **Reputation Management** — "claim your name" (name-as-URL + vanity social handles), stay on top of search (Google/Bing/Yahoo + Google Alert), be a social CEO. (2) **Establish Thought Leadership** — 3 varieties: **Celebrity** (Branson/Robbins/Oprah) / **Cerebral** (Gates/Zuckerberg/Lagarde) / **Consequential** (Jobs/Sandberg/Merkel); top activity = speak at conferences. (3) **Content Marketing** — CEO blog/articles/white papers/podcast/ebook/webcasts. (4) **Strengthen Personal & Executive Presence** — executive presence = gravitas (heaviest: confidence, decisiveness, integrity, EQ, vision, reputation) + communication + appearance.
- **Worked example:** "Martha," a CIO, shifted her brand from "a manager who effectively problem-solves / hands-on implementation" → "an influential leader creating a culture of continuous improvement," via high-visual, fun, inclusive town-hall meetings — a 3–4-month Personal Brand Project.
- **Source:** Ch. 8 — The C-Suite/CEO Brand Matrix.

### E14. The Five Expressions of a Brand-Centric Company + the Quiz + CBI
- **When to use:** Aligning a whole company to deliver its brand promise (87% of execs: a strong corporate brand is as important as product brands).
- **Pattern (5 functions of brand alignment):** (1) **Customer Experience** — 3 dimensions: Interpersonal (staff attitude), Product (physical/website quality), Process (ease of doing business). (2) **Management Commitment** — "build your capacity from your brand-delivery non-negotiables" (don't promise then fail). (3) **Employee Engagement** — everyone is a brand ambassador. (4) **Processes/Procedures/Systems/Standards** — "the fulcrum." (5) **Organizational Infrastructure** — roles/rewards/recognition. **Quiz:** 10 items, 1–5; bands 10–17 "At the gate," 18–34 "Taking off," 35–50 "Flying." **The CBI (Continuous Brand Improvement) 6-step recipe:** (1) capture all problems via a confidential form, sorted into the 5 categories; (2) choose the right problem ("start small — a win builds momentum"); (3) select a facilitator (respected, feedback-open, sets own views aside, trained — need NOT know the problem); (4) bring 6–10 hands-on members together (~6–8 weeks, then disband); (5) implement (test: who's impacted? ease? cost? new problems? conflicts?); (6) start over (Kaizen — "never ending").
- **Worked example:** A Silicon Valley client solved a year-old issue in 4 hours using the CBI circle — the origin of the method.
- **Source:** Ch. 9 — The Five Expressions of a Brand-Centric Company.

### E15. The 7 Misguided Myths of Personal Branding (myth-buster framework)
- **When to use:** Overcoming a founder/exec's resistance to building a personal brand; reframing objections.
- **Pattern (myth → truth):** 1. "It's new" → centuries old (Napoleon/Churchill/Chaplin). 2. "Reputation IS your brand" → online brand can differ. 3. "It's all about you" → others benefit (91% who work for good leaders enjoy work). 4. "A personal brand is good" → not always (the phone-throwing bank president). 5. "Image IS brand" → "a personal brand is not strategically conceived, it's authentically discovered." 6. "Nice but unnecessary" → you already have one (Bezos). 7. "At odds with the business brand" → a parallel brand "can double your business brand exposure" (Hsieh/Zappos, Jobs/Apple).
- **Worked example:** Founder says "I don't want to seem like a braggart" (Myth 3) → reframe: "A strong, positive brand benefits the people you lead — it's service, not ego."
- **Source:** Ch. 6 — The 7 Misguided Myths of Personal Branding.

### E16. The Social-Media Site Selector (4 ranked criteria)
- **When to use:** Choosing which 1–3 social channels to work "consistently and deeply" instead of spreading thin.
- **Pattern (rank candidates on 4 criteria):** (1) **Site Demographics** (is your audience here?) → (2) **Content Type & Style** → (3) **Resources Required** → (4) **Brand Fit** (do tone/temperament/energy/UBP match the site?). Reference demos: LinkedIn (#1 B2B), Facebook (#1 B2C), Twitter (experts, 140 chars), Pinterest (80% women 25–54), Instagram/YouTube (visual), etc.
- **Worked example:** Holly Xerri / Camibands chose Pinterest over the *Today* show because her exact demo (80% women 25–54, avg order $80) lived there — 40,000 hits in 4 days vs. 3,500 from *Today*.
- **Source:** Ch. 5 — Sync Up Your Online Presence.

## F — Brand Voice & Brand-Building Language Patterns

> The author's reusable voice machinery as ready-to-use lists with example lines. This is what lets a copy specialist write ON-brand and richly instead of thinly/concisely — the direct fix for "too-concise funnel/website copy."

### F1. Signature power phrases (use)
- **Pattern:** Drop the book's coined phrases as section headers, CTAs, and hooks.
- **Example lines:** "Brand by design, not by default." · "Brand Before You Build." · "Become the haystack, not the needle." · "A marathon, not a sprint." · "What's the weather you bring with you?" · "Customers, Credibility, and Cash." · "Beware the Blow Dryer Effect." · "Start and maintain a bunch of small fires."

### F2. Vague benefit-speak to AVOID
- **Pattern:** Ban abstractions a reader can't picture; every claim must fit a pattern fast.
- **Example lines (do NOT write):** "I help women find their passion and be happier." · "I have a passion for building high-performance teams." · "We deliver world-class solutions." → replace each with a specific, numbered Signature Service.

### F3. The Show-Don't-Tell sentence structure
- **Pattern:** `[claim] → [specific number + outcome + named context].`
- **Example line:** "While I was CIO of company X, my team streamlined IT to achieve a 30 percent overall reduction in technical support time."

### F4. The Be/Do/Have layering structure
- **Pattern:** Never ship a "do-only" sentence; thread Do → Have → Be.
- **Example line:** "We sell businesses (DO), getting owners 20–40% above appraisal (HAVE), because we love handing entrepreneurs their freedom (BE)."

### F5. "Bold, not boring / bold without bragging (or lying)"
- **Pattern:** Make claims confident and specific, never timid, never inflated.
- **Example line:** "I've founded and sold three startups and hold five patents." (bold + true, not "I'm a creative entrepreneur.")

### F6. Proof & credibility language (Sexy Stats)
- **Pattern:** `[your number] vs. [benchmark]` / `[count] over [timeframe]` / `[ranking]`.
- **Example line:** "98% success rate vs. an industry average of 40%." · "Inc. 5000 #1,226 — 343% three-year growth."

### F7. Naming patterns (own your IP)
- **Pattern:** Coin a `[Vivid Noun Phrase]©` for processes, programs, and mechanisms; reuse everywhere.
- **Example line:** "The Brand Mapping Process©." · "'Rent My Brain' 1-hour session." · "Signature Story / Signature Services."

### F8. Emotional-capital feeling words
- **Pattern:** Pick the ONE feeling the copy must create, then bend word/color/image to it.
- **Example line:** Menu — Joy · Security · Hope · Excitement · Glamour · Effectiveness · Authenticity · Toughness (each with 4 synonyms in the book).

### F9. Color-psychology language
- **Pattern:** `[color] = [brand meaning]` — justify every color by brand reason, never taste.
- **Example line:** "Purple = high-end exclusivity; Blue = respectability/solidity; Gray-green = growth; yellow vetoed (off-brand cheerfulness for a CEO)."

### F10. Analogy / metaphor templates
- **Pattern:** Teach an abstract brand idea with one concrete image.
- **Example lines:** "Like a New York skyline puzzle, one piece at a time." · "Seven optometrist lenses — clearer or blurrier?" · "The haystack, not the needle." · "The Blow Dryer Effect — what was special is now expected."

### F11. CTA / ethical-bribe language
- **Pattern:** `Get [free asset] in exchange for your [email + permission].`
- **Example line:** "Download the free brand-clarity ebook — just your email and permission to send occasional brand tips."

### F12. Positioning-not-competition language
- **Pattern:** Frame difference as flavor, never superiority.
- **Example line:** "I don't believe in ruthless competition — there's enough work to go around. I believe in positioning." · A UBP "does not make you better… it just gives your brand its particular flavor."

### F13. Clarity-test reframe
- **Pattern:** Expose vagueness with a deadpan unrelated question, then rewrite.
- **Example line:** "'How's your chicken?' 'Fine.' — if your brand statement lets that happen, it created no pattern. Rewrite it."

### F14. Conversational (not robotic) delivery
- **Pattern:** Write the Anchor Statement as talking points to weave, "not something you repeat robotically word for word."
- **Example line:** Layer proof points conversationally as the listener asks follow-ups (see the C1 ladder).

### F15. "Take what you like and leave the rest" inclusive close
- **Pattern:** Invite the reader to self-select, lowering resistance.
- **Example line:** Hula-teacher proverb the book closes with — "A'ohe pau ka 'ike i ka hālau ho'okahi" ("All knowledge is not learned in just one school") → "take what you like and leave the rest."

### F16. Archetype I-statement voice
- **Pattern:** Write in the first-person voice of the brand's 1–2 energies.
- **Example line:** Connector: "You should talk to so-and-so — I'll introduce you." Fixer: "Let's see what problems we have waiting for us today."

### F17. Ubiquity / "persistent many" framing (urgency without hype)
- **Pattern:** Replace one-big-hit urgency with compounding-consistency urgency.
- **Example line:** "Book promotion is no longer a sprint won by the lucky few but a marathon conquered by the persistent many."

> Offer/guarantee/bonus/scarcity language is **THIN** in this source: it provides the ethical-bribe CTA (F11) and a productized-service menu ("Rent My Brain" 1-hr; 1-day program; 6–12-mo program; online course), but **no** formal guarantee, bonus-stack, or scarcity/urgency scripts — logged THIN/ABSENT in Section H.
