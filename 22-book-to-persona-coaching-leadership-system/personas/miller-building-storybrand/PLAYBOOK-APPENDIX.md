## A — Headline, Hook & Subject-Line Formula Bank

### A1. The Grunt-Test Header (above-the-fold offer)
- **When to use:** The first line of text above the fold on any homepage/landing page. Must let a "caveman" grunt what you offer in 5 seconds.
- **Pattern:** `We [verb] [customer] [the aspirational identity OR the specific result].` Must satisfy ≥1 of 3 criteria: promise an aspirational identity, promise to solve a problem, or state exactly what you do. No founder backstory.
- **Worked example:** "We Will Make You a Pro in the Kitchen!" · Squarespace: "We Help You Make Beautiful Websites" · Local Honey: "We sell clothes. We do hair."
- **Source:** Ch. 11 (The Five Things Your Website Should Include).

### A2. The Aspirational "What the Customer Wants" Headline
- **When to use:** Hero headline / tagline that opens a story gap by naming the single thing the customer wants (mapped to survive/thrive).
- **Pattern:** `[The aspirational outcome] you've [been looking for / dreamed about].` Pare to ONE desire.
- **Worked example:** Resort: "Find the luxury and rest you've been looking for." · Real estate agent: "The Home You've Dreamed About." · Bookstore: "A Story to Get Lost In." · Financial advisor: "A Plan for Your Retirement." · Alumni assoc.: "Leave a Meaningful Legacy."
- **Source:** Ch. 3 (Character).

### A3. The Problem-Promise Headline (resolve all 3 problem levels)
- **When to use:** Tagline that hits the external + internal + philosophical problem at once (the "climactic scene").
- **Pattern:** `[Product/result] That Will [resolve the internal feeling].`
- **Worked example:** House painter — external = ugly home, internal = embarrassment → "Paint That Will Make Your Neighbors Jealous."
- **Source:** Ch. 4 (Problem).

### A4. The Survive-&-Thrive Benefit Tagline
- **When to use:** A short brand slogan tied directly to one of the 7 survival desires (save money, save time, status, etc.).
- **Pattern:** `[Survival verb]. [Better-life verb].`
- **Worked example:** Walmart: "Save Money. Live Better." · Starbucks: "to inspire and nurture the human spirit – one person, one cup… at a time."
- **Source:** Ch. 3.

### A5. The Lead-Generator "X Things" Title
- **When to use:** Naming a downloadable guide / lead magnet (a transitional CTA). Best starting lead gen.
- **Pattern:** `[Number] Things [Your Audience] Should [Know / Include / Get Right] [About X].`
- **Worked example:** StoryBrand's own: "5 Things Your Website Should Include" (40k+ downloads, drove past $2M). · Architect: "Building Your Dream Home: 10 Things to Get Right Before You Build."
- **Source:** Ch. 13 (Lead Generator).

### A6. The "X Mistakes" Lead-Generator Title
- **When to use:** Authority-building lead magnet that agitates a fear of loss.
- **Pattern:** `[Number] Mistakes [Audience] Make[s] With [High-Stakes Thing].`
- **Worked example:** Financial advisor: "5 Mistakes People Make with Their First Million Dollars." · Dog boarding: "5 Things Your Dog Thinks About When You're Away."
- **Source:** Ch. 13.

### A7. The One-Liner (logline) Statement
- **When to use:** Verbal headline for intros, business cards, email signatures, bios, site sub-header.
- **Pattern:** `We [help/provide] [CHARACTER] who [PROBLEM] [PLAN] so they can [SUCCESS].` (Character + Problem + Plan + Success.)
- **Worked example:** "We provide busy moms with a short, meaningful workout they can use to stay healthy and have renewed energy." · "We help retired couples who want to escape the harsh cold avoid the hassle of a second mortgage while still enjoying the warm, beautiful weather of Florida in the winter."
- **Source:** Ch. 12 (The One-Liner).

### A8. Direct-CTA Button Copy
- **When to use:** The primary buy-button, repeated top-right + center above the fold and down the page in a contrasting color.
- **Pattern:** `[Imperative verb] [Now / Today].`
- **Worked example:** "Order Now" · "Call Today" · "Schedule an Appointment" · "Register Today" · "Buy Now."
- **Source:** Ch. 7 (Call to Action).

### A9. Email Subject Line — Question Hook (nurture)
- **When to use:** Opening a weekly nurturing email; raises the customer's own question.
- **Pattern:** `Should We [Common Question The Customer Already Asks]?`
- **Worked example:** Crest Hill Boarding nurture email — Subject: "Should We Free Feed Our Dogs?"
- **Source:** Ch. 14 (Automated Email).

### A10. Email Subject Line — Fear/Solution Hook (sales)
- **When to use:** The sales (offer) email in a sequence; names the internal fear and promises relief.
- **Pattern:** `A solution for [scary / frustrating version of the problem].`
- **Worked example:** Crest Hill sales email — Subject: "A solution for scary boarding."
- **Source:** Ch. 14.

### A11. The Identity-Ad Direct-Address Hook
- **When to use:** Brand/identity ad that sells who the customer becomes, not the product.
- **Pattern:** `Hello, [Villain or Aspirational-Identity, personified]. [Direct second-person address that assumes the relationship].`
- **Worked example:** Gerber Knives: "Hello, Trouble. / It's been awhile since we last met. / But I know you're still out there. / And I have a feeling you're looking for me…"
- **Source:** Ch. 10 (Identity Transformation).

### A12. The Plan-Name Value Lift
- **When to use:** Naming your process/agreement plan to raise its perceived value.
- **Pattern:** `The [Benefit Adjective] [Plan / Agreement / Guarantee].`
- **Worked example:** "Easy Installation Plan" · "World's Best Night's Sleep Plan" · "Customer Satisfaction Agreement" · "Our Quality Guarantee."
- **Source:** Ch. 6 (Plan).

### A13. The Success-Promise Slogan (guaranteed outcome)
- **When to use:** Closing the story loop with a vivid, often-guaranteed success vision.
- **Pattern:** `You'll [feel/look/become the success state].`
- **Worked example:** Men's Wearhouse: "You're going to like the way you look. I guarantee it." · Nike: "bring inspiration and innovation to every athlete in the world."
- **Source:** Ch. 9 (Success).

### A14. The Cocktail/Community Lead-Gen Title
- **When to use:** Recurring community-builder lead magnet for a local/retail business.
- **Pattern:** `[Club Name]: Learn to [Do One Small Valuable Thing] Each [Cadence].`
- **Worked example:** Garden store: "Cocktail Club: Learn to Make One New Cocktail Each Month." · Speaking coach: "Becoming a Professional Speaker."
- **Source:** Ch. 13.

## B — Funnel & Page Recipes (page-by-page)

> StoryBrand is a message/clarity book, not a 22-funnel-type catalog. It teaches ONE master page (the website), the lead-gen page, the email funnel, the multi-revenue umbrella structure, and the overall Marketing Roadmap. All captured below; funnel types the book does NOT cover (webinar/VSL/checkout/upsell-downsell/order-bump) are marked ABSENT in Section H — pair with Brunson for those.

### B1. The StoryBrand Homepage / Landing Wireframe (the master page)
- **When to use:** Any homepage or landing page. "Your website is the drum solo" — nothing on it that isn't generated by a BrandScript box.
- **Pattern (block order, top → bottom):**
  1. **Header / above the fold** — the offer (A1/A2 headline), an image of success, **Direct CTA top-right** + a **second Direct CTA center**. Eye scans a **Z-pattern**: top-left logo+tagline → top-right "Buy Now" → diagonal to center offer + "Buy Now." A transitional CTA sits in a less-bright button beside the direct CTA. ["first date" — short, bold, customer-centric.]
  2. **The Stakes** — a few bullets of what the customer loses by not engaging (salt, a pinch).
  3. **The Value Proposition** — 3-ish benefit bullets ("Morse code," not paragraphs).
  4. **The Guide** — empathy line + authority (≈3 testimonials, a stat, award + client logos).
  5. **The Plan** — the 3-step process plan (named).
  6. **Explanatory paragraph / "junk drawer"** — the longer SEO copy hidden behind a "read more" at the bottom for the few who want detail.
  7. **Repeated Direct CTA** — the same bright button again near the bottom.
  8. **Images of success** throughout — smiling/satisfied people, product in happy hands (never a photo of your building).
- **Worked example:** Local Honey hero: "We sell clothes. We do hair." + "Buy Now"/"Schedule a Visit." Kyle Shultz cut his page to <300 words (90% of text removed) and went $25k → $103k to the same list — proof of "very few words."
- **Source:** Ch. 11 + the StoryBrand Marketing Roadmap backmatter.

### B2. The Multi-Revenue "Umbrella + Sub-Pages" Recipe
- **When to use:** A business with several divisions/products that compete for attention on one site.
- **Pattern:** Find ONE **umbrella message** for the homepage hero → split each division onto its **own page, each driven by its own BrandScript** → keep one persistent **top + bottom button** for the highest-value action.
- **Worked example:** Life-planning + strategic-ops + facilitator-certification business → umbrella "The Key to Success Is a Customized Plan" + a persistent "Become a Trained Facilitator" button top and bottom.
- **Source:** Ch. 11 (A Bite-Sized Breakdown of Your Revenue Streams).

### B3. The Lead-Generator / Opt-In Page Recipe
- **When to use:** Capturing emails (a transitional CTA). "No one wants to sign up for your newsletter" — offer a valuable asset instead.
- **Pattern:** A lead gen must (1) deliver enormous value and (2) establish authority. Page = a value-driven **title (A5/A6)** + 1–2 lines on the payoff + email field + button. Choose one of **5 types**: (1) Downloadable Guide/PDF (~3 pages — cheapest, best start), (2) Online Course/Webinar, (3) Software Demo/Free Trial, (4) Free Samples, (5) Live Event. Deliver with a **pop-up on a ~10-second buffer** (outperforms most internet ads). A sub-$5M business can see results with as few as ~250 qualified emails.
- **Worked example:** "5 Things Your Website Should Include" PDF → thousands of downloads → StoryBrand doubled revenue in 12 months with $0 ad spend. Health clinic replaced lobby magazines with a "The Healthy Body Checklist" → tagged leads → automated email upsells.
- **Source:** Ch. 13.

### B4. The Automated Email Funnel (nurture + sales) Recipe
- **When to use:** After the lead gen captures the email — the ongoing follow-up engine.
- **Pattern:** Weekly cadence → **#1 Nurturing → #2 Nurturing → #3 Nurturing → #4 Sales (with CTA)**, then repeat monthly. "There is great power in simply reminding our customers we exist." 20% open rate is industry standard; unsubscribes are GOOD (they clean the list). Full email templates in Section D.
- **Worked example:** Reed's Dairy ran one sales-style email day → $3,000 → $52,000 in one day. Tools: MailChimp (simple) or Infusionsoft (segmentation/e-commerce; StoryBrand's choice). Draft emails in a Word doc first.
- **Source:** Ch. 14.

### B5. The Referral / Word-of-Mouth Funnel Recipe
- **When to use:** Turning existing happy customers into a lead source (referrals are up to 2.5x more responsive than any other channel).
- **Pattern (3 steps):** (1) **Identify** ideal existing customers into a dedicated database (Domino's "pizza profile" model); (2) **Give a reason to spread the word** — don't ask for names; hand them a shareable PDF/video + the referral email (Section C5); (3) **Offer a reward** — referral credit or ~10% affiliate commission. Automate via MailChimp/Infusionsoft/HubSpot; opt customers out after several orders so you don't over-pitch.
- **Worked example:** SAT/ACT academy — "100% Refund for 3 New Referrals Within a Semester" + "100% Referral Club" seminars; driving range — "Invite-a-Friend" free bucket of balls → +40% lesson signups; contractor "Open-House Party" cookout on the new deck → filled 12 months of work.
- **Source:** Ch. (Referrals, Marketing Roadmap).

### B6. The Marketing Roadmap (the master funnel of funnels)
- **When to use:** Sequencing the entire StoryBrand rollout — the "opening moves" on top of the BrandScript "foundation."
- **Pattern (5 plays in order):** (1) **One-Liner** → (2) **BrandScript-driven website** (B1) → (3) **Lead Generator** to collect emails (B3) → (4) **Automated email nurture + sales sequence** (B4) → (5) **Collect & tell transformation stories** (testimonials, Section C4).
- **Worked example:** StoryBrand's own stack: one-liner recited everywhere + the SB7 site + the "5 Things" PDF + Infusionsoft drip + customer transformation videos → doubled revenue past $2M.
- **Source:** StoryBrand Marketing Roadmap (backmatter).

## C — Script Bank (sales / objection / follow-up / discovery / close)

### C1. The "Marry Me / Date" CTA Pairing Script
- **When to use:** Deciding/placing the two CTAs together — Direct ("marry me") beside Transitional ("go on a date").
- **Pattern:** Run a high-commitment **Direct CTA** and a low-risk **Transitional CTA** side by side; keep asking. Transitional CTA does 3 things: stake a claim to territory, create reciprocity, position you as the guide.
- **Worked example:** Us: "Will you marry me?" — Customer: "No." — Us: "Will you go out with me again?" — Customer: "Yes." … (repeat) … Customer: "Okay, I'll marry you now." Direct = "Buy Now"; Transitional = "Download the free guide."
- **Source:** Ch. 7.

### C2. Empathy Statement Starters
- **When to use:** The Guide's empathy line — "Customers won't know you care until you tell them."
- **Pattern:** `We understand how it feels to [pain].` / `Nobody should have to [injustice].` / `Like you, we are frustrated by [villain].` / `We care about your [thing].`
- **Worked example:** Discover Card positions you to "talk to a replica of yourself." Amy Cuddy's two questions a guide must answer: *Can I trust you?* (empathy) + *Can I respect you?* (authority).
- **Source:** Ch. 5 (Guide).

### C3. The 4-Step Fear-Appeal Script (stakes)
- **When to use:** Introducing stakes/loss without fearmongering ("Fear is salt in the recipe" — a pinch).
- **Pattern:** (1) make them know they're **vulnerable**; (2) tell them to **act** to reduce vulnerability; (3) give a **specific protective CTA**; (4) **challenge** them to act now.
- **Worked example (termites):** "Nearly 30 percent of all homes have evidence of termite infestation." → "Since nobody wants termites, you should do something about it." → "We offer a complete home treatment that will insure your house is free of termites." → "Call us today and schedule your home treatment." (Basis: Kahneman loss aversion — people are 2–3x more motivated to avoid a loss.)
- **Source:** Ch. 8 (Failure).

### C4. The Testimonial 5-Question Elicitation Script
- **When to use:** Collecting "stories of transformation" — give future customers "the gift of going second." Don't ask "are we great?"; lead them.
- **Pattern (ask in order):** (1) What was the **problem** you had before our product? (2) What did the **frustration feel like** as you tried to solve it? (3) What was **different** about our product? (4) Take us to the **moment you realized** it was working. (5) What does **life look like now** that it's solved?
- **Worked example:** The five answers paste straight into a case study and naturally form a before→after transformation arc; feature in emails, videos, keynotes, podcast outros.
- **Source:** Ch. (Testimonials / Stories of Transformation).

### C5. The Referral Email Script
- **When to use:** Step 2 of the referral system — give customers an easy way to introduce friends.
- **Pattern:** `Dear Friend, Thanks for doing business with us. A number of our clients have wanted to tell their friends about [how we help] but weren't sure how… We put together a [video/PDF] that helps your friends solve [X problem]… feel free to send it along… Sincerely, [Name]. P.S. [X problem] can be frustrating — if you'd rather introduce us in person, just let us know.`
- **Worked example:** Verbatim model above (the "Nancy" letter), used to drive shareable transitional CTAs.
- **Source:** Ch. (Referrals).

### C6. The Three Crucial Story Questions (discovery)
- **When to use:** Diagnosing/building any BrandScript — must always be answerable.
- **Pattern:** (1) What does the **hero want**? (2) **Who/what opposes** the hero getting it? (3) What will the hero's life look like **if she does (or does not)** get it?
- **Worked example:** Run before writing one word of copy; if any answer is missing, the message will confuse.
- **Source:** Ch. 1–2.

### C7. The Grunt-Test Audit Script (close/QC)
- **When to use:** Testing a page before ship — "Could a caveman grunt what you offer?"
- **Pattern:** A stranger must answer in 5 seconds: (1) What do you offer? (2) How will it make my life better? (3) What do I do to buy it?
- **Worked example:** Aspirin company → "You sell headache medicine, me feel better fast, me get it at Walgreens."
- **Source:** Ch. 1.

### C8. Authority-Demonstration Script (4 ways)
- **When to use:** Proving competence without bragging ("just check the box").
- **Pattern:** Use (1) **Testimonials** (≈3, brief — not 10–20, or you become the hero); (2) **Statistics**; (3) **Awards** (small logos at page bottom); (4) **Logos** (client / "as seen in").
- **Worked example:** Infusionsoft stat: "125,000 users trust [our] award-winning automation software."
- **Source:** Ch. 5.

### C9. The Process-Plan Script (3 steps)
- **When to use:** Removing confusion about how to do business — "stepping stones across the creek."
- **Pattern:** 3–6 named steps (ideally 3; group into phases if >6).
- **Worked example:** Expensive product → "1) Schedule an appointment 2) Allow us to create a customized plan 3) Let's execute the plan together." Car → "1) Test-drive a car 2) Purchase the car 3) Enjoy free maintenance for life." Garage storage → "1) Measure your space 2) Order the items that fit 3) Install in minutes with basic tools."
- **Source:** Ch. 6.

### C10. The Agreement-Plan Script (fear list → counter-promises)
- **When to use:** When trust/risk is the blocker. Method: list everything the customer is worried about, counter each with an agreement.
- **Pattern:** `We agree to [counter to fear #1]. We agree to [counter to fear #2]. …`
- **Worked example:** CarMax 4-point agreement (no haggling, no commission, quality-certified). Newt Gingrich's "Contract with America." Whole Foods values list.
- **Source:** Ch. 6.

### C11. The Fearmonger-Objection Reframe (founder objection)
- **When to use:** Owner says "Won't talking about what they'll lose make me a fearmonger?"
- **Pattern:** Acknowledge → invert with data → reframe stakes as salt. "Fearmongering isn't your problem; 99.9% of leaders **undersell** the stakes. Kahneman proved people are 2–3x more motivated to avoid a loss than chase a gain. You're not threatening — you're naming what's already true if nothing changes, then handing them the button that fixes it."
- **Worked example:** Allstate "Mayhem" campaign personifies the stakes without fearmongering.
- **Source:** Ch. 8.

### C12. The $5-Bill One-Liner Team Drill (internal close)
- **When to use:** Getting the whole team to memorize and recite the one-liner "as fast as their own name."
- **Pattern:** Offer a $5 bill to any staffer who can recite the one-liner on the spot; drill until it "feels borderline excessive."
- **Worked example:** Be the music star who plays the hit every night — repeat the one-liner on the website, cards, signatures, packaging, social bios.
- **Source:** Ch. 12.

## D — Email & Sequence Bank

### D-SEQ. The StoryBrand Nurture-+-Sales Sequence (one complete sequence)
- **When to use:** The standing email funnel after a lead opts in. Weekly send; repeat monthly.
- **Pattern (cadence):** Week 1 **Email #1 — Nurturing** → Week 2 **Email #2 — Nurturing** → Week 3 **Email #3 — Nurturing** → Week 4 **Email #4 — Sales/Offer (CTA)** → repeat. Footer on every email = logo + one-liner + phone. Always add a **P.S.** ("often the only thing somebody reads").

**Email #1–3 — Nurturing Email**
- **Role:** Build trust + authority; remind customers you exist (even a deleted email "brands" you).
- **Subject pattern:** `Should We [Common Question]?` (A9)
- **Body skeleton (3 parts + P.S.):** (1) Talk about a **problem**; (2) Explain a **plan** to solve it; (3) Describe **how life looks** once solved; **P.S.** soft transitional invite.
- **Worked example (Crest Hill Boarding):** Subject "Should We Free Feed Our Dogs?" → opens with the common question → recommendation (set amount, once/twice daily, discard after 20 min) → payoff (you can monitor health and catch illness early) → warm close "Here's to enjoying our pets for a long, long time." → **P.S.** invites them to bring the dog in.
- **Timing:** Weeks 1–3, one per week.

**Email #4 — Sales / Offer Email**
- **Role:** Make the ask; convert.
- **Subject pattern:** `A solution for [scary version of the problem].` (A10)
- **Body skeleton (4 parts + P.S.):** (1) Talk about a **problem**; (2) Describe **your product** that solves it; (3) Describe **life once solved**; (4) **Direct CTA** to a sale (add scarcity); **P.S.** reinforce the call.
- **Worked example (Crest Hill Boarding):** Subject "A solution for scary boarding." → opens with the internal fear ("you hate leaving your dog behind… locked in a crate next to barking dogs") → positions the product (three staff throwing tennis balls so the dogs sleep at night) → one-time half-price offer (scarcity) → explicit CTA "Call us today at 555-5555." → **P.S.** reinforcing the call.
- **Timing:** Week 4; then loop.

### D2. The Referral Email (standalone)
- **When to use:** Dropped into the referral funnel (B5). Full Pattern + Worked example captured in **C5**.
- **Pattern / Worked example:** See C5 (the "Dear Friend… P.S." model).
- **Source:** Ch. 14 + Referrals.

## E — Frameworks, Models & Templates (with the steps)

### E1. The SB7 Framework (Story in a Nutshell)
- **When to use:** The spine under every other asset. Use to structure any message.
- **Pattern (the master template):** "A **CHARACTER** who wants something encounters a **PROBLEM** before they can get it. At the peak of their despair, a **GUIDE** steps into their lives, gives them a **PLAN**, and **CALLS THEM TO ACTION**. That action helps them avoid **FAILURE** and ends in a **SUCCESS**." 7 modules, each a fixed StoryBrand Principle: (1) Character — *the customer is the hero, not your brand*; (2) Has a Problem — *companies sell solutions to external problems, customers buy solutions to internal problems*; (3) Meets a Guide — *customers want a guide, not another hero*; (4) Who Gives a Plan — *customers trust a guide who has a plan*; (5) Calls to Action — *customers don't act unless challenged*; (6) Helps Avoid Failure — *every human is trying to avoid a tragic ending*; (7) Ends in Success — *never assume people understand how you change their lives; tell them*. **+ 8th block: Identity Transformation (from → to).**
- **Worked example:** Brain-science basis (per "Science Mike"): the brain wants to (a) survive/thrive and (b) conserve calories; story is a "sense-making device" that "makes music out of noise." The enemy of every business = NOISE.
- **Source:** Ch. 1–10.

### E2. The BrandScript One-Pager (the tool)
- **When to use:** The fill-in worksheet you build BEFORE any copy (free at mystorybrand.com). Build order: overall-brand BrandScript first → one per division → one per product → optionally one per segment.
- **Pattern (box layout):** CHARACTER (what the customer wants) → HAS A PROBLEM [VILLAIN | EXTERNAL | INTERNAL | PHILOSOPHICAL] → A GUIDE [EMPATHY | AUTHORITY] → PLAN (3-step process; agreement plan in notes) → CALL TO ACTION [DIRECT | TRANSITIONAL] → FAILURE (~3 bullets, deliberately few — "failure is salt") → SUCCESS (a longer bullet list) → IDENTITY TRANSFORMATION [FROM → TO]. Treat it as the "drum kit"; the website is the "drum solo."
- **Worked example:** "When you're confused, create a StoryBrand BrandScript." Nothing goes on the site that isn't generated by a BrandScript box.
- **Source:** Ch. 2.

### E3. The 7 Survive-&-Thrive Desire Categories
- **When to use:** Mapping the customer's "want" (Character module) to a survival driver so it actually motivates.
- **Pattern:** Map the want onto one of: (1) Conserving financial resources; (2) Conserving time; (3) Building social networks; (4) Gaining status; (5) Accumulating resources (B2B: more revenue/productivity/less waste); (6) The innate desire to be generous; (7) The desire for meaning (Frankl).
- **Worked example:** Walmart → #1 ("Save Money. Live Better"). Rolex/Mercedes/Louis Vuitton → #4 (sell identity/status). Test image: "Imagine your customer is a hitchhiker… the one burning question is: Where are you going?"
- **Source:** Ch. 3.

### E4. The Villain (4 characteristics)
- **When to use:** Personifying the problem so the story has an antagonist.
- **Pattern:** A good villain is (1) a **root source** (not a feeling — "frustration is what a villain makes us feel; high taxes is a good villain"); (2) **relatable**; (3) **singular** (one villain only); (4) **real** (never a fearmonger).
- **Worked example:** Time-management software → vilify "distractions."
- **Source:** Ch. 4.

### E5. The Three Levels of Problem
- **When to use:** Writing the Problem module — the engine of motivation.
- **Pattern:** **External** = the tangible problem (easiest to name, NOT why they buy). **Internal** = the frustration/self-doubt it causes ("Do I have what it takes?") — *the real buying trigger*. **Philosophical** = why it's just/unjust, argued with ought/should.
- **Worked example:** CarMax built a $15B franchise on the **internal** problem (fear of the used-car salesman). Starbucks sells sophistication/belonging, not coffee. Apple addresses intimidation by tech. House painter → external = ugly home, internal = embarrassment, philosophical = neighbors ought to admire your home.
- **Source:** Ch. 4.

### E6. The Brand-Promise Formula (4-line villain grid)
- **When to use:** A one-screen distillation of the Problem module across all four layers (the "climactic/obligatory scene").
- **Pattern:** `Villain: [___] | External: [___] | Internal: [___] | Philosophical: [___].`
- **Worked example:** TESLA — Villain: gas-guzzling inferior tech | External: I need a car | Internal: I want to be an early adopter | Philosophical: my car ought to help save the environment. NESPRESSO — machines that make bad coffee | better coffee at home | feel sophisticated | I shouldn't have to be a barista. EDWARD JONES — firms that don't listen | I need investment help | I'm confused how to do this | I deserve an advisor who explains things in person.
- **Source:** Ch. 4.

### E7. The Guide Formula (Empathy + Authority)
- **When to use:** The Guide module — answers Cuddy's "Can I trust you?" + "Can I respect you?"
- **Pattern:** **Empathy** (a statement of understanding/care — see C2 starters) + **Authority** via the 4 proofs (testimonials ≈3, statistics, awards, logos — see C8).
- **Worked example:** Oprah: people want "to be seen, heard, and understood." Infusionsoft: "125,000 users trust [our] award-winning automation software."
- **Source:** Ch. 5.

### E8. The Plan Formula (Process + Agreement)
- **When to use:** The Plan module — all plans either clarify HOW to do business (process) or REMOVE the sense of risk (agreement).
- **Pattern:** **Process Plan** = 3–6 named steps (C9). **Agreement Plan** = a list of promises countering each fear (C10). Name the plan to raise value (A12).
- **Worked example:** Software process plan → "1) Download the software 2) Integrate your database 3) Revolutionize your customer interaction."
- **Source:** Ch. 6.

### E9. The CTA Formula (Direct + Transitional)
- **When to use:** The Call-to-Action module — always run BOTH side by side (C1).
- **Pattern:** **Direct CTA** (leads to a sale; repeated, bright, top-right + center). **Transitional CTA** (low-risk/free on-ramp: free info/PDF/webinar/podcast/event, testimonials, samples/test-drive, free trial).
- **Worked example:** "Most people think they're overselling when, in truth, their calls to action fall softer than a whisper." StoryBrand's free PDF → thousands of downloads → doubled revenue, $0 ad spend.
- **Source:** Ch. 7.

### E10. The 3 Dominant Story Endings (Success tactics)
- **When to use:** Choosing what success to promise in the Success module.
- **Pattern:** (1) **Win Power/Position (STATUS)** — tactics: offer ACCESS (tiers/points), create SCARCITY ("Limited"), premium TITLE ("Preferred," "Diamond Member," "Emerald Club," nonprofit "Anchor Donor"), IDENTITY ASSOCIATION (Mercedes/Rolex). (2) **Union that makes whole** — REDUCED ANXIETY, REDUCED WORKLOAD ("makes you superhuman"), MORE TIME. (3) **Self-realization/Acceptance** — INSPIRATION (Red Bull, Under Armour), ACCEPTANCE (Dove Real Beauty; Aerie un-retouched), TRANSCENDENCE (TOMS "one for one"; FUBU "For Us By Us").
- **Worked example:** Vision must be SPECIFIC — "put a man on the moon," not "a productive space program" (Stew Friedman: "a compelling image of an achievable future").
- **Source:** Ch. 9.

### E11. The Before/After Grid
- **When to use:** Generating ready-made success copy (Ryan Deiss / DigitalMarketer).
- **Pattern:** Fill 4 rows — **Have / Feel / Average day / Status** — for **BEFORE** vs **AFTER** engaging your brand.
- **Worked example:** The filled cells become headline + bullet copy directly (e.g., Before: "drowning in receipts, anxious, dreads month-end, feels amateur" → After: "audit-ready books, calm, ignores month-end, looks like a pro").
- **Source:** Ch. 9.

### E12. Identity Transformation (FROM → TO)
- **When to use:** The foundational 8th block — defines who the customer becomes; the guide returns at the END to affirm it.
- **Pattern:** `FROM [current identity] → TO [aspirational identity].` Question: "How does your customer want to be described by others?"
- **Worked example:** Pet food: Passive dog owner → Every dog's hero. Financial advisor: Confused and ill-equipped → Competent and smart. Shampoo: Anxious and glum → Carefree and radiant. (Dave Ramsey affirms it with the climactic "Debt-Free Scream.")
- **Source:** Ch. 10.

### E13. The One-Liner Formula
- **When to use:** The verbal BrandScript. Full Pattern + Worked example in **A7**; deploy everywhere (cards, signatures, bios, packaging); drill with the $5-bill game (C12).
- **Pattern:** Character + Problem + Plan + Success.
- **Source:** Ch. 12.

### E14. The Five Things Your Website Should Include
- **When to use:** The homepage checklist — full block recipe in **B1**.
- **Pattern:** (1) An offer above the fold; (2) Obvious CTAs (Z-pattern, repeated, bright); (3) Images of success; (4) A bite-sized breakdown of revenue streams (B2); (5) Very few words ("write it in Morse code").
- **Worked example:** Some best sites use ≤10 sentences total; replace paragraphs with bullets; "read more" links hide long copy.
- **Source:** Ch. 11.

### E15. The On-Mission Internal BrandScript (culture, Ch. 13)
- **When to use:** Engaging employees — run SB7 internally with the TEAM as hero and LEADERSHIP as guide.
- **Pattern (5-step process):** (1) Create a BrandScript with leadership; (2) Audit the existing **thoughtmosphere**; (3) Create a custom On-Mission implementation plan; (4) Optimize internal communications to support it; (5) Install a self-sustained team to run it. Named villains: the **Narrative Void** (no unifying story → disengagement; Gallup ~1 in 5 engaged; ~$450–550B/yr cost) and the **Thoughtmosphere** ("an invisible mixture of beliefs and ideas that drives employee behavior").
- **Worked example:** A fast-food chain went from ~5% to ~30% growth in under 3 years with the same people; onboarding redesigned around the CUSTOMER's story (BrandScript keynote + short film). The CEO's #1 job: "remind the stakeholders what the mission is, over and over."
- **Source:** Ch. 13 (On Mission).

## F — Brand Voice & Brand-Building Language Patterns

> Voice = plainspoken, mildly self-deprecating, movie-obsessed, parable-driven, contrarian-but-warm, with a hard ROI bent. Use these patterns to write ON-brand, richly, not concisely.

1. **Coin a memorable named villain/concept.** Example: "Narrative Void," "thoughtmosphere," "grunt test," "write it in Morse code."
2. **Explain everything through a famous movie.** Example: position the brand as Yoda and the customer as Luke (Star Wars); reference Hunger Games, Moneyball, The King's Speech, Jason Bourne.
3. **Anchor every claim in a behavioral-science name.** Example: "Kahneman proved people are 2–3x more motivated to avoid a loss" (also Cuddy, Frankl, Maslow).
4. **Drop a one-line mantra.** Example: "If you confuse, you'll lose." / "Story makes music out of noise."
5. **Attach a concrete client revenue number to each concept.** Example: "Kyle Shultz cut 90% of his page text and went $25k → $103k."
6. **Empathy line.** Example: "We understand how it feels to leave your dog behind."
7. **Injustice / 'nobody should' line.** Example: "Nobody should have to be a barista to make gourmet coffee."
8. **Survive-and-thrive benefit framing.** Example: frame the offer as saving money, saving time, status, or meaning ("Save Money. Live Better").
9. **Aspirational-identity (from→to) language.** Example: "From a passive dog owner to every dog's hero."
10. **Stakes / loss-aversion language (a pinch of salt).** Example: "Don't postpone your retirement. You've worked too hard for too long to not enjoy time with your grandchildren."
11. **Success / 'taken somewhere' vision language.** Example: "Everybody wants to be taken somewhere" — paint the specific after-state.
12. **Plan-naming language.** Example: "The World's Best Night's Sleep Plan."
13. **Status/scarcity/access language.** Example: "Preferred," "Diamond Member," "Emerald Club," "Limited," "Anchor Donor."
14. **Direct-CTA imperative verbs.** Example: "Buy Now," "Schedule an Appointment," "Register Today."
15. **The P.S. line (most-read line in an email).** Example: "P.S. X Problem can be frustrating. If you'd rather introduce us in person, just let us know."
16. **Morse-code brevity.** Example: replace paragraphs with ≤10 punchy bullets; "write it in Morse code."
17. **"Give the gift of going second" (social proof framing).** Example: lead testimonials so the next customer feels safe being second.
18. **Proof/credibility statistic phrasing.** Example: "125,000 users trust our award-winning software."
19. **Words/phrases to AVOID (jargon & clever).** Example: cut "f-stop," "synergy," "best-in-class," and vague taglines like "Inhale knowledge, exhale success" → replace with "Helping you become the leader everybody loves."
20. **The contrarian invert.** Example: "Most people think they're overselling when, in truth, their calls to action fall softer than a whisper."
