# Extraction Notes — Building a StoryBrand (Donald Miller)

20 structured items across two lenses. Items 1–11 = COACHING methodology lens. Items 12–20 = AGENT-GOVERNANCE lens. All grounded in the actual text of the book.

## COACHING LENS (1–11)

1. **Core thesis — "If you confuse, you'll lose."** Customers don't buy the best products; they buy the products they can understand the fastest. The brain conserves calories and is drawn to clarity, away from confusion. Job #1 of marketing is clarity, not cleverness or beauty.

2. **The customer is the hero, not the brand.** Position the customer as Luke Skywalker and your brand as Yoda (Nancy Duarte's speech research). Brands that play the hero compete with their own customers and lose (cautionary tale: Jay Z's Tidal, $56M, failed by making artists the heroes).

3. **Identify what the hero WANTS.** The catalyst of every story is desire. Define one thing the customer wants relative to your brand ("Find the luxury and rest you've been looking for"; "A hassle-free MBA you can complete after work"). This opens the story gap.

4. **Three levels of problem.** External (tangible — leaky pipe, hunger), Internal (the *feeling* the external problem causes — embarrassment, worry), Philosophical (why it's *wrong* the hero should suffer this). Key line: "Companies tend to sell solutions to external problems, but people buy solutions to internal problems."

5. **Every story needs a villain.** A single, relatable, real, root-source antagonist (e.g. "distractions," "high taxes") gives conflict a focal point. Four villain rules: root source, relatable, singular, real (never fearmonger).

6. **The Guide = Empathy + Authority.** Customers want a guide, not another hero. Empathy ("We understand how it feels to…"; Oprah: people want to be seen, heard, understood) + Authority/competence shown with four light tools: Testimonials (~3, brief), Statistics, Awards, Logos. Amy Cuddy: first impressions answer "Can I trust you? Can I respect you?"

7. **A guide gives a Plan.** Customers trust a guide with a plan — "stepping stones across the rushing creek." Two types: Process Plan (3–4 steps removing the "how do I buy from you" fog) and Agreement Plan (promises that dissolve fear/objections).

8. **Calls to Action — Direct + Transitional.** Characters act only when challenged. Direct CTA ("Buy Now," "Schedule") must be one obvious, repeated, brightly colored button. Transitional CTA (free value: PDF, webinar, demo) "on-ramps" the not-yet-ready ("Can I take you on a date?" vs. "Will you marry me?"). Most brands undersell — CTAs "fall softer than a whisper."

9. **Stakes / Avoiding Failure.** Define what's at stake. Loss aversion (Kahneman, Prospect Theory): people are 2–3x more motivated to avoid a loss than to win a gain. Four-step fear appeal (vulnerable → act → specific fix → do it now). "Failure is salt in the recipe" — a pinch, never a bath; never fearmonger.

10. **Success + Aspirational Identity.** "People want to be taken somewhere." Paint the resolved vision and the from→to transformation — who the customer *becomes* (Gerber's "Hello Trouble" tough/adventurous identity; Dave Ramsey emboldening listeners). Tell people how their life will look; never assume they'll figure it out.

11. **Tools: BrandScript + Grunt Test + one-liner.** BrandScript = one-page capture of all 7 elements (mystorybrand.com); start with the master brand, then divisions, then products. Grunt Test = in 5 seconds a caveman answers: what do you offer / how does it improve my life / what do I do to buy. One-Liner = Character + Problem + Plan + Success, memorized by the whole team.

## AGENT-GOVERNANCE LENS (12–20)

12. **Execution standard — pass the Grunt Test.** No customer-facing copy ships unless a stranger can answer the three grunt-test questions in ≤5 seconds. This is the measurable bar for marketing/web/funnel agents.

13. **The BrandScript is the source of truth.** "The script is the drum kit; the website is the drum solo." No word, image, or idea ships that isn't generated by a BrandScript module. Off-script content = noise. Agents must build/load the script first.

14. **The Five Things every website must have.** (1) Offer above the fold (aspirational identity / solves a problem / states exactly what you do), (2) obvious + repeated CTAs in a Z-pattern (top-right & center, same bright button), (3) images of success (happy customers, not buildings), (4) bite-sized breakdown of revenue streams under one umbrella message, (5) very few words. Governance rubric for any landing/home page.

15. **"Write it in Morse code."** Brief, punchy, scannable. People scan, they don't read. Cut half the words; replace paragraphs with bullets; use "read more" links. Above-the-fold = first-date short.

16. **The Marketing Roadmap = the funnel SOP.** Six near-free moves in order: One-Liner → fix Website → Lead Generator (collect emails) → Automated Email Nurture+Sales Drip → Stories of Transformation → Referral System. This is the canonical persona-grounded funnel build sequence.

17. **Lead generator standard (transitional CTA).** Must (a) provide enormous value and (b) establish authority. Five types: downloadable guide (PDF, ~3 pages), online course/webinar, software demo/free trial, free samples, live events. Title pattern: "[N] Things/Mistakes about [topic]" (e.g. "5 Things Your Website Should Include"). Never offer a "newsletter."

18. **Email nurture + sales drip formulas.** Pattern: 3 nurture emails then 1 sales email, repeatable monthly. Nurture formula = (1) talk about a problem, (2) explain a plan, (3) describe life solved, + a P.S. Sales formula = problem → product that solves it → life solved → direct CTA. Unsubscribes are good; 20% open rate is industry standard.

19. **Decision logic for trust & risk.** Add empathy + ≤3 authority proofs where trust is needed (never bragging = re-hero-ing the brand). Add a plan where the reader hovers but won't commit. Add stakes (a pinch of failure) wherever the story falls flat. Always include the aspirational-identity payoff.

20. **Failure patterns agents must reject.** Brand-as-hero copy; external-only selling; whisper/missing CTAs; walls of words above the fold; no stakes (or fearmongering); clever-over-clear taglines that fail the grunt test. Amateur = comprehensive & confusing; Expert = clear & story-shaped, customer wins.
