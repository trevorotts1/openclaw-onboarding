## A — Headline, Hook & Subject-Line Formula Bank

### A1. The Website Header H1 ("I Help You…" benefit headline)
- **When to use:** the hero/header of a coaching or consulting landing page; the one line a stranger reads first ("the first date").
- **Pattern:** `I Help You [verb the transformation] for [outcome 1] and [outcome 2]` — bold-highlight the two outcome words.
- **Worked example:** "I Help You **Optimize** Your Small Business for **Revenue** and **Profit**." (Revenue / Profit bold-highlighted; sub-byline "Terrence Jones / Business Coach.")
- **Source:** Step 3 — Website, Section 1 Header (wireframe image OCR).

### A2. The Stakes / "Often Fail" Problem Headline
- **When to use:** the second section of a sales page, or any block whose job is urgency. "The problem is the hook."
- **Pattern:** `[Audience] Often Fail for One of [N] Reasons` + subhead `You can avoid these pitfalls`.
- **Worked example:** "Small Businesses Often Fail for One of Six Reasons" / "You can avoid these pitfalls."
- **Source:** Step 3 — Website, Section 2 The Stakes.

### A3. The Stat-Shock Keynote/Email Opener
- **When to use:** opening line of a keynote, webinar, ad, or sales email — names a scary statistic, then defuses it with your mechanism.
- **Pattern:** `Over [X]% of [audience] will [bad outcome] this year. But [bad outcome] shouldn't worry you. Not if you [your mechanism].`
- **Worked example:** "Over 80 percent of small business owners will be surprised by a tax bill or a vendor bill this year. But surprise bills shouldn't worry you. Not if you use five checking accounts to manage your cash flow."
- **Source:** Step 2 — keynote lead-capture tips.

### A4. The Checklist Lead-Magnet Title ("How to … with Only …")
- **When to use:** naming a downloadable checklist lead generator.
- **Pattern:** `How to [achieve desirable outcome] with Only [surprisingly small constraint]`.
- **Worked example:** "How to Run Your Small Business with Only Five Meetings per Week."
- **Source:** Step 4 — Lead-Generator Types (The Checklist).

### A5. The "Never Again" Road-Map Title
- **When to use:** lead magnet or product name that removes a recurring fear.
- **Pattern:** `The [Topic] Road Map—How to Never [recurring pain] Again`.
- **Worked example:** "The Small Business Cash Flow Road Map—How to Never Run Out of Money Again."
- **Source:** Step 4 — Lead-Generator Types (The Checklist).

### A6. The Worksheet / Template Title
- **When to use:** naming a fill-in-the-blank tool lead magnet.
- **Pattern:** `The [Desired Outcome] Template` or `The [Outcome] Creation Worksheet`.
- **Worked example:** "The Perfect Week Template"; "The Tag Line Creation Worksheet."
- **Source:** Step 4 — Lead-Generator Types (The Worksheet).

### A7. The "Reasons You're…" Solution-Article Title
- **When to use:** a teaching article / listicle lead magnet that diagnoses a pain.
- **Pattern:** `[N] Reasons You're [experiencing the costly problem]`.
- **Worked example:** "Ten Reasons You're Paying Too Much in Taxes."
- **Source:** Step 4 — Lead-Generator Types (The Solution Article).

### A8. The "Essential [N]" Authority Title
- **When to use:** a lead magnet that promises the exact, finite set of things that matter.
- **Pattern:** `The [Outcome]: The [N] Essential [components] Necessary to [achieve the outcome]`.
- **Worked example:** "The Perfect Leadership Team: The Three Essential Roles Necessary to Manage Any Small Business."
- **Source:** Step 4 — targeted lead-gen example.

### A9. The "[N] Things Your [thing] Should Include" Title
- **When to use:** an evergreen audit-style lead magnet that makes the reader self-diagnose.
- **Pattern:** `[N] Things Your [asset/area] Should Include`.
- **Worked example:** "Five Things Your Website Should Include."
- **Source:** Step 4 — Miller's own origin lead gens.

### A10. The "[N]-Minute Makeover" Video-Series Title
- **When to use:** a short-form video lead magnet / series.
- **Pattern:** `[N]-Minute [Domain] Makeover`.
- **Worked example:** "Five-Minute Marketing Makeover."
- **Source:** Step 4 — Miller's own origin lead gens.

### A11. The Assessment Lead-Gen Headline
- **When to use:** the opt-in headline for a diagnostic assessment / quiz funnel.
- **Pattern:** `Take My [N]-Minute Assessment and Get a [custom deliverable] To [outcome]`.
- **Worked example:** "Take My 12-Minute Assessment and Get a Custom Plan To Grow Your Business."
- **Source:** Step 3 — Website, Section 7 Lead Generator.

### A12. The "Working with a [Role] Is Easy!" Plan Headline
- **When to use:** heading the 3-step "Plan" section that bridges the prospect's hesitation.
- **Pattern:** `Working with a [Role] is Easy!` followed by exactly 3 steps.
- **Worked example:** "Working with a Coach is Easy!" → (1) Schedule an intake call (2) Get a custom report (3) Enjoy a growing business.
- **Source:** Step 3 — Website, Section 5 The Plan.

### A13. The Numbered-System Email Subject Line
- **When to use:** subject line for a Problem/Solution nurture email that teaches a named system.
- **Pattern:** `[N] [things] You Need to [achieve outcome]`.
- **Worked example:** "5 Meetings You Need to Manage Your Small Business."
- **Source:** Step 4 — nurture email (Problem/Solution).

### A14. The "[N] Steps to…" How-To Subject Line
- **When to use:** subject line for a Step-by-Step nurture email.
- **Pattern:** `[N] steps to [achieving a specific task]`.
- **Worked example:** "Three steps to hiring a virtual assistant."
- **Source:** Step 4 — nurture email (Step-by-Step).

### A15. The 7-Email Sales-Sequence Subject Arc
- **When to use:** naming the seven sales emails so each does one job.
- **Pattern:** (1) `Good to meet you` → (2) `Thank you + [the offer]` → (3) `[empathy/origin]` → (4) `[testimony + CTA]` → (5) `[overcome the #1 objection]` → (6) `Time is running out` → (7) `Final reminder`.
- **Worked example:** Email 1 "Good to Meet You"; Email 6 "Time Is Running Out"; Email 7 "Final Reminder" closing on the word HOPE.
- **Source:** Step 4 — The 7 Sales Emails.

### A16. The Explanatory-Paragraph Headline ("You Don't Have to … Alone")
- **When to use:** heading the long-form explanatory paragraph deep on a sales page.
- **Pattern:** `It's Hard Enough to [do the hard thing]. You Don't Have to [do it] Alone.`
- **Worked example:** "It's Hard Enough to Build a Business. You Don't Have to Build It Alone."
- **Source:** Step 3 — Website, Section 6 Explanatory Paragraph.

### A17. The Named-Program Title Formula
- **When to use:** naming a paid product so it sounds specific, premium, and benefit-loaded (never "coaching").
- **Pattern:** `[Brand/Descriptor] [Outcome verb-phrase] [Format word: Program / Workshop / Mastermind / Flight School / Audit / Playbook]`.
- **Worked example:** "Double Your Sales Transformation Program"; "Small Business Flight School Mastermind"; "Product Profitability Audit"; "Leadership Alignment Workshop."
- **Source:** Step 1 — Starter / Advanced Menus.

### A18. The One-Liner / Elevator Pitch (Customer-Is-the-Hero in one sentence)
- **When to use:** card backs, header sub-copy, networking, social bios — the portable curiosity hook.
- **Pattern:** `[Audience] often [problem]. I help them [product as solution] so they [result].`
- **Worked example:** "Small business owners often feel like their people are wearing too many hats and nobody knows their real job description. I help them install a simple leadership system so the business runs without the owner."
- **Source:** Step 5 — Curiosity tools / one-liner.

### B1. The Coaching Sales Website — 7-Section Wireframe (the flagship recipe)
- **When to use:** the primary sales page / homepage for any coach, consultant, or expert. Build the TEXT (wireframe) before any design — "text is the hardest and most important part." Each scroll = the "next date"; more copy is allowed the deeper you go.
- **Pattern (block order + each block's job + length):**
  1. **HEADER** — says 3 things in one glance: what you offer, how it makes their life better, what to do next. Logo + nav-CTA + H1 benefit headline + button + coach byline + 3 offering icons. *Short.*
  2. **THE STAKES** — "the problem is the hook." Headline naming how/why they fail + numbered list of the problems you solve + one-line closer + repeat CTA. *Short-medium.*
  3. **MENU OF SERVICES** — short blurb per product, each with a NAME, a STATED PRICE, and its own button; consider linking each to its own page. *Medium.*
  4. **PROOF OF VALUE** — answers "Will this work for me?" with statistics OR a testimonial the prospect can "see a future version of themself" in. *Medium.*

  6. **EXPLANATORY PARAGRAPH** — long-form, uses the Customer-Is-the-Hero framework; "many clients tell me they only decided to purchase after reading the explanatory paragraph." *Long.*

### B2. The Lead-Generator Opt-In / Assessment Page
- **When to use:** the standalone opt-in that captures email in exchange for a checklist/worksheet/assessment/interview/solution-article; ">50 percent of your future business depends on a lead generator and weekly automated emails."
- **Pattern:** `[Assessment/Resource headline: Take My [N]-min [tool] and get [deliverable]]` → `[1-2 sentence problem-framed why]` → `[single button CTA]` → `[visual of the deliverable]` → fire weekly automated emails on submit. ### B3. The "Sell From the Stage" Webinar / Keynote-to-Offer Funnel
- **When to use:** monetizing a talk, webinar, or virtual presentation into immediate enrollments.
- **Pattern:** `[Deliver value-rich talk]` → `[in the final minutes, name a productized offer]` → `[show a StoryBrand/Customer-Is-the-Hero landing page on screen]` → `[a live "SIGN UP NOW" button that takes payment immediately]` → `[scarcity: limited cohort]` → `[upsell graduates into workshops]`.
- **Worked example:** Coach Jason Daily turned a $4,000 keynote into ~$50,000: built a 4-week group coaching program, a StoryBrand 7-part landing page, a "SIGN UP NOW" button taking payment, and pitched it in the last minutes of a 75-minute talk to 500+ viewers; two cohorts filled in 30 minutes; +$10,000 in later workshops.

### B4. The Keynote Lead-Capture Funnel (QR shown twice)
- **When to use:** in-person or virtual speaking where the goal is opt-ins, not immediate sales.
- **Pattern:** `[Open by stating the problem (stat-shock)]` → `[teach valuable content]` → `[show lead-generator QR code MID-talk]` → `[show the SAME QR at the END]` → `[make the final line a restated problem + the lead-gen offer]`. Showing the offer twice ≈ "100 percent increase" in opt-ins; "up to 98 percent of an audience opt in" when shown twice.
- **Worked example:** Open: "Over 80 percent of small business owners will be surprised by a tax bill… Not if you use five checking accounts to manage your cash flow." Close: restate the problem + "scan the code to get the Five-Account Cash Flow checklist."
- **Source:** Step 2 — keynote presentations.

- **When to use:** converting a curious lead into a paying client via a diagnostic conversation.

### B7. The Agency / Annual-Conference Value-Ladder Funnel
- **When to use:** scaling past solo into a multi-coach agency (the seven-figure model).
- **Pattern:** `[Build the list via lead generators]` → `[sell paid intro sessions]` → `[host ONE large annual conference (200+) as a low-cost entry product]` → `[upsell 6-month small groups (led by your coaches)]` → `[upsell to leadership/management/marketing/sales workshops]` → `[upsell to YOUR personal elite mastermind]`.
- **Worked example (math):** 4 coaches × 4 ten-person flagship groups × $5,000 = $200k/coach = $800k; pay coaches ~$125k → ~$300k profit; add workshops + your own elite mastermind → $500k+. Staff: you + an EA/event planner + a freelance marketer + 4 freelance coaches. Model exemplar: Howard Partridge (annual 3-day event + bimonthly small groups led by his coaches + one elite group + books/speaking).

## C — Script Bank (sales / objection / follow-up / discovery / close)

### C1. The Six Qualifying Questions (discovery question set)
- **When to use:** a discovery/intake conversation or networking chat to qualify a lead and create problem-awareness — ask the question matched to each of the six problem areas.
- **Pattern:** ask one diagnostic either/or question per pillar (Leadership / Marketing / Sales / Product / Management / Cash Flow), each framed as "Is [good state] or do you [pain state]?"
- **Worked example:** Leadership — "Is your business focused on three economic priorities or does it feel like you are always diving for dollars?" · Marketing — "Is your marketing working or are you struggling to get the word out about what you offer?" · Sales — "Do you think your sales effort could be more effective or is your team closing big sales every day?" · Product — "Does it seem like your profit margins have been shrinking?" · Management — "Do you have a repeatable set of meetings that allow you to organize and inform your team so you can reach your goals?" · Cash Flow — "Do you feel good about your rainy-day fund or do you always feel like you're treading water financially?"
- **Source:** Step 2 — The 6 Qualifying Questions.

### C2. The Plug-and-Play Networking Close (card + segment)
- **When to use:** turning a live "we have that problem" admission into a captured, segmented lead.
- **Pattern:** `[Qualifying question]` → `[when they admit the problem]` → "I have a process that will solve that problem… Give me your card and I'll send you some information." → `[write the topic on the back of the card]` → `[segment in CRM]`.
- **Worked example:** "Does every member of your team have a clear job description or do you feel like people are making it up as they go along?" → "I have a process that will solve that problem. Give me your card and I'll send you some information." (writes "job descriptions / leadership" on the card back).
- **Source:** Step 2 — qualifying-question follow-on.

### C3. The "Phrases You'll Hear" Listening Cheat Sheet (diagnostic script)
- **When to use:** real-time listening on calls — map what the prospect SAYS to the underlying problem and product.
- **Pattern:** when you hear `[verbatim complaint]` → tag `[problem area]` → route to `[product + email segment]`.
- **Worked example:** "We are basically making it up as we go. Everybody is wearing a bunch of different hats." → Leadership. · "We aren't sure how to talk about what we offer." → Messaging. · "We don't know how to close the big deal… basically just taking orders." → Sales. · "People love our products but they only buy one and then we never hear from them again." → Products. · "I need help organizing my company so it's less frustrating and more productive." → Operations. · "We are always running out of cash. Bills just seem to come out of nowhere." → Cash Flow.
- **Source:** Step 2 — "Phrases You'll Hear."

### C4. The Segmentation Survey (self-select biggest challenge)
- **When to use:** drop as the 3rd/4th nurture email or a form field to segment the list by problem.
- **Pattern:** "Which of these is your biggest challenge right now?" + a list of one-line pains, each mapped to a problem area.
- **Worked example:** "We need to generate more revenue / We are paying too much in taxes / Our marketing strategy isn't working / We aren't aligned around a common mission / Nobody really knows what they are supposed to be doing / We are not closing sales / Our profit margins are shrinking / We are losing market share to the competition."
- **Source:** Step 2 — Segmentation Survey.

### C5. The Customer-Is-the-Hero Call-to-Action Line
- **When to use:** the direct ask that closes any email, page, or talk — a single, easy, calendar-based next step.
- **Pattern:** `I have made room on my schedule to talk to [audience] about [problem] every [day]. To schedule [N] minutes with me, simply [click this link].`
- **Worked example:** "I have made room on my schedule to talk to small business owners about these challenges every Monday. To schedule thirty minutes with me, simply click on this link."
- **Source:** Step 4 — Customer Is the Hero, Step 6 CTA.

### C6. The "No Time" Objection Handler (the #1 objection)
- **When to use:** prospect says "I don't have time for coaching." Reframe the objection as the exact problem you solve.
- **Pattern:** `[Acknowledge]` → `[Reframe: lack of time IS the problem we solve]` → `[name the mechanism: own vs. manage]` → `[repeat CTA]`.
- **Worked example:** "Not having time is the very problem our Small Business Community Groups solve. We help you build systems so you OWN your business rather than MANAGE your business. Let's get your time back." (Email #5.)
- **Source:** Step 4 — Sales Email #5 "Overcome Objection."

### C7. The 10x-Return Guarantee Script
- **When to use:** de-risking a premium offer for a QUALIFIED client; pairs with the 10% pricing rule.
- **Pattern:** "I only take clients who are positioned to get a return: [criteria]. For those clients I offer [a full refund] if they don't get a [10x] return. I am not looking for a winning record; I am looking for a perfect record."
- **Worked example:** "I offer a full refund… if my client does not get a ten-times return." Qualifiers: good business, good team, resources, ability to make more money. "I am not looking for a winning record; I am looking for a perfect record."
- **Source:** Step 1 — Pricing Doctrine (the 10% Rule + guarantee).

### C8. The "Starting At" Price-Flexibility Line
- **When to use:** quoting a premium price while preserving room to scope up; protects perceived value.
- **Pattern:** `[Product] starts at $[anchor].` (Never lead with a discount; "when you reduce your prices, you reduce the perceived value.")
- **Worked example:** "The Marketing Strategy Workshop starts at $5,000." (Anecdote: Miller doubled his speaking honorarium to reduce bookings → requests INCREASED.)
- **Source:** Step 1 — Value-Perception Pricing.

### C9. The Intake-Call Positioning Script
- **When to use:** describing the free discovery/intake call so it feels safe and valuable, not "salesy."
- **Pattern:** "In a [30]-minute intake call, you and I will talk about [what's holding you back] and [where you want to go]. Then I'll send you a [custom report / plan]."
- **Worked example:** "In a thirty-minute intake call, you and I will talk about what's holding you back and where you want your business to go… I'll send you a custom report about how we could work together to grow your small business."
- **Source:** Step 3 — The Plan (Section 5).

### C10. The Pivot-Announcement Phone CTA
- **When to use:** an existing coach expanding into a new field / new program, asking for a call.
- **Pattern:** `[Announce the new program]` → `[scarcity: room for N more]` → `[phone/booking CTA]` → `[referral ask]`.
- **Worked example:** "I've opened a management mastermind and have room for five more small business owners… call me and let's see if it's a fit." (Result anecdote: friend Brad sent one of these and "within six months was billing $45,000 per month.")
- **Source:** Step 2 — Pivot-Announcement (Swipe Template #2).

### C11. The Lunch-to-Assessment Follow-Up
- **When to use:** after a prospect lunch — convert the warm conversation into a captured diagnostic lead.
- **Pattern:** `[Thank for lunch]` → `[send the assessment link]` → `[book the review]`.
- **Worked example:** after lunch, "send the MyBusinessReport.com link and follow up" → review the surfaced problems → invite into the flagship.
- **Source:** "A Week in the Life" — Monday/Wednesday lunch blocks.

### C12. The Five-Meetings Teaching Script (give-the-why)
- **When to use:** a content/sales conversation that teaches a complete system to earn trust while withholding the "how to install it" (which is the paid product).
- **Pattern:** name the system → list its components in one line each → end with "implementing these is what we do together."
- **Worked example:** "There are five meetings that run a small business: an all-staff meeting, a leadership meeting, a daily department stand-up, a weekly personal priority speed check, and a quarterly performance review. Knowing them is the why; installing them in your calendar is the how — that's what we do together."
- **Source:** Step 4 — nurture email (Problem/Solution) + Give-the-Why-Sell-the-How rule.

## D — Email & Sequence Bank

### D1. The Complete 7-Email Sales Sequence (every email, in order)
- **When to use:** the close sequence fired after a lead is qualified or downloads a lead magnet; runs ~7 emails, then rolls into the ongoing nurture track. Flagship product in the worked examples = the "Small Business Community Group."
- **Pattern + Worked example, email by email:**
  - **Email 1 — "Good to Meet You."** *Role:* recall the conversation + establish empathy/authority. *Subject pattern:* `Good to meet you, [Name]`. *Body skeleton:* warm callback → empathy for their problem → authority (you've solved it) → soft mention of the resource. *Worked example line:* "the only people who struggle with cash flow management are the people who are capable of building a company that brings in cash… the problem is a problem of success." (Signed "Caitlyn Smith, Caitlyn Small Business Coaching"; sells "Small Business Cash Flow Made Simple" — the FIVE checking accounts system.) *Timing:* immediately after contact.
  - **Email 2 — "Thank You + Clarification of Offer."** *Role:* after a lead-magnet download, define the problem and lay out the offer's goal. *Subject pattern:* `Thanks for downloading [the lead magnet]`. *Body skeleton:* thank → restate the problem → list what the program does. *Worked example:* after "Small Business Growth Plan Checklist," names the Small Business Community Group goal — "double your revenue" via rewrite mission / clarify message / overhaul sales / optimize products / streamline operations / manage cash flow. (Signed Jim Spencer.) *Timing:* ~day 2.
  - **Email 3 — "Empathy & Competency" (origin story).** *Role:* prove you've lived their problem; position as the guide. *Subject pattern:* `I've been where you are`. *Body skeleton:* personal origin story → the lesson → "every business needs the exact same process." *Worked example:* "I owned a car wash business, or, rather, a car wash business owned me… three locations… organized chaos." (Signed Jim Spencer.) *Timing:* ~day 4.
  - **Email 4 — "Testimony & Call to Action."** *Role:* social proof + scarcity. *Subject pattern:* `How [Name] grew [result]`. *Body skeleton:* testimonial with a number → scarcity → CTA. *Worked example:* "My revenue has increased by 57 percent…" —John Gerber; "we're making room for ten… we will almost certainly fill up." *Timing:* ~day 6.

### E2. The Customer Is the Hero Sales Framework (6 steps)

Underpinning: empathy + authority = "the guide."
- **Worked example:** Problem — "Small business owners often feel like their people are wearing too many hats and nobody knows what their real job description is." → Solution — position your leadership program. → Bridge — the 3-step plan. → Negative stakes — chaos, burnout, lost revenue. → Positive stakes — a calm, self-running company. → CTA — "click to schedule thirty minutes every Monday."

## F — Brand Voice & Brand-Building Language Patterns

> Miller's voice = warm, plainspoken, confidence-instilling, lightly self-deprecating, anecdote-driven (real names: Brek, Nicole Burke/Gardenary, Howard Partridge, Mignon Francois), film/story metaphors (hero/guide, story loop, climactic scene), and every section ends with a forward push.

### F1. The Homespun "Vague Offer" Analogy
- **Pattern:** compare a vague offer to a `[mundane, absurd vagueness]` to make it feel ridiculous.
- **Example:** "Nobody wants to pull out their debit card and buy a patch of fog." / "Offering 'business advice' on a retainer is akin to a restaurant that advertises 'food items'" (vs. naming the cheeseburger).

### F2. The "You Sell Money" Value Reframe
- **Pattern:** reframe the service as literally selling a return.
- **Example:** "As a small business coach, you sell money." / "If there was a store where people could buy $1,000 for $500, there would be a line around the block."

### F3. The Mechanic/Doctor Diagnostic Framing
- **Pattern:** frame your assessment as a `[trusted professional]`'s diagnostic.
- **Example:** "Think of the assessment like a diagnostic check a mechanic might perform on an automobile or a doctor might perform on your body."

### F4. The Fly-Fishing Persistence Metaphor
- **Pattern:** equate follow-up emails to keeping bait in the water.
- **Example:** "Every email is a cast of the fly… If you don't keep a hook in the water, you will never catch a fish."

### F5. The Rocket/Orbit "Hardest Stage" Metaphor
- **Pattern:** reassure that the solo/early stage is the hardest.
- **Example:** "Getting a rocket off the launching pad takes much more energy than whirling it around the earth once in orbit."

### F6. The Dating-Stages Analogy (relationship arc)
- **Pattern:** map the buyer journey to dating to justify pacing.
- **Example:** Curiosity → Enlightenment → Commitment = "first date / second date / you don't propose marriage on the first date."

### F7. Empathy + Authority = "The Guide"
- **Pattern:** position the brand as the guide, the client as the hero; lead with empathy then competence.
- **Example:** "Your pain is my pain." / "an empathetic authority figure."

### F8. The "Competency Follows Action" Maxim
- **Pattern:** push past insecurity by acting.
- **Example:** "We all feel insecure until we become competent." / "Competency and confidence will only follow action. You can't dream competency into existence."

### F9. The Closing Transformation Maxim (forward push)
- **Pattern:** end with agency + a transformation promise.
- **Example:** "This book will not transform you, but taking action on the steps certainly will… The question is whether or not you will do the work… You are in complete control of the outcome. Here's to your transformation."

### F10. The Stat-Shock Power Hook
- **Pattern:** open with a fear statistic, then offer the escape.
- **Example:** "65 percent of small businesses fail." / "Over 80 percent of small business owners will be surprised by a tax bill or a vendor bill this year."

### F11. The Problem-First Sentence Structure
- **Pattern:** "[Audience] often feel like [emotionally specific problem]…" — open every asset with THEIR problem, not your features.
- **Example:** "Small business owners often feel like their people are wearing too many hats and nobody knows what their real job description is."

### F12. The "Problem of Success" Reframe
- **Pattern:** flatter the prospect by reframing their pain as evidence of competence.
- **Example:** "the only people who struggle with cash flow management are the people who are capable of building a company that brings in cash… the problem is a problem of success."

### F13. The "Own vs. Manage" Antithesis
- **Pattern:** contrast a draining verb with a freeing verb to sell the system.
- **Example:** "OWN your business rather than MANAGE your business."

### F14. The Bold-Highlight Power Words
- **Pattern:** in a headline, bold the 1–2 outcome words the buyer most wants.
- **Example:** "I Help You Optimize Your Small Business for **Revenue** and **Profit**."

### F15. The Named-Program Naming Pattern
- **Pattern:** never sell "coaching"; sell a named asset with a format word — Flight School / Community Group / Mastermind / Workshop / Audit / Playbook / Protocol / Road Map.
- **Example:** "Small Business Flight School Mastermind"; "Firing an Employee Protocol"; "Cash Flow Road Map."

### F16. The Negative-then-Positive Stakes Pattern
- **Pattern:** paint what's lost by NOT acting, then foreshadow the climactic win.
- **Example:** Negative — "most small businesses fail." Positive — "In only six months you will not recognize yourself or your business. You will move from chaos to efficiency and from confusion to confidence."

### F17. The "Give the Why, Sell the How" Content Rule
- **Pattern:** teach the complete WHY for free; sell the installation/HOW.
- **Example:** "Give away the WHY but sell the HOW" — teach the five meetings; sell installing them.

### F18. The Result-Number Testimonial Format
- **Pattern:** select testimonials where the prospect sees "a future version of themself," and lead with a number.
- **Example:** "My revenue has increased by 57 percent…" —John Gerber. "Ever since I hired Terrence… my business is growing and I have a plan."

### F19. Offer / Guarantee Language
- **Pattern:** de-risk with a waiver or a multiple-return guarantee.
- **Example:** "I'd like to waive your first month's payment for the flagship small group." / "a full refund if my client does not get a ten-times return."

### F20. Urgency / Scarcity Language
- **Pattern:** real cohort caps + real next-availability dates.
- **Example:** "we're making room for ten… we will almost certainly fill up." / "If you don't sign up soon, I won't be offering another small group for at least six months."

### F21. CTA Language Patterns
- **Pattern:** one calendar-anchored action verb phrase; reused everywhere on the page.
- **Example:** "Schedule a Discovery Call" · "Take the Assessment" · "Let's get your time back."

### F22. The "Largest Billboard" Self-Presentation Line
- **Pattern:** remind the founder THEY are the advertisement.
- **Example:** "You are the largest billboard for your business."

### F23. Words/Phrases to AVOID
- **Pattern:** ban fog, modesty-as-vagueness, and discount-from-fear language.
- **Example:** avoid "business advice," "food items," "it depends," generic "coaching," hidden prices, and reflexive discounts ("when you reduce your prices, you reduce the perceived value").
