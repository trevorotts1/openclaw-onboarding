## A — Headline, Hook & Subject-Line Formula Bank

> The book has no direct-response headline formulas, but it has a repeatable set of **barrier-lowering, permission-giving, contrarian-reframe hooks** that are pure brand voice. These convert dense or intimidating subject matter into "you can do this" copy.

### A1. The "X-Plus" Familiar-Anchor Reframe Hook
- **When to use:** Introducing a new/intimidating capability. Lowers the barrier by anchoring the new thing to something the audience already does, then "+ amplify."
- **Pattern:** `[New thing] is just [the thing you already do], with [added element] to amplify [benefit].`
- **Worked example:** "I call sketchnotes **Notes Plus** because they're like the notes you already take, with visual elements added to amplify ideas."
- **Source:** Ch. 1 (Sketchnoting Quickstart).

### A2. The Capability-Permission Hook
- **When to use:** First line of a page/post that meets a "but I can't…" fear head-on. Names the intimidating thing, then immediately grants permission.
- **Pattern:** `Can YOU [do the intimidating thing]? YES!`
- **Worked example:** "Can you create sketchnotes? **YES!**" (paired with the reframe: "it's about good structure, not art").
- **Source:** Ch. 1.

### A3. The ALL-CAPS Positioning Line
- **When to use:** A hand-lettered hero/banner line that states the single big promise of the method in one breath.
- **Pattern:** `[SUBJECT] [EMPHASIZE / IS ABOUT] [THE ONE BIG PROMISE].`
- **Worked example:** "SKETCHNOTES EMPHASIZE CAPTURING BIG IDEAS IN THE MOMENT."
- **Source:** Ch. 1.

### A4. The Permission Mantra Headline (anti-perfectionism)
- **When to use:** Reassurance copy / subhead that dissolves perfectionism and gets the reader to start. Rohde's signature move.
- **Pattern:** `[Imperfect-state noun] ARE OKAY.` OR `[The value], NOT [perfection].`
- **Worked example:** "MESSY SKETCHNOTES ARE OKAY." · "PROGRESS, NOT PERFECTION." · "Stay Loose • Work Quickly • Get Crazy!"
- **Source:** Ch. 3 (Idea Mapping), Ch. 5 (Documentation), Ch. 2 (Idea Generation).

### A5. The Chapter-Promise Benefit Subhead
- **When to use:** A one-line section opener that states what the method does + where it applies (great for page section headers and carousel cover slides).
- **Pattern:** `[Method] is [a great tool / well suited / an effective way] for [outcome][, secondary context].`
- **Worked example:** "Sketchnoting is a great tool for capturing ideas, on your own or while working with a team." · "Sketchnoting is an effective way to convert information into concise visual documents that communicate processes and ideas."
- **Source:** Section/chapter openers (Ch. 2, 4, 5).

### A6. The "X, Not Y" Contrarian Reframe
- **When to use:** Flipping the reader's wrong assumption about where value lives — the highest-converting line in the book.
- **Pattern:** `It's about [the real lever], not [the assumed/decorative lever].`
- **Worked example:** "The foundation of a good sketchnote isn't about **art** — it's about good **structure**." · "Ideas, not art."
- **Source:** Ch. 1.

### A7. The Action-CTA Closer (all-caps imperative stack)
- **When to use:** Closing a section/post by killing hesitation and demanding one small physical action.
- **Pattern:** `ENOUGH [talk/theory] — IT'S TIME FOR [action]. [Imperative]. [Imperative]. LET'S [verb]!`
- **Worked example:** "ALL RIGHT, ENOUGH TALK — IT'S TIME FOR ACTION. GRAB A NOTEBOOK AND A PEN. LET'S SKETCHNOTE!"
- **Source:** Ch. 1.

### A8. The "Experiments, Not Assignments" De-Risk Subhead
- **When to use:** Above any exercise/lead-magnet/challenge to remove fear of failure and raise completion.
- **Pattern:** `These are [low-pressure frame], not [high-pressure frame]. [Permission to fail + have fun].`
- **Worked example:** "This book and its exercises should be seen as **experiments and not assignments**. Have fun!" · "Try new things, don't fear mistakes, and have fun!"
- **Source:** "How to use this book" + closing manifesto.

### A9. The Benefit-List Lead-In Pattern (icon + ALL-CAPS lead)
- **When to use:** A scannable benefit block (page, deck, or carousel) — each item is an icon + bold all-caps lead-in + one supporting sentence.
- **Pattern:** `[ICON] **[ALL-CAPS BENEFIT WORD]** — [one supporting sentence].` (Repeat 4–6×; the lead words often alliterate.)
- **Worked example (the Documentation "C-words"):** "**COMPACT** — say more in less space. **COMMUNICATE** — image and text reinforce each other. **SCANNABLE** — readers find what they need fast. **FUN** — drawings draw readers in."
- **Source:** Ch. 5 (benefits list); pattern repeats in every chapter.

### A10. The Mnemonic Mantra (3-beat rule of three)
- **When to use:** A memorable, hand-letterable rule that the audience repeats — great for social captions and brand taglines.
- **Pattern:** `[Verb 1] • [Verb 2] • [Verb 3]!` (short, punchy, imperative).
- **Worked example:** "Stay Loose • Work Quickly • Get Crazy!" · "Observe and Capture." · "The best camera is the one you have with you."
- **Source:** Ch. 2, Ch. 5, Ch. 6.

## B — Funnel & Page Recipes (page-by-page)

> This is a visual-document book, so "page recipes" = **reusable page-LAYOUT templates for visual notes/infographics/explainer sections**, not paid-funnel pages. The full set is captured below (block order + what each zone holds + its job). Marketing funnel pages (opt-in / sales letter / VSL / checkout / upsell) are **ABSENT** — see Section H and route to Brunson/StoryBrand.

### B1. The 7 Structure-Pattern Page Skeletons (choose ONE before you draw)
- **When to use:** The very first decision on any visual page/infographic/carousel — pick the skeleton that matches the content type, then place elements into it.
- **Pattern (the menu + what each is for):**
  1. **LINEAR** — like a book, left→right, top→bottom. *For:* sequential narrative, a talk recap.
  2. **RADIAL** — central hub + spokes. *For:* one topic with many subpoints (hub-and-spoke).
  3. **VERTICAL** — runs top→bottom. *For:* timelines, stacked steps, mobile-shaped social.
  4. **PATH** — winds across the page. *For:* a journey/process with a sense of travel.
  5. **MODULAR** — broken into discrete sections/boxes. *For:* parallel categories.
  6. **SKYSCRAPER** — aligned in columns. *For:* comparisons, parallel lists.
  7. **POPCORN** — placed randomly/where it lands. *For:* freeform brainstorm, loose collection.
- **Worked example:** A conference-talk recap → LINEAR; a product's feature wheel → RADIAL; a "5 steps to X" carousel → VERTICAL or PATH; a pricing/feature comparison → SKYSCRAPER columns.
- **Source:** Ch. 1 (Sketchnote Structure Patterns).

### B2. The Idea-Generation GRID Format
- **When to use:** Brainstorming many ideas fast with structure that "frees you to focus on creating new ideas" and beats blank-page paralysis.
- **Pattern (block order):** TOPIC TITLE (top) → specs/requirements line (under title) → a numbered GRID (4 large cells = more detail; 10–12 small cells = many quick ideas) → number each cell as you fill it.
- **Worked example:** "Lemonade-stand brainstorm" grid — cells for locations, material cost, pricing, stand design, hours; 4-panel grid, one concept per cell, numbered 1→4.
- **Source:** Ch. 2 (3 page formats).

### B3. The Idea-Generation RADIAL Format
- **When to use:** One central idea you want to explode outward into sub-ideas.
- **Pattern:** Main idea/topic/reference in a **CENTRAL HUB** → notes, drawings, icons placed in the spaces around the hub → separate sub-ideas with dividers.
- **Worked example:** Tree-house brainstorm Part 2 — pick the best grid idea, redraw it tighter in a radial hub with spokes for materials, safety, features.
- **Source:** Ch. 2.

### B4. The Idea-Generation FREEFORM Format
- **When to use:** "The ultimate in flexibility" — let ideas dictate placement.
- **Pattern:** Wide-open page → start anywhere → let each idea dictate where the next goes → **number each step as you go** so you can reconstruct your thought process.
- **Worked example:** Freeform lemonade-stand pass — same criteria as the grid, but placed organically and numbered 1, 2, 3… across the page.
- **Source:** Ch. 2. ("Numbering your ideas helps you reconstruct your thought process.")

### B5. The Grid-Cell Micro-Template
- **When to use:** Inside any grid cell (B2) — the smallest reusable unit.
- **Pattern:** `[ Concept name (bold) | Notes & observations | optional tag-icon ]`
- **Worked example:** Cell = "**Pricing** / $1 cup, $5 pitcher / 💡 great-idea icon."
- **Source:** Ch. 2.

### B6. The Idea-Map Layout (research → visual document)
- **When to use:** Turning gathered research into one visual map (beats writer's block; gives a "unified perspective").
- **Pattern (block order):** Choose a surface (bigger = more ideas; smaller = more focus) → **TOPIC headline at top** (center/left/right) → map ideas as concise text + drawings, **leaving MARGIN space around each item** to append later → review & connect with arrows/connectors → mark repeated **patterns** with icons → if it gets messy, **redraw a cleaner second map.**
- **Worked example:** A dissertation/report mapped from research themes; a 4th-grader's "missions report" mapped before writing — themes as nodes, arrows for connections, icons on repeats.
- **Source:** Ch. 3 (7 Idea-Map steps).

### B7. The Travel-Plan Page (named zones)
- **When to use:** A single visual page that holds an entire trip (also a model for any "one page holds the whole plan" explainer).
- **Pattern (named zones):** DESTINATION/**TRIP TITLE** (top) → **FLIGHTS** → **LODGING** → **WHERE TO EAT** → **TRANSPORTATION** → **PEOPLE TO VISIT** → **SPECIFIC DETAILS** (reservations, maps, sights) → icons for each zone (ticket/flight/taxi/bus) + bold section type + dividers + arrows → photograph it to share.
- **Worked example:** An SXSW trip page — title banner, flight block, hotel block, eats list, transit icons, a sights checklist, then snapped and texted to the family.
- **Source:** Ch. 4 (Planning Trips; travel-plan diagram).

### B8. The Sketchnoted Persona Card (Agile/UX)
- **When to use:** A portable, memorable user-persona card (index-card sized) for product/UX/marketing avatar work.
- **Pattern (card layout):** **User name** (bold) + drawn **portrait** ("a good reminder the persona represents a real person") → **Quick Stats** (age/role/location) → **Goals** → **Needs / Pains** → **Preferred Tools** (icons) → **Skill-Level scale** (Novice ●●○○ Expert).
- **Worked example:** "Maria, 34, Operations Lead, Austin — Goals: ship faster; Pains: manual handoffs; Tools: [Slack][Sheets]; Skill: Novice ●●●○ Expert."
- **Source:** Ch. 4 (Agile/Scrum Sketchnoting).

### B9. The Sketchnoted Sprint-Plan Board
- **When to use:** A movable, visual sprint/backlog plan for any project rollout.
- **Pattern:** Product Backlog Items (PBIs) on **cards ordered LESS-important → MORE-important** → tasks per PBI on **sticky notes** (movable). PBI sticky template = Task name + timeboxed-hours icon + days-in-progress + estimated time remaining.
- **Worked example:** PBI "Onboarding email" card → stickies "draft copy ⏱2h · 1d in · 1h left," "design header ⏱1h," re-ordered as priorities shift.
- **Source:** Ch. 4 (Sketchnoted Sprint Plan).

### B10. The Sketchnoted Retrospective Board (two-column)
- **When to use:** A team feedback/retro page (also a "pros vs cons" or "keep vs change" social or report layout).
- **Pattern:** Divide the board in two — **"what works"** | **"what can be improved"** → team adds sticky feedback to each side → photograph/scan to share.
- **Worked example:** Retro board after a launch — left: "fast standups, clear owners"; right: "QA too late, unclear scope."
- **Source:** Ch. 4 (Sketchnoted Retrospective).

### B11. The Recipe / Step-by-Step Layout (tools-left, steps-right)
- **When to use:** Any "how to do X" explainer, recipe, or onboarding doc.
- **Pattern:** **Tools + ingredients/inputs on the LEFT** → **numbered steps on the RIGHT** → NUMBER the steps → tie steps with arrows → (for concept teaching where order doesn't matter, use images over numbers — see "Sketchplanations," E6).
- **Worked example:** "Inverted AeroPress" — left column: AeroPress, grinder, kettle, scale; right column: ① grind ② add coffee ③ add water ④ flip ⑤ press.
- **Source:** Ch. 5 (Step-by-step / Recipes).

### B12. The Structured-Task Template (the "court-hearing" pattern)
- **When to use:** A reusable form-style page for any recurring structured event (meeting, intake, hearing, discovery call).
- **Pattern:** Title + date (top) → **check-boxes** for attendees/parties → a dedicated **main-content space** (the event/decision) → a **notify/next-actions box** → a **left sidebar for live notes.**
- **Worked example:** Attorney David Sparks's court-hearing template — attendee check-boxes, hearing/ruling space, "notify parties" box, left sidebar for live notes.
- **Source:** Ch. 4 (Task Lists; David Sparks case).

### B13. The Five-Year-Plan Hub Page
- **When to use:** A visioning/goals page (founder vision board, brand north-star, annual planning).
- **Pattern:** **NAME + portrait + a future date** in a central hub → surround with work / life / family / relationships / travel attributes → write each as a **present-tense declarative statement** ("as if you are already living it") → post where you'll see it daily → review yearly.
- **Worked example:** Center: "Sacha — June 2031." Spokes: "I run a profitable studio," "I speak at 4 conferences a year," "We travel one new country annually."
- **Source:** Ch. 4 (Planning Your Future; Challenge 4.2).

### B14. The Video / Media Title + Character-Matrix Layout
- **When to use:** Capturing or summarizing a film/show/episode visually (and a model for "cast/lineup/comparison" social).
- **Pattern:** **TITLE block built BEFORE you start** (directors, writers, actors) → a **TOPIC LIST** (message, dialogue, conflict, takeaways) → a **CHARACTER MATRIX** (portraits + names + relationships, with space to add for serial shows) → consistent **SEASON/EPISODE icons** (S2 EP4) → page-number & index.
- **Worked example:** *Firefly/Serenity* — pre-built title card + "TOPICS" list + "THE CREW" matrix of crew portraits and roles.
- **Source:** Ch. 8 (6-step video process; Firefly worked example).

### B15. The Pre-Built Speaker Title Page (multi-speaker events)
- **When to use:** Reducing live pressure at a conference/webinar with multiple speakers — build the title pages in advance.
- **Pattern:** Before the event, per speaker: research **name spelling** → draw **portrait from a photo** → estimate **page count + margin** → leave the body blank to fill live.
- **Worked example:** A 6-speaker conference — six title cards drawn the night before (name, portrait, session title), so the live job is only capturing ideas.
- **Source:** Ch. 9 (Pre-Create Title Pages).

## C — Script Bank (sales / objection / follow-up / discovery / close)

> The book is non-commercial, so there are **no sales/close/objection scripts in the revenue sense** (marked ABSENT in H). What it DOES contain — captured below — are genuine **positioning, resistance-reframe, discovery, sharing, and self-talk scripts** that are directly reusable in brand/social copy and coaching.

### C1. The "Notes Plus" Elevator Pitch (positioning script)
- **When to use:** Explaining what the practice/product is to a skeptic in one sentence.
- **Pattern:** `I call [thing] "[familiar-anchor name]" because it's like [what you already do], with [added element] to amplify [benefit].`
- **Worked example:** "I call sketchnotes Notes Plus because they're like the notes you already take, with visual elements added to amplify ideas."
- **Source:** Ch. 1.

### C2. The "I Can't Draw" Resistance Reframe (objection handler)
- **When to use:** The #1 objection to anything visual. Acknowledge → invert → hand them the tool.
- **Pattern:** `You don't need [the assumed prerequisite]. [The real prerequisite] is the whole game — and you already have it: [the simple primitive set].`
- **Worked example:** "But I can't draw!" → "It's not about art, it's about good structure. **Everything you want to draw can be created with these 5 elements** — circle, square, triangle, line, dot." (Skill arc to repeat back: "But I can't draw!" → "Hey, I'm getting a bit better." → "Now I'm feeling more confident.")
- **Source:** Ch. 1 (5 elements) + Ch. 9 (Path of Skill Building).

### C3. The Social-Share Caption Script (tag the source)
- **When to use:** Posting a sketchnote/visual of someone's talk, product, or content to social — built to earn a re-share.
- **Pattern:** `Hey @[creator] here are my [topic] #sketchnotes from [event/place]. [One-line thanks/compliment]!`
- **Worked example:** "Hey @JoeSorge here are my food #sketchnotes from Water Buffalo. Thanks for lunch!"
- **Source:** Ch. 9 (Sharing Sketchnotes — Social Media).

### C4. The Email-to-Speaker Follow-Up Script
- **When to use:** Sending a captured visual directly to the person it features (network-building move).
- **Pattern:** `[First name], thought you might like to see my [sketchnotes/visual] of your [talk/work]. They're hyperlinked if you'd like to share. —[Your name]`
- **Worked example:** "Margot, thought you might like to see my sketchnotes of your talk. They are hyperlinked if you'd like to share. —Jess"
- **Source:** Ch. 9 (Sharing — Email; Jessica Esch). See full email in D1.

### C5. The Meal/Subject Discovery Question Script
- **When to use:** Eliciting story/detail before you document anything (food, travel, product, interview).
- **Pattern (ask in order):** `What's special or unusual about [the thing]?` → `Where is it sourced / where did it come from?` → `What was the [chef/creator]'s idea behind it?`
- **Worked example:** Sketchnoting a restaurant dish — "Any special or unusual ingredients? Is it locally sourced? What were the chef's ideas?"
- **Source:** Ch. 7 (7-step food process, step 2).

### C6. The Mistake-Recovery Self-Talk Script (4 moves)
- **When to use:** When something is drawn/written wrong and panic sets in (reframes errors as creative opportunity).
- **Pattern:** `Don't panic — it's a chance to test your creativity. Choose one: (1) CREATE A SHAPE over it + write the fix below; (2) FIX IT on the computer; (3) OWN IT — cross out + rewrite, or add honest commentary; (4) AVOID it next time — start the word, leave the unsure part, finish later.`
- **Worked example:** Misspelled a name live → draw a dark inky arrow over it and write the correct name below; or own it with a wink of commentary.
- **Source:** Ch. 9 (Mistakes — 4 Strategies).

### C7. The Listening-Loop Coaching Script (seek what resonates)
- **When to use:** Coaching someone (or self) through the discomfort of selecting big ideas instead of transcribing.
- **Pattern:** `Immerse → cache ideas, make connections, spot patterns → illustrate the patterns. At first, seeking ideas that resonate will feel awkward. That's normal. As you practice, important ideas will reveal themselves.`
- **Worked example:** Coaching a note-taker on a webinar: "Stop writing everything. Listen for what repeats and what resonates — capture that. It'll feel awkward at first; that's the work."
- **Source:** Ch. 1 (The Listening Loop) + Ch. 9 (Seek Repetition).

### C8. The Steal-and-Share Permission Script
- **When to use:** Building community/brand goodwill and lowering the barrier to reuse.
- **Pattern:** `If you see [assets] other people are using, feel free to steal them for your own [library]. Keep everything you make; review it to see how you've improved.`
- **Worked example:** "If you see icons other people are using, feel free to steal them for your icon library." · "Don't be afraid to show your sketchnotes. Keep everything you draw."
- **Source:** Ch. 2 (Build an Icon Library) + Ch. 7 (Mauro Toselli).

## E — Frameworks, Models & Templates (with all steps)

> This is the book's richest seam. Every named framework is captured with its complete internal steps + a worked example, so a specialist can execute the method without the book.

### E1. The 5 Basic Drawing Elements (the core library)
- **When to use:** Any time someone says "I can't draw" — everything decomposes to five primitives.
- **Pattern:** **CIRCLE • SQUARE • TRIANGLE • LINE • DOT.** "Everything you want to draw can be created with these 5 elements."
- **Worked example:** House, coffee cup, dollar, tree, bank, bird — each built from the five. Teaching move: "Can you identify the 5 basic elements in these simple drawings?"
- **Source:** Ch. 1.

### E2. The 7 Sketchnote Structure Patterns
- **When to use:** Choosing a page skeleton before drawing (full layout details in **B1**).
- **Pattern:** Linear • Radial • Vertical • Path • Modular • Skyscraper (columns) • Popcorn.
- **Worked example:** Sequential talk → Linear; one-topic-many-spokes → Radial; comparison → Skyscraper. See B1.
- **Source:** Ch. 1.

### E3. The Idea-Generation Process
- **When to use:** Generating and refining new ideas on the page.
- **Pattern (flow):** **OBSERVE your thinking → IDENTIFY ideas → CAPTURE (sketchnote) → REVIEW → REFINE → COMMUNICATE.** Mantra: "Speed and flow are important — Stay Loose • Work Quickly • Get Crazy!" Pair with a page format (B2/B3/B4) + **timeboxing** (E16).
- **Worked example:** Tree-house ideas — 4-panel grid, 25/5 timebox, number ideas, tag with icons (Part 1) → pick the best, redraw tighter in radial (Part 2).
- **Source:** Ch. 2.

### E4. The 7-Step Idea-Mapping Process
- **When to use:** Turning research into a single visual map.
- **Pattern:** (1) Gather research; (2) Choose a mapping surface (bigger = more ideas, smaller = focus); (3) Start with the topic (headline top); (4) Map ideas (concise text + drawings, **leave margin to append**); (5) Review & connect (arrows); (6) Spot & mark patterns (icons on repeats); (7) Create a NEW cleaner map if messy. Mantras: "Progress, not perfection." / "Messy sketchnotes are okay."
- **Worked example:** Dissertation themes mapped to beat writer's block; flash-card language maps (native word + drawing front, new language back).
- **Source:** Ch. 3.

### E5. The 5-State Task-Action Icon System (a complete bullet system)
- **When to use:** Any visual task list / planner / project board — a reusable status vocabulary.
- **Pattern:** `○ Open Task` (the base all others build on) → `• Started` (dot) → `✓ Completed` (check) → `✗ Canceled` (X) → `→ Deferred` (arrow = pushed later/delegated). Append **status icons** to any task: Deadline, Important, Website, Email, Address, Meetup.
- **Worked example:** "○ Draft launch email → (deferred) ⚑important · • Book venue · ✓ Confirm speakers · ✗ Print flyers."
- **Source:** Ch. 4 (Planning — task icons).

### E6. The 5-Step Documentation Process
- **When to use:** Documenting a process/recipe/diagnostic as a concise visual instead of "pages of boring text."
- **Pattern:** (1) **OBSERVE** the process (several times — more detail than once); (2) **CAPTURE** (build order of events with numbering + hierarchy); (3) **CREATE A SECOND SKETCHNOTE** (refine, double-check flow, tie with arrows); (4) **TEST** (optional run; video to catch missed steps); (5) **DESCRIBE** (add final observations). Variant — **"Sketchplanations":** when teaching *concepts* where order doesn't matter, use images over numbers; focus on key **principles** not steps.
- **Worked example:** V60 pour-over documented via its "5 elements": grind size, amount of coffee, amount of water, water temp, pouring pattern.
- **Source:** Ch. 5.

### E7. The 3-Step Travel-Sketchnote Process
- **When to use:** Capturing an experience/event as a keepsake artifact.
- **Pattern:** (1) **SEEK WHAT'S INTERESTING** (the landmark; what's *different*; people's stories; memorable/strange events); (2) **TAKE NOTES & SHOOT PHOTOS** ("the best camera is the one you have with you"); (3) **MAKE TIME TO SKETCHNOTE** (downtime/longer lunches/end of day). Technique — **"Leave Space for Later"** (E17).
- **Worked example:** Portland's four-spout fountains noted as "what's different"; landscape drawn in the moment with space left for earlier activities, filled at day's end.
- **Source:** Ch. 6.

### E8. The 7-Step Food-Sketchnote Process
- **When to use:** Documenting a meal/tasting (and a model for any sensory/experience capture).
- **Pattern:** (1) Prepare tools (sketchbook, pen, camera); (2) **ASK QUESTIONS** (special ingredients? locally sourced? chef's idea? — see C5); (3) Photograph the meal (so you can eat first); (4) Enjoy the meal (tastes/textures/smells); (5) Observe surroundings (environment + people); (6) Start sketchnoting (when done, from photos/notes/memory); (7) **ADD PERSONAL COMMENTS** (what you liked/didn't — "improves recall").
- **Worked example:** Sam "Pub" Smith starts with the restaurant **LOGO** + date + city; "Food isn't always the star."
- **Source:** Ch. 7.

### E9. The 6-Step Video / Media Process
- **When to use:** Capturing a film/show/talk/podcast as a visual summary.
- **Pattern:** (1) Prepare environment (queue the video); (2) **CREATE A TITLE** before it starts (directors/writers/actors); (3) **PREPARE** — decide what to capture (message, dialogue, characters, conflict/resolution, takeaways) → make a TOPIC LIST or CHARACTER MATRIX; (4) Begin sketchnoting (use the pause button, then wean off); (5) **REVIEW** ("I often see connections at the end I didn't see during"); (6) FINALIZE with research (times, settings, references).
- **Worked example:** *Firefly/Serenity* — pre-built title + TOPICS list + "THE CREW" matrix. Layout in B14.
- **Source:** Ch. 8.

### E10. The 3-Step Advanced-Lettering Recipe (+ 4 easy type styles)
- **When to use:** Hand-drawn headlines and hierarchy in any visual/social asset.
- **Pattern (every advanced style uses the SAME recipe):** (1) Draw simple **single-line** letter shapes as a framework; (2) **Thicken/double** the strokes + add the style detail; (3) **Fill in** the completed letter, adjusting shape. Styles + reference typefaces: Serif (Century Schoolbook) · Slab Serif (Rockwell) · Script (Susa Light) · Condensed (Myriad Condensed, for long headlines/short space) · Extended (Eurostile Extended, short headlines/long space) · 3D (Futura Shaded; consistent 45° angle, shade bottom OR right only). 4 EASY styles for quick hierarchy: **Single line • Double lines • Triple lines • Drop shadow.** Mantra: "The key to mastery: LOTS-O-PRACTICE!"
- **Worked example:** A condensed slab-serif headline for a tall, narrow carousel cover: single-line skeleton → thicken with straight serifs → fill.
- **Source:** Ch. 1 (lettering tutorials).

### E11. The 3 People-Drawing Methods
- **When to use:** Adding figures to any visual without "art."
- **Pattern:** **STAR METHOD** — head → arms+legs as a 5-point star → full body. **DAVE GRAY METHOD** — body → head+neck → legs → full body. **TWO-PEN PEOPLE** — loose figure in a light-color pen, then tighten with heavier black lines on top.
- **Worked example:** "Draw Lots-O-People" challenge — 50 figures in poses (sitting, running, presenting); compare drawing #1 to #50.
- **Source:** Ch. 1.

### E12. The 4 Face-Drawing Techniques
- **When to use:** Portraits for personas (B8), speaker title cards (B15), character matrices (B14).
- **Pattern:** (1) **Austin Kleon 3-line / 4-face matrix** — many expressions from 3 line types (nose/eyes/eyebrows/mouth) in a matrix; (2) **Shape first** — capture oval vs rectangular head, then details; (3) **Emphasize dominant features** (beard, nose, brows) and build from there; (4) **Build in sections** — light head shape → divide H&V (horizontal = eyes/ears; halve below for nose; mouth between nose and chin) → add features in a darker pen over the guides.
- **Worked example:** Persona portrait — start with an oval, mark the eye line, exaggerate the one dominant feature (glasses), ink over the light guides.
- **Source:** Ch. 1.

### E13. The 4 Mistake-Recovery Strategies
- **When to use:** Live capture errors (full say-this in C6).
- **Pattern:** (1) **Create a shape** over it + correction below; (2) **Fix on the computer**; (3) **Own it** (cross out + rewrite, or honest commentary); (4) **Avoid it** (start the word, leave the unsure part, finish later; use the phone dictionary).
- **Worked example:** Author missed a Zappos/Tony Hsieh list → owned it with honest on-page commentary.
- **Source:** Ch. 9.

### E14. The Advanced-Metaphor 3 Keys
- **When to use:** Making an abstract idea memorable with a visual metaphor (great for hooks/social).
- **Pattern:** (1) Use **crazy imagery** (memory-boosting mnemonic); (2) Combine **absurd, unrelated** ideas; (3) Be **humorous.**
- **Worked example:** "Like chocolate-covered lutefisk." Swipe metaphor bank: Cabin Fever, Lame Duck, Boiling Mad, Tunnel Vision, Snake Oil, Holy Grail, Heart of Gold, Catch-22.
- **Source:** Ch. 9 (Advanced Metaphors; Worksheet 1.1).

### E15. The Refinement Ladders
- **When to use:** Progressing any rough capture or icon to a finished, reusable asset.
- **Pattern:** Ideas: **ROUGH → INTERMEDIATE → FINAL** (loose first draft → a bit more detail → refine/finalize for your library). Icons: **SIMPLE → MEDIUM → COMPLEX.** Rule: "Create icons BEFORE you need them."
- **Worked example:** A "deadline" concept → rough clock scribble → cleaner clock+exclamation → final library icon reused everywhere.
- **Source:** Ch. 2 + Ch. 9 (Advanced Icons).

### E16. The Timeboxing Method
- **When to use:** Beating blank-page paralysis and staying on big ideas, not details.
- **Pattern:** `[Work span] work → [break] break → REPEAT.` Choose a time span, keep your mind moving, create quickly.
- **Worked example:** 25 min work → 5 min break → repeat (also 10–15 min sprints for fast idea passes).
- **Source:** Ch. 2.

### E17. The Live-Capture Trio (Caching · Reserve Spaces · Leave Space for Later)
- **When to use:** Capturing in real time without falling behind or leaving gaps.
- **Pattern:** **CACHING** — store a new idea in a mental cache while finishing the current one; drop an "idea kernel" (icon/word) with room to expand, jump on. **RESERVE SPACES** — for info that must be exact (quotes, formulas, names), leave the spot blank, jump ahead, insert later. **LEAVE SPACE FOR LATER** — draw the in-the-moment item, estimate space needed, leave room to backfill earlier content.
- **Worked example:** Reserve a blank box for an exact quote ("E=mc²") and fill it after the session.
- **Source:** Ch. 9 (Live-Capture Techniques).

### E18. The Sketchnoted Agile/Scrum Kit
- **When to use:** Visualizing any iterative project (layouts in B8/B9/B10).
- **Pattern:** SCRUM cycle = 2-week sprints → Sprint Planning → daily check-ins → Retrospective + Review. Assets: **Persona card** (B8) + **Sprint-plan board** (B9, PBIs least→most important; movable task stickies) + **Retrospective board** (B10, "what works" | "what can be improved").
- **Worked example:** Laurens Bonnema's sprint plan — PBIs ordered by priority, task stickies with timeboxed-hours icons.
- **Source:** Ch. 4 (Agile/Scrum Sketchnoting).

### E19. The Five-Year Sketchnote Plan
- **When to use:** Vision/goal setting (layout in B13).
- **Pattern:** Name + portrait + future date in a hub → surround with life attributes written as **present-tense declaratives** ("as if already living them") → post visibly → review yearly.
- **Worked example:** See B13 (Sacha/Ivan five-year plans).
- **Source:** Ch. 4.

### E20. The Chapter-Spine Course-Module Template (teaching framework)
- **When to use:** Building any teaching module, lesson, or content series — the book's own repeatable instructional spine.
- **Pattern (module order):** Section banner → title + one-line promise → **"What is X?"** definition → **BENEFITS** (icon + ALL-CAPS lead, A9) → **"HOW TO CREATE…"** numbered STEPS → **PRACTICAL USES** (with real-person mini case studies) → **BUILD-IT** habit tip → **CHALLENGE + QUESTIONS** → **FEATURED [practitioner]** profile + pull-quote → **WORKSHEET** (icon grid) → **RECAP** (arrow bullets ➔) → **"NEXT: ___"** teaser.
- **Worked example:** Every applied chapter (Idea Generation, Planning, Documentation, Travel, Food, Media) follows this exact spine — reuse it as a generic course-module / email-course outline.
- **Source:** Book architecture (all applied chapters).

### E21. The Tasting Scorecard Rubric
- **When to use:** Structured comparison/review content (product reviews, tasting posts, scorecards).
- **Pattern:** Score each item 1–10 across fixed dimensions: **Fragrance · Aroma · Sweetness · Acidity · Body · Overall.**
- **Worked example:** A coffee tasting card — three coffees scored across all six dimensions for a side-by-side review carousel.
- **Source:** Ch. 7 (Challenge 7.2, tasting rubric).

## F — Brand Voice & Brand-Building Language Patterns

> Voice = warm, playful, permission-giving, anti-perfectionist, action-first, community-minded, with hand-lettered ALL-CAPS emphasis and arrow bullets as the visual signature. Use these to write ON-brand, encouraging, richly human copy.

1. **Permission-giving mantra (anti-perfectionism).** Ex: "PROGRESS, NOT PERFECTION." / "MESSY SKETCHNOTES ARE OKAY."
2. **Meet-the-resistance-head-on reframe.** Ex: "But I can't draw!" → "It's about good structure, not art."
3. **Familiar-anchor naming ("X Plus").** Ex: "Notes Plus — the notes you already take, with visual elements added to amplify ideas."
4. **Capability-affirmation.** Ex: "Can you create sketchnotes? YES!"
5. **Rule-of-three imperative.** Ex: "Stay Loose • Work Quickly • Get Crazy!"
6. **ALL-CAPS hand-lettered positioning.** Ex: "SKETCHNOTES EMPHASIZE CAPTURING BIG IDEAS IN THE MOMENT."
7. **Arrow-bullet recap (➔).** Ex: a chapter RECAP as 6–8 "➔" lines restating the principles.
8. **"Experiments, not assignments" de-risk.** Ex: "These should be seen as experiments and not assignments. Have fun!"
9. **Steal-and-share ethos.** Ex: "If you see icons other people are using, feel free to steal them."
10. **Inclusive community voice.** Ex: "We're welcoming and encouraging to everyone." / "This book is for you."
11. **Playful sign-offs.** Ex: "Rock & Roll!" / "Let's sketchnote!" / "We did it!"
12. **Action-closing CTA.** Ex: "ENOUGH TALK — IT'S TIME FOR ACTION. GRAB A NOTEBOOK AND A PEN."
13. **Commitment-device framing (treat-yourself).** Ex: "Buy a good-quality sketchbook and pen — a treasured luxury gift to yourself."
14. **Memory/keepsake framing.** Ex: "When memories fade, travel sketchnotes act as time capsules."
15. **Collaboration = buy-in framing.** Ex: "Sketchnotes created collaboratively tend to have higher buy-in because everyone is part of the solution."
16. **Reassuring "that's normal" coaching.** Ex: "At first, seeking ideas that resonate will feel awkward. That's normal."
17. **Effort-over-talent law.** Ex: "The best way to get better is to sketchnote as much as possible. Repetition hones your skills."
18. **Concrete-tool specificity (credibility).** Ex: naming exact gear — "Ugmonk green leather book + Retro 51 Tornado Stealth rollerball."
19. **"Worth more than…" value reframe.** Ex: "A memento worth more than a thousand pictures."
20. **Whimsy / Easter-egg delight.** Ex: fill leftover nooks with "visual Easter eggs" — squeaky mice, teeter-totters, royal crowns.
