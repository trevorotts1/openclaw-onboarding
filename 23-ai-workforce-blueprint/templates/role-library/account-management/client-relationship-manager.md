## 9. Standard Operating Procedures (Numbered)

### SOP 9.1 — Discovery-Driven Touchpoint Protocol

**When to run:** Every scheduled client call (proactive touchpoint, QBR pre-call, expansion call, check-in). This protocol governs the structure of the conversation, not just the scheduling.
**Frequency:** Per call, every call. The protocol takes 5 minutes to prepare and transforms every call from a status update into a discovery session.
**Inputs:** The client's account record in {{CRM_PLATFORM_NAME}} (last touchpoint notes, health score, current delivery status, open action items, expansion pipeline status), the client's stated business goals from the last QBR or account plan, any recent news about the client's company (LinkedIn, news monitoring).

**Steps:**
1. **Pre-call preparation (5 minutes):** Review the account record and identify: (a) one thing {{COMPANY_NAME}} has delivered since the last call that the client may not have fully connected to their business impact — this becomes the value anchor for the call, (b) one recent development in the client's business or industry (from news monitoring or prior conversation notes) that you can reference to demonstrate attention, (c) one open question you have about the client's evolving goals or priorities that you genuinely do not know the answer to.
2. **Call opening (first 2 minutes):** Reference the recent client business development if applicable: "I saw [company news/achievement/development] — how is that affecting your priorities right now?" This resets the call as a business conversation, not an account management check-in.
3. **Value acknowledgment (3 minutes):** Introduce the value anchor: "I wanted to make sure we talked about [specific delivery result] — from our side, this was [what it means operationally], but I wanted to hear from you what you're seeing as the impact." Listen to the client's framing of the result — this is critical intelligence for expansion conversations and QBR preparation.
4. **Discovery questions (10-15 minutes):** Ask open-ended questions about the client's current state. Examples calibrated to account status: "What are the two or three things you're most focused on in the next 90 days?" / "Where are you feeling the most friction in [relevant business area]?" / "What would have to be true for Q4 to feel like a great quarter for you?" Do NOT use this time to pitch expansion. The purpose is to understand.
5. **Transition to business (remaining time):** Address any open action items, confirm delivery milestones, and answer any questions the client raises. If an expansion trigger naturally surfaces from the discovery phase, you may reference it briefly: "That's interesting — we've actually been working with a similar challenge for [reference company type, not name]. Would it be useful to see how we approached it?" Do not pressure the expansion in this call — plant the seed.
6. **Call close (2 minutes):** Confirm all commitments made during the call with specific dates. End with: "The most important thing I want to make sure we're doing right for you — is there anything that's been on your mind about our work together that you haven't said yet?" This final question is the most powerful retention question in the script.
7. **Post-call documentation (within 1 hour):** Log in {{CRM_PLATFORM_NAME}}: client's exact language about their current priorities (verbatim where possible), any satisfaction signals (positive or negative), any expansion trigger mentioned, any commitment made (with date and owner), the client's overall tone (engaged/distracted/concerned/enthusiastic), and an updated health dimension note for any dimension affected by the conversation.

**Outputs:** Call summary in {{CRM_PLATFORM_NAME}}, updated health score notes, expansion pipeline update (if trigger identified), open action items with owners and dates.
**Hand to:** Director (if any concern, health score change, or expansion trigger above a threshold ACV is identified); Retention Specialist (if call reveals Yellow warning signals); Delivery team (if commitments made require delivery action).
**Failure mode:** If the client is rushed or unwilling to engage in the discovery portion of the call, do not force it. Respect the client's time and context. Move to addressing open items and commitments. Log the limited engagement as a passive disengagement signal (one data point) — do not classify it as a health score change from a single rushed call. Follow up within 3 days with a brief email that references the one discovery insight you did get from the call to demonstrate that you were listening.

### SOP 9.2 — Quarterly Business Review (QBR) Execution Protocol

**When to run:** Once per quarter for all Strategic and Enterprise tier accounts, within the first 3 weeks of each quarter.
**Frequency:** Quarterly per eligible account. QBR dates are scheduled at the prior quarter's QBR (confirming the next one before leaving the current one).
**Inputs:** Delivery results for the quarter (from project management system and delivery team), the client's stated goals from the prior QBR and account plan, expansion opportunities and their current status, the client stakeholder map (who should be in the room), any satisfaction data (CSAT, informal feedback from touchpoints), any business intelligence on the client company's recent news.

**Steps:**
1. **3 weeks before QBR:** Schedule the QBR and confirm attendees. Aim for the client's decision-maker (not just the day-to-day contact) to attend — the QBR is most valuable when someone who can approve expansions is in the room. Prepare the QBR pre-read: a 1-2 page document summarizing {{COMPANY_NAME}}'s delivered results since the last QBR and the proposed agenda. Send the pre-read at least 5 business days before the QBR.
2. **QBR deck structure (prepare in the week before the QBR):** Section 1 — Goals Reviewed (what the client said they wanted to achieve at the last QBR or when they contracted — this demonstrates institutional memory and respect for their stated agenda). Section 2 — Results Delivered (specific, measurable outcomes tied to those goals — not a list of activities, but a link between activity and business impact). Section 3 — Where We Fell Short (any gap between what was committed and what was delivered — proactively naming this builds more trust than the client discovering it). Section 4 — Business Context Update (what has changed in the client's business or industry since the last QBR, and what that means for the partnership). Section 5 — Q[N+1] Recommended Focus (3 specific recommendations for what the partnership should prioritize next quarter, tied to the client's evolving goals). Section 6 — Open Questions (3-5 questions that the client must answer to finalize the Q[N+1] plan).
3. **QBR call/meeting facilitation:** Spend the first 5 minutes confirming that the agenda reflects the client's current priorities — if something has shifted, update the agenda before the content presentation. Present Section 1-3 as a shared review of the past, not a defense of {{COMPANY_NAME}}'s work. Section 4-6 should drive the majority of the conversation. The QBR should be a 70/30 split: 70% conversation, 30% presentation.
4. **QBR close:** Before ending the QBR, confirm: (a) the 1-3 specific actions {{COMPANY_NAME}} will take in Q[N+1] based on the conversation, (b) the date and format of the next QBR, (c) any expansion opportunity that the client wants to explore further and the next step. Do not end without scheduling the next QBR.
5. **QBR follow-up (within 24 hours):** Send a written QBR summary to all attendees: what was decided, what {{COMPANY_NAME}} committed to for Q[N+1], what next steps were identified, and the confirmed date of the next QBR. This summary is the most-referenceable document in the account relationship.
6. **Update the account plan in {{CRM_PLATFORM_NAME}}** within 48 hours of the QBR: new goals, updated expansion opportunities, updated stakeholder map, updated health notes, and the confirmed Q[N+1] action plan.

**Outputs:** QBR deck, QBR follow-up summary, updated account plan in {{CRM_PLATFORM_NAME}}, next QBR scheduled.
**Hand to:** Director (for review of QBR deck before delivery and for awareness of strategic conversations and expansion opportunities surfaced); Delivery team (for Q[N+1] committed deliverables); Finance (if expansion was agreed to and contract modification is needed).
**Failure mode:** If the client cancels the QBR within 48 hours of the scheduled time, do NOT reschedule without a brief diagnostic: is this a scheduling conflict (reschedulable) or a sign of disengagement (warrants a health score review and Director notification)? Two cancelled QBRs in a row without proactive rescheduling from the client is a Yellow-status trigger. The QBR is a relationship health indicator, not just a calendar event.

### SOP 9.3 — Referral Request Protocol

**When to run:** When an account reaches advocate status: (a) client has provided unsolicited positive feedback in the last 30 days, (b) client has completed 2+ successful QBR cycles without a single Yellow episode, (c) client has proactively introduced {{COMPANY_NAME}} to another person in their network (a soft referral), or (d) client has agreed to provide a testimonial or case study.
**Frequency:** Once per identified advocate moment; not more than twice per year per account to avoid oversaturation.
**Inputs:** The advocacy signal (what triggered the "advocate status" identification), the client's network context (do they serve customers or know businesses that match {{COMPANY_NAME}}'s ideal client profile?), any prior referral requests to this client and their outcome, the specific success story from the client's account that would make the most compelling referral context.

**Steps:**
1. **Personalize the referral ask** around the client's specific success: "You mentioned [result from last QBR] — has that come up in conversations with other [business owners / executives / peers in your network]?" Do not open with "Do you know anyone who might benefit from our services?" This is generic and puts the burden on the client to do the matching.
2. **Make the referral easy:** provide the client with a 2-sentence description of {{COMPANY_NAME}}'s ideal client profile so they can self-identify the match without doing analysis. Example: "The kinds of businesses we work best with are [ICP description]. If that sounds like anyone you've worked with or know, a simple intro email from you is all it takes."
3. **Offer value in exchange:** at minimum, a personal thank-you note from the Director or {{OWNER_NAME}}. If the referral converts, a substantive acknowledgment (depending on {{COMPANY_NAME}}'s referral policy — could be a service credit, a gift, or a public acknowledgment).
4. **If the client agrees:** provide a referral email template they can customize, or offer to draft it for them and have them approve it before sending. Reduce every friction point.
5. **Log the referral request in {{CRM_PLATFORM_NAME}}:** date, method of ask, client response (agreed/declined/considering), and outcome (intro received/converted/did not convert).
6. **Follow up once** if the client said they would think about it — one follow-up within 2 weeks, then respect the non-response as a "not now."

**Outputs:** Referral request logged, referral email sent (if client agreed), Referral Scorecard updated, Director notified of any referral introduction received.
**Hand to:** Director and Sales (when a referral introduction is received — this is a warm lead for the sales team); {{OWNER_NAME}} (for personal thank-you to the referring client).
**Failure mode:** If the client declines the referral request, do not reference it again for at least 6 months and do not let the decline affect the relationship's warmth. A client who declines to refer is still a valuable client. The relationship is primary; the referral is a secondary benefit of a great relationship.

### SOP 9.4 — Multi-Threading (Stakeholder Expansion Within Accounts)

**When to run:** During account planning for any Strategic account that currently has a single-threaded relationship (only one active contact at the client organization); triggered annually for all Strategic accounts as part of the quarterly account plan refresh.
**Frequency:** Annual assessment for Strategic accounts; execution whenever a single-threaded risk is identified.
**Inputs:** Current stakeholder map for the account (in {{CRM_PLATFORM_NAME}}), organizational chart or LinkedIn research on the client organization's relevant leadership, the client's primary contact relationship quality (their openness to facilitating introductions).

**Steps:**
1. **Map the current relationship:** who at the client organization has had direct contact with anyone at {{COMPANY_NAME}} in the last 6 months? Note their role, seniority, and relationship quality (strong/developing/minimal).
2. **Identify the target stakeholders:** which 2-4 additional people inside the client organization would benefit from knowing {{COMPANY_NAME}} directly? Prioritize: (a) the decision-maker above the primary contact (for renewal authority and expansion approval), (b) someone in a function adjacent to the services {{COMPANY_NAME}} provides (for cross-sell), (c) someone who would succeed the primary contact if they were to leave (churn risk mitigation).
3. **Create a natural introduction opportunity:** the best multi-threading happens when the new relationship provides genuine value to the new stakeholder, not when the client contact is pressured to facilitate a "vendor introduction." Options: (a) invite the target stakeholder to a relevant QBR section where their expertise is relevant, (b) produce an insight or piece of research relevant to the target stakeholder's function and deliver it with a brief introduction, (c) ask the primary contact: "Is there a natural time when I could meet [name/role]? I think there might be a way we could support [their specific function] as well."
4. **First contact with the new stakeholder:** brief, value-focused. The goal is not to sell — the goal is for them to have a positive first experience with {{COMPANY_NAME}} so that a future conversation (about renewal, expansion, or continuity if the primary contact departs) is not a cold conversation.
5. **Document the new relationship** in {{CRM_PLATFORM_NAME}}: name, role, contact information, first contact date, and relationship quality (initial assessment).
6. **Maintain the new relationship** at a lower frequency than the primary contact but consistently: a relevant article once a quarter, a brief mention in the QBR follow-up summary ("I'm copying [Name/Role] on this summary as they joined us for the strategy section").

**Outputs:** Updated stakeholder map in {{CRM_PLATFORM_NAME}}, documented first-contact with new stakeholder, multi-threading action plan for the account.
**Hand to:** Director (for awareness of new stakeholder relationships developed); no other hand-off required.
**Failure mode:** If the primary client contact is visibly uncomfortable with or obstructive to multi-threading efforts (they want to be the sole conduit to {{COMPANY_NAME}}), do not push. Log the single-thread risk in the account health notes and flag it to the Director. A protective primary contact who controls all access is itself a churn risk indicator — if they leave, the account relationship resets to zero.

## 10. Quality Gates

Before any client-facing output ships (QBR deck, account plan, expansion proposal, touchpoint email, referral request), it must pass these gates:

### Gate 1 — Self-check

- [ ] Every QBR deck leads with the client's stated goals and measured delivery results — not with {{COMPANY_NAME}}'s capabilities or future services.
- [ ] Every touchpoint call has a documented agenda prepared and sent at least 24 hours before the call.
- [ ] Every expansion conversation is preceded by a demonstrated understanding of the client's business goal that the expansion serves — no product pitch without a problem linkage.
- [ ] Every call summary is in {{CRM_PLATFORM_NAME}} within 1 hour of call end.
- [ ] Every commitment made to a client is logged in {{CRM_PLATFORM_NAME}} with a specific owner and deadline.
- [ ] The referral ask is personalized — no generic "do you know anyone?"

### Gate 2 — Director Review

The Director reviews: (a) QBR decks before delivery — does the deck demonstrate value and business context literacy, or is it a service delivery status list?, (b) expansion proposals before delivery — is the proposal grounded in the client's stated goals or in {{COMPANY_NAME}}'s product catalog?, (c) touchpoint cadence compliance — is every account in the portfolio receiving the minimum frequency for its tier?

### Gate 3 — Devil's Advocate

For expansion proposals above a defined ACV threshold: (a) is the client's stated readiness for the expansion genuine, or is the account manager projecting readiness based on a positive QBR?, (b) does this expansion proposal reflect what the client actually needs, or what we most want to sell?, (c) is the QBR summary an accurate representation of the conversation, or has it been written to emphasize positive signals while minimizing concerns?

### Gate 4 — Owner Approval

Required for: (a) any case study or testimonial that represents the client's business results publicly (client approval required first, then {{OWNER_NAME}} approval for the content), (b) any expansion proposal where {{COMPANY_NAME}} has not previously delivered the proposed service, (c) any executive relationship-building activity (client event, joint speaking engagement, board-level introduction) that involves {{OWNER_NAME}}'s participation.

- **Delivery team** — you give them: client context for all active deliverables (client's tone, current expectations, any sensitivity to delays), QBR outcome action items that require delivery team action, in format: direct message + CRM notes, frequency: weekly.

## 14. Bad Output Examples (Anti-Patterns)

### Anti-Pattern A — The Status Update QBR

**What went wrong:** A QBR deck was delivered that consisted of a list of deliverables completed in the quarter with checkboxes, followed by a product overview slide for the next tier of service, and ended with "Any questions?"

**Why this fails:**
- A checklist of completed tasks does not demonstrate value — it demonstrates activity. Value is what changed in the client's business because of those activities.
- The product overview slide positions {{COMPANY_NAME}} as a vendor pitching their catalog, not a strategic partner understanding the client's goals.
- No question was asked about the client's Q4 priorities, so the conversation produced no actionable intelligence.
- The client left the call with the same information they already had — no reason to increase their engagement with {{COMPANY_NAME}}.

### Anti-Pattern B — Touchpoint Without Purpose

**What went wrong:** A client received a touchpoint email every 2 weeks that said "Just checking in to see if there's anything you need from us this month." After three months, the client stopped responding. The account manager logged each email as a "touchpoint completed."

**Why this fails:**
- Logging a sent email as a touchpoint is process compliance without relationship building.
- "Let me know if you need anything" puts the responsibility on the client to identify their own needs — which a truly value-adding partner should be doing proactively.
- After three of these emails with no meaningful content, the client has learned that these check-ins carry no value, and they stop responding — which is the beginning of a disengagement pattern.

## 15. Common Mistakes (Pre-Empted)

| # | Mistake | Root Cause | Prevention |
|---|---------|------------|------------|
| 1 | Letting the QBR slide into a status update. | Default to presenting what you know (what you delivered) rather than asking about what the client needs. | SOP 9.2 deck structure requires Section 4-6 (business context + recommendations + open questions) to be prepared before the QBR, so there is always substance beyond the delivery review. |
| 2 | Single-threading a Strategic account. | The primary contact is easy to work with, so the relationship stays anchored there. | Annual multi-threading assessment (SOP 9.4) is required for every Strategic account. Single-thread risk is logged as a health note. |
| 3 | Skipping the QBR confirmation at the end of each QBR. | Call is running long; the next QBR feels far away. | SOP 9.2 step 4 includes "confirm next QBR date" as a mandatory final action. No QBR is closed without confirming the next one. |
| 4 | Treating a positive QBR as evidence that no further attention is needed. | A happy client feels low-priority relative to at-risk clients. | The expansion pipeline scan runs every Monday for all Green accounts — a happy client is a selling opportunity, not a file-and-forget. |
| 5 | Advancing an expansion conversation before value has been demonstrated. | Quota pressure; the expansion opportunity is obvious to the account manager but has not been established in the client's mind. | Expansion conversations follow value demonstration — always. Every expansion proposal references a specific result from the client's own account, not a feature description. |

## 17. Edge Cases

### Edge Case 17.1 — Client Pushes Back on QBR Frequency

**Trigger:** A Strategic or Enterprise account client says the quarterly QBR cadence is too frequent and they want to move to annual or semi-annual.

**Action:** Treat this as a Yellow-status signal — a client who wants less contact is often a disengaging client. Do not immediately agree to a reduced cadence. Instead: "I absolutely understand — I want to make sure these conversations are worth your time. Can I ask what would make them more valuable? I'd rather adjust the format than the frequency, because the quarterly touchpoint has been the way we've caught potential issues before they became problems." If the client insists, brief the Director and agree to a compromise: reduce QBR frequency to semi-annual but increase the quality and Executive-level participation for each remaining QBR. Document the reduced cadence as a health risk note.

### Edge Case 17.2 — Client Requests Internal {{COMPANY_NAME}} Resource Access

**Trigger:** A client requests direct access to {{COMPANY_NAME}}'s delivery systems, project management tools, or internal team members without going through the account management relationship.

**Action:** Do NOT grant direct access without Director approval. Respond: "That's a great idea — let me check the most efficient way to set that up so it works within how we manage access and security across our client base. I'll get back to you within 24 hours." Internally: assess with the Director and Operations whether direct access is appropriate, what access controls are needed, and whether this represents a scope change. Some direct access (e.g., read access to a shared project board) may be appropriate and welcomed; uncontrolled direct access can create scope confusion and relationship boundary issues.
