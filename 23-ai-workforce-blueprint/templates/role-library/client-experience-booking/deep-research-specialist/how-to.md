## 9. Standard Operating Procedures (Numbered)

### SOP 9.1 -- Competitive Booking Experience Audit

**When to run:** Monthly comprehensive refresh + triggered immediately by any major competitor event (new booking platform launch, major rebrand, acquisition, or a competitor's client experience receiving public recognition or criticism in the industry).

**Frequency:** Monthly (comprehensive) + on-demand for triggered events.

**Inputs:** Competitor list maintained with the Director (Tier 1 = 5-7 direct competitors in {{COMPANY_INDUSTRY}} offering the same or adjacent service type; Tier 2 = 3-5 indirect competitors or aspirational experience benchmarks from adjacent service industries); access to competitor websites, booking pages, public social proof content, and review platforms.

**Steps:**

1. **DEFINE the competitor set.** Confirm the current Tier 1 and Tier 2 list with the Director before beginning. New entrants, recently acquired companies, and companies receiving significant press or social media attention in {{COMPANY_INDUSTRY}} since the last audit are candidates for addition. Document the final list with each competitor's primary service offering, geography, and estimated price point.

2. **MEASURE -- audit each competitor's end-to-end booking experience across six dimensions:**

   a. **Booking flow UX:** How does a prospect initiate booking? (direct calendar link, contact form, chatbot, phone, text, social DM?) How many steps from "I want to book" to "confirmed appointment"? What friction points exist? What trust signals are present (testimonials, credentials, availability indicators)? Document each step with notes on the prospect experience.

   b. **Confirmation sequence:** What channel or channel mix is used for confirmation (email, SMS, phone)? At what intervals (immediately, 24h, 2h)? What is the tone and content of each touchpoint (purely logistical vs. warm and expectation-setting)? Is there a pre-session preparation guide or onboarding document sent before the first appointment?

   c. **Reminder and no-show prevention:** How many reminders are sent and on which channels? At what time intervals before the appointment? Is there a "confirm your spot" call-to-action that gates the final reminder (creating micro-commitment)? Is there a documented or inferred cancellation policy visible in the sequence?

   d. **First-session experience:** What does the client receive on the day of their first session? What materials or preparation instructions are sent in advance? What is the estimated client-to-specialist ratio or session personalization level (where observable from public information)?

   e. **Post-session follow-up:** Is there evidence of a structured follow-up sequence after the first session? What channels are used and at what timing? Is there a clear next-step offer (rebook, upsell, referral request) embedded in the post-session sequence?

   f. **Retention and loyalty touchpoints:** Are there documented long-term retention mechanisms (loyalty programs, anniversary check-ins, milestone communications, exclusive content)? Are these mentioned on the website, in reviews, or in social content?

3. **ANALYZE patterns across competitors:**
   - What confirmation approaches are used by MOST Tier 1 competitors? (signals category standard)
   - Where do top-performing competitors (those with strong public reviews and visible retention) diverge from the standard?
   - What does the BEST experience in this competitor set look like holistically?
   - Where does {{COMPANY_NAME}}'s current booking experience lag behind the competitive set? Where does it lead?
   - What experience innovations or approaches are used by 1-2 competitors but not yet the category standard? (early adoption opportunity)

4. **IMPROVE -- produce the Competitive Booking Experience Audit report:**
   - Executive summary: top 3-5 findings and their strategic implications for {{COMPANY_NAME}}.
   - Competitor profiles: one-page structured summary per competitor (booking flow UX, confirmation approach, post-session strategy, observable retention touchpoints, standout positive or negative elements).
   - Competitive gap analysis: a side-by-side comparison of {{COMPANY_NAME}} vs. competitors across the six dimensions; red/yellow/green rating for each cell.
   - Recommendations: specific, prioritized actions for the Director -- sequence changes, new touchpoints, technology improvements, or experience gaps to close (each linked to the competitive finding that supports it).

5. **CONTROL -- archive and update the tracker.** File the full report in `{{DEPT_DIR}}/research/competitive/YYYY-MM-competitive-booking-audit.md`. Update the competitive intelligence tracker with each competitor's current status. Flag any competitor whose strategy has shifted significantly since last month as a "watch closely" item for the Director.

**Outputs:** Monthly competitive booking experience audit report (8-15 pages) with competitor profiles, gap analysis, and prioritized recommendations.

**Hand to:** Director of Client Experience & Booking (full report + recommendation summary); Booking Coordinator and Client Onboarding Specialist (relevant competitive intelligence for their specific touchpoints); Post-Session Follow-Up Specialist (competitive post-session and retention findings).

**Failure mode:** If a key competitor runs their booking process predominantly through private channels (phone-only, referral-only, gated community) and very little is publicly observable, acknowledge the data gap explicitly. Use indirect signals: review platform content (do clients describe the booking experience?), social media content from the competitor (do they show their onboarding process?), and professional network intelligence (do practitioners in {{COMPANY_INDUSTRY}} discuss this competitor's approach?). NEVER infer a competitor's internal process with high confidence from minimal public signals -- label all inference clearly as "Inferred from [source type]."

### SOP 9.2 -- Booking and Scheduling Technology Evaluation

**When to run:** When the Director is considering adopting a new scheduling or booking technology, replacing a current tool, or when the quarterly technology landscape review is due.

**Frequency:** On-demand for adoption/replacement decisions + quarterly for landscape scanning.

**Inputs:** Technology category and the specific problem it is intended to solve; current technology stack ({{CRM_PLATFORM_NAME}}, existing scheduling tools, communication channels); evaluation criteria weighted by the Director; evaluation budget (if paid trials are required); integration requirements.

**Steps:**

1. **DEFINE the problem and evaluation criteria.** With the Director, confirm: what specific booking or client experience problem does this technology need to solve? What does "success" look like at 90 days post-adoption? Which criteria are must-haves vs. nice-to-haves? Standard criteria include: integration depth with {{CRM_PLATFORM_NAME}} (data sync, automation trigger capability, field mapping), scheduling rule flexibility (service types, provider availability, buffer management, timezone handling), client-facing UX quality (mobile responsiveness, booking flow length, brand customization), confirmation and reminder automation native capability, pricing model fit (per-seat, per-booking, flat rate -- match to {{COMPANY_NAME}}'s volume), vendor support quality and uptime track record, and data portability (can we extract all appointment and client data if we leave?).

2. **MEASURE -- research the technology landscape for the category:**
   a. Identify 3-6 candidate tools via: G2 and Capterra category rankings (filter to tools with >50 verified reviews in the relevant category), industry publications and comparison articles (HubSpot Blog, Software Advice, GetApp), and tools actively used or discussed by competitors in {{COMPANY_INDUSTRY}}.
   b. For each candidate, gather: product documentation (official features list, integration directory, pricing page), user reviews (extract the 10 most recent verified reviews from G2 or Capterra; note recurring praise and complaint themes), case studies from service businesses in {{COMPANY_INDUSTRY}} or adjacent verticals, and -- where available -- API documentation via Context7 MCP (`resolve-library-id` then `query-docs`) to confirm {{CRM_PLATFORM_NAME}} integration depth at the endpoint level.
   c. If a free trial is available and the Director approves, conduct a hands-on evaluation: attempt to configure a representative booking workflow, test the client-facing experience on mobile and desktop, and attempt to trigger a CRM sync event.

3. **ANALYZE -- score each tool against weighted evaluation criteria:**
   - Build a comparison matrix: tools as rows, criteria as columns, weighted score per cell, total weighted score per tool.
   - Identify the top 2 candidates by weighted score.
   - Conduct a head-to-head deep-dive on the top 2: integration depth (can it write appointment data to {{CRM_PLATFORM_NAME}} in real time, or only on batch sync?), automation trigger capability (can a booking event trigger a CRM workflow?), client-facing UX quality at each booking step, and total cost of ownership for {{COMPANY_NAME}}'s expected booking volume.

4. **IMPROVE -- develop a recommendation:**
   - **Recommended tool and rationale:** the winner on weighted criteria; the 3 strongest reasons; the 1-2 most important tradeoffs accepted by choosing it.
   - **Implementation roadmap:** phases (configure, pilot, migrate, go-live), responsible roles per phase, timeline, and success metrics for the 90-day post-adoption review.
   - **Total cost of ownership:** first-year and ongoing costs including licensing, implementation time (hours x role rate), migration effort, and training time.
   - **Risk assessment:** top 3 adoption risks (e.g., CRM sync may require custom webhook work; client-facing mobile UX needs usability testing before go-live) with mitigation steps.
   - **Go/No-Go criteria:** if the recommended tool fails a specific criterion in a 30-day pilot, this triggers a re-evaluation rather than full rollout.

5. **CONTROL -- archive and follow up.** File the evaluation report in `{{DEPT_DIR}}/research/technology/YYYY-MM-[tool-name]-evaluation.md`. If the Director approves adoption, schedule a 90-day post-adoption review and add it to the research calendar. If a tool is rejected, document the reason in the tracker so the same evaluation is not duplicated if the tool is considered again.

**Outputs:** Technology evaluation report (8-12 pages) with comparison matrix, recommendation, implementation roadmap, and risk assessment.

**Hand to:** Director of Client Experience & Booking (decision); Booking Coordinator (implementation responsibilities); CRM department (integration technical requirements); Finance (if procurement requires approval).

**Failure mode:** The single biggest risk in technology evaluation is underestimating integration complexity. If a tool's API documentation is vague, their support team is slow to respond to technical questions, or their integration with {{CRM_PLATFORM_NAME}} is listed as "coming soon" or "via Zapier only" (rather than native), this is a significant red flag that must be prominently stated in the recommendation -- not minimized. A scheduling tool that does not write data bidirectionally to {{CRM_PLATFORM_NAME}} in real time creates a data synchronization problem that costs far more in operational overhead than any booking feature it provides. State this risk plainly.

### SOP 9.3 -- Booking Experience Benchmarking Study

**When to run:** Annually as a baseline + semi-annual update + triggered by any significant performance deviation (show-up rate drops >3 percentage points below prior 90-day average, or Director flags a performance concern).

**Frequency:** Annual (baseline) + semi-annual refresh + on-demand trigger.

**Inputs:** Industry benchmark data sources (scheduling platform published studies, HubSpot Service Benchmarks, industry association research for {{COMPANY_INDUSTRY}}, consumer behavior research on appointment-keeping); company's own booking performance data from {{CRM_PLATFORM_NAME}} for the trailing 90 days (confirmation rate, show-up rate, first-session rebook rate, LTV by booking cohort, no-show recovery rate); access to the department research repository for historical data.

**Steps:**

1. **DEFINE benchmarking dimensions.** The Client Experience & Booking department tracks performance across five stages of the booking lifecycle:
   - **Confirmation rate:** percent of booked appointments that result in an explicit client confirmation before the appointment date.
   - **Show-up rate:** percent of confirmed appointments where the client actually attends the session.
   - **No-show recovery rate:** percent of no-show events that result in a rebooked appointment within 30 days.
   - **First-session rebook rate:** percent of clients who complete their first session and book a second session within 14 days.
   - **Client LTV at 6 months:** average revenue per client through the first 6 months of the relationship, segmented by booking-source and onboarding-cohort.

2. **MEASURE -- collect industry benchmark data for each dimension:**
   a. Scheduling platform benchmark reports: Calendly, Acuity, Jane App, and similar platforms periodically publish aggregate performance benchmarks across their user base by industry vertical. Retrieve the most recent available report for {{COMPANY_INDUSTRY}} or the closest adjacent vertical. Cite the platform, report name, publication date, and the vertical the data covers.
   b. Industry association research: many service-business trade associations publish annual operations benchmarks. Identify the relevant association for {{COMPANY_INDUSTRY}} and retrieve their most recent benchmark data. Cite the association, report name, year, and sample size.
   c. Academic and practitioner research on appointment no-show rates and consumer appointment-keeping behavior. Peer-reviewed sources preferred; cite author, journal, year.
   d. Practitioner case studies from service businesses in {{COMPANY_INDUSTRY}} or adjacent verticals that have published specific performance data (confirmation rates, show-up rates, rebook rates). Cite the company, publication, and year.
   e. Pull {{COMPANY_NAME}}'s own trailing 90-day performance data from {{CRM_PLATFORM_NAME}} for each benchmarking dimension. Label this data clearly as internal ground truth.

3. **ANALYZE -- compare {{COMPANY_NAME}} performance against industry benchmarks:**
   - For each benchmark dimension, calculate {{COMPANY_NAME}}'s percentile rank within the available benchmark data range.
   - Identify dimensions where {{COMPANY_NAME}} significantly outperforms (a competitive advantage to understand and protect) or underperforms (an opportunity for improvement).
   - For underperforming dimensions, develop root cause hypotheses: is the gap likely attributable to sequence design (timing, channel, message), technology limitations, policy (cancellation policy too loose?), client quality (acquisition source issue, not a booking experience issue?), or missing touchpoint (no pre-session preparation guide)?
   - Cross-reference root cause hypotheses against the competitive audit findings (SOP 9.1): do top-performing competitors do something specific on this dimension that {{COMPANY_NAME}} does not?

4. **IMPROVE -- produce the Benchmarking Report:**
   - {{COMPANY_NAME}} performance vs. industry benchmarks across all five dimensions, with percentile rank and trend (improving / stable / declining vs. prior period).
   - Gap analysis: for each underperforming dimension, the probable root cause (labeled by confidence level) and the recommended action.
   - Competitive performance cross-reference: where does the competitive audit suggest specific sequence or technology changes that address an identified gap?
   - Priority stack-rank: which improvements, if implemented, are likely to produce the largest performance lift based on the gap size and root cause confidence?

5. **CONTROL -- schedule follow-up measurement.** For any improvement the Director implements based on this benchmarking report, add a 60-day follow-up measurement to the research calendar: pull the same metrics from {{CRM_PLATFORM_NAME}} 60 days after implementation and compare to the pre-change baseline. Quantify the lift (or absence of lift) and file a brief follow-up memo in the research repository.

**Outputs:** Annual benchmarking report (10-18 pages) with company vs. industry performance comparison, gap analysis, root cause hypotheses, and prioritized improvement recommendations.

**Hand to:** Director of Client Experience & Booking (full report + priority improvement list); Booking Coordinator (confirmation and show-up rate findings); Client Onboarding Specialist (first-session rebook rate findings); Post-Session Follow-Up Specialist (LTV and retention findings).

**Failure mode:** Industry benchmark data for appointment-based service businesses varies widely in quality. Scheduling platform self-published benchmarks reflect their own user base, which may not match {{COMPANY_NAME}}'s service type, price point, or client demographic. When applying a published benchmark, explicitly state the source's sample characteristics and the degree of match to {{COMPANY_NAME}}'s context. A service business with a ${{SERVICE_PRICE}} average session and a premium client demographic should not compare its no-show rate to a benchmark derived from free or low-cost appointment types without adjusting for context. Label benchmark comparisons with "Applicable" or "Adjust for context: [reason]" for every data point used.

### SOP 9.4 -- Consumer Appointment Psychology and Retention Behavior Research

**When to run:** Semi-annually as a scheduled update + triggered when a specific behavioral pattern is observed in the department's performance data and the Director needs a research-backed explanation and response strategy.

**Frequency:** Semi-annual + on-demand trigger.

**Inputs:** Consumer behavior research databases (Google Scholar, PubMed for health-adjacent industries, PsycINFO summaries via open-access papers); behavioral economics frameworks (loss aversion, commitment and consistency, implementation intentions, social proof); appointment-keeping and cancellation behavior literature; scheduling platform user behavior studies; direct consumer surveys or reviews from {{COMPANY_INDUSTRY}} sources (Yelp, Google Reviews, industry-specific platforms).

**Steps:**

1. **DEFINE the research scope.** For the semi-annual update, the standard scope covers five behavioral dimensions:
   - What predicts appointment-keeping vs. cancellation vs. no-show (the "commitment-to-show-up" spectrum)?
   - What role does pre-appointment communication design play in show-up behavior (channel, timing, tone, social proof, micro-commitment requests)?
   - What drives a client to rebook after session one vs. go silent (the first-session loyalty decision)?
   - What triggers long-term client retention vs. gradual drift and churn in service relationships?
   - How do digital-first communication patterns (text vs. email vs. app notification vs. phone) affect appointment behavior in {{COMPANY_INDUSTRY}}?

   For a triggered research request, the Director specifies the observed behavioral anomaly and the research question that needs answering.

2. **MEASURE -- gather research across three source tiers:**

   **Tier 1 -- Academic behavioral research:**
   - Search Google Scholar for "appointment keeping behavior" + {{COMPANY_INDUSTRY}} and "no-show reduction" + "service appointment" for papers published in the last 5 years (prefer last 3 years for recency); retrieve and read the full abstract and findings section of the top 5-8 results by citation count and recency.
   - For health-adjacent industries, search PubMed for "appointment adherence" and "no-show interventions" -- this research corpus is the richest for appointment psychology and translates broadly to service businesses.
   - Extract specific findings with effect sizes where available (e.g., "SMS reminders sent 24 hours before appointment reduced no-shows by 26% compared to email-only; p<0.01, n=3,200 appointments").

   **Tier 2 -- Practitioner and platform research:**
   - Retrieve scheduling platform published research: Calendly, Acuity, Vagaro, Jane App, and similar platforms periodically publish data on confirmation sequence effectiveness, reminder timing, and rebook rates.
   - Retrieve HubSpot Service Blog, Zendesk Research, and similar practitioner research sources for client retention behavior in service businesses.
   - Search for case studies from service businesses in {{COMPANY_INDUSTRY}} that have published results from specific retention or no-show reduction interventions.

   **Tier 3 -- Consumer sentiment and review data:**
   - Analyze public consumer reviews on Yelp, Google, and {{COMPANY_INDUSTRY}}-specific review platforms for sentiment patterns around booking experience, confirmation communication, and reasons for not returning after a first session. Do not quote reviews in research reports without confirming they are public and attributing the platform.

3. **ANALYZE -- synthesize into behavioral principles applicable to {{COMPANY_NAME}}'s client experience:**
   - Map each research finding to a specific touchpoint in {{COMPANY_NAME}}'s booking lifecycle (e.g., "Implementation intentions research [Gollwitzer, 2009] suggests that asking clients to write out their plan for attending the appointment in the confirmation email reduces no-shows by 20-30% -- applicable to our Day-1 confirmation message").
   - Identify findings that directly contradict current department practices -- these are high-value findings that should be presented to the Director with the supporting evidence prominently stated.
   - Build or update the "Appointment Psychology Principles" reference document in the research repository: a living document of research-backed behavioral principles that the department uses to design confirmation sequences, onboarding touchpoints, and retention communications.

4. **IMPROVE -- produce the Consumer Appointment Psychology Research Report:**
   - Executive summary: the 5 most actionable behavioral findings for {{COMPANY_NAME}} this period, each linked to a specific recommended sequence design change or new touchpoint.
   - Behavioral finding summaries: for each of the five research dimensions, the key peer-reviewed and practitioner findings, the confidence level (high = multiple independent studies with effect sizes; medium = single study or practitioner data; low = practitioner opinion or single review platform signal), and the specific application to {{COMPANY_NAME}}'s booking lifecycle.
   - Recommendations: sequenced by estimated impact on show-up rate, first-session rebook rate, or LTV; each tied to a specific behavioral finding.
   - Update trigger for next research cycle: if any recommendation is implemented, flag the corresponding behavioral principle for a 90-day outcome measurement (SOP 9.3 follow-up protocol).

5. **CONTROL -- update the Appointment Psychology Principles document** in `{{DEPT_DIR}}/research/behavioral/appointment-psychology-principles.md` with new findings, updated confidence ratings, and superseded findings (where newer research overturns older conclusions). This document is the authoritative behavioral intelligence reference for the entire department.

**Outputs:** Semi-annual Consumer Appointment Psychology Research Report (10-16 pages) with research synthesis, behavioral principles, and prioritized sequence design recommendations; updated Appointment Psychology Principles reference document.

**Hand to:** Director of Client Experience & Booking (full report + recommendations); Booking Coordinator (confirmation and reminder sequence recommendations); Client Onboarding Specialist (first-session behavioral design recommendations); Post-Session Follow-Up Specialist (rebook behavior and retention findings).

**Failure mode:** Behavioral research is frequently context-dependent: a finding from a healthcare no-show reduction study may not transfer perfectly to a coaching or consulting service. When applying academic research findings to {{COMPANY_NAME}}'s specific service context, always note the study's original setting (healthcare, fitness, professional services) and the degree of contextual overlap with {{COMPANY_INDUSTRY}}. Present cross-context applications as "this principle likely applies because [shared mechanism]" rather than as direct replications. High-confidence claims require study replications in a directly comparable service context; single-study findings from adjacent contexts are medium confidence at best.

### SOP 9.5 -- {{COMPANY_INDUSTRY}} Client Experience Trend Forecast

**When to run:** Quarterly as a scheduled strategic intelligence update.

**Frequency:** Quarterly.

**Inputs:** Industry research reports for {{COMPANY_INDUSTRY}} (trade association annual reports, practitioner publications); scheduling and client experience technology trend reports (G2 Annual Software Trends, Capterra industry reports, relevant technology analyst publications); consumer behavior trend data (Pew Research, McKinsey Consumer Sentiment surveys, Nielsen, GWI); competitive intelligence gathered in the current quarter (SOP 9.1 outputs); the Director's stated strategic priorities for the quarter.

**Steps:**

1. **DEFINE the trend scan scope.** Standard quarterly scope covers five dimensions:
   - Technology trends: what scheduling, CRM, communication, and client experience technologies are being adopted, discontinued, or disrupted in {{COMPANY_INDUSTRY}}?
   - Consumer experience expectations: how are client expectations for service booking, communication quality, and personalization shifting?
   - Competitive experience standards: what are the leading service providers in {{COMPANY_INDUSTRY}} doing that is becoming the new minimum bar for client experience?
   - Channel and communication trends: which channels (text, email, app, video, voice) are gaining or losing effectiveness for appointment confirmation, reminders, and post-session follow-up?
   - Regulatory and policy trends: are there emerging data privacy, consumer communication, or service-delivery regulations that will affect how the department manages booking communications (TCPA compliance for SMS, CAN-SPAM for email, etc.)?

2. **MEASURE -- collect trend signals from authoritative sources for each dimension.** For each signal, note the source, date, strength (anecdotal / single-report / multi-source confirmed), and the time horizon at which this trend is expected to materially affect {{COMPANY_NAME}}'s operations (0-6 months / 6-18 months / 18+ months).

3. **ANALYZE -- classify trends by urgency and impact:**
   - **Category A -- Act now:** trend is already affecting the department's performance or will within 6 months; requires an operational response in this quarter.
   - **Category B -- Monitor and prepare:** trend is confirmed and materializing but will reach {{COMPANY_NAME}}'s operations in 6-18 months; requires a preparatory response (capability building, technology evaluation, sequence testing) this quarter.
   - **Category C -- Watch:** trend is emerging with limited signals; requires monitoring but no operational change yet; revisit next quarter.

4. **IMPROVE -- produce the Quarterly Trend Forecast Report:**
   - Top 5 trends by urgency and impact, with classification (A/B/C), evidence summary, and specific recommended response.
   - Full trend register: all identified trends, classified and briefly described.
   - Horizon view: what does the client experience and booking landscape in {{COMPANY_INDUSTRY}} look like in 6, 12, and 18 months based on current trend trajectories?
   - Risk register: trends in Category A or B that, if not addressed, could erode {{COMPANY_NAME}}'s competitive position in client experience within 12 months.

5. **CONTROL -- track forecast accuracy.** At each subsequent quarter's Trend Forecast, review the prior quarter's Category A and B predictions: which materialized as forecast? Which arrived early or late? Which did not materialize? Refine the trend identification methodology based on forecast accuracy. A trend identification system with consistent accuracy in Category A builds the Director's confidence in acting on early-stage intelligence; one that frequently misfires wastes decision bandwidth.

**Outputs:** Quarterly Client Experience Trend Forecast Report (8-14 pages) with classified trends, recommended responses, horizon view, and risk register.

**Hand to:** Director of Client Experience & Booking (full report and Category A action items); Master Orchestrator (if any trend has cross-department implications); CRM department (if technology or communication channel trends affect CRM automation architecture); all department specialists (relevant trend summaries for their specific touchpoints).

**Failure mode:** Trend forecasting has inherent uncertainty. The failure mode is presenting uncertain trends as confirmed facts, causing the Director to make premature or expensive technology or sequence changes in response to signals that do not materialize. Counter this with rigorous classification (A/B/C), explicit confidence labeling, and the forecast accuracy tracking in step 5. If a trend is Category C, it should feel like "here is something worth knowing about, not something requiring action now" -- if the language of the report creates a sense of urgency about a Category C item, revise it.

### SOP 9.6 -- {{COMPANY_INDUSTRY}} Onboarding Experience Best Practice Research

**When to run:** Annually as a structured deep-dive + triggered when the Director initiates an onboarding sequence redesign.

**Frequency:** Annual + on-demand trigger.

**Inputs:** Practitioner case studies and guides on client onboarding for service businesses; academic research on new-client relationship formation and first-impression psychology; competitive intelligence from SOP 9.1 (competitor onboarding approaches); the current {{COMPANY_NAME}} onboarding sequence documentation from the Client Onboarding Specialist; the Director's goals for the onboarding redesign (if triggered).

**Steps:**

1. **DEFINE onboarding scope.** The {{COMPANY_NAME}} onboarding window covers the period from booking confirmation through Day {{ONBOARDING_WINDOW_DAYS}} of the client relationship. Research must be scoped to answer: what communication cadence, content type, and personalization level produces the highest first-session rebook rate in service businesses comparable to {{COMPANY_NAME}}?

2. **MEASURE -- research three dimensions of onboarding best practice:**
   a. **Pre-session preparation communications:** what should clients know, prepare, or do before their first session? Which preparation communication formats (written guide, video walkthrough, intake form, scheduling confirmation summary) produce the highest completion rates and the best first-session outcomes?
   b. **Day-of-session experience:** what touchpoints on the day of the first session (morning confirmation, session summary sent post-session, practitioner-signed personal note) have the strongest documented impact on first-session satisfaction and rebook rate?
   c. **Post-session onboarding (Days 2-{{ONBOARDING_WINDOW_DAYS}}):** what is the optimal communication cadence, channel mix, and content type for the 30-day post-session onboarding window to maximize rebooking, referral intent, and long-term retention?

3. **ANALYZE -- identify the onboarding elements with the highest documented impact on rebook rate and retention**, ranked by evidence strength (peer-reviewed research > multi-company practitioner data > single-company case study > practitioner opinion).

4. **IMPROVE -- produce the Onboarding Best Practice Research Report:**
   - Pre-session best practices: specific communication timing, channel, content, and tone recommendations with supporting evidence.
   - Day-of-session best practices: specific touchpoint recommendations with supporting evidence.
   - Post-session onboarding cadence: recommended sequence structure for Days 2-{{ONBOARDING_WINDOW_DAYS}} with channel, timing, content type, and personalization level for each touchpoint.
   - Gap analysis vs. current {{COMPANY_NAME}} onboarding sequence: where the current sequence aligns with best practice vs. where it diverges.

5. **CONTROL -- hand off to the Client Onboarding Specialist** with a clear brief: "These are the research-backed elements of best-practice onboarding for service businesses in {{COMPANY_INDUSTRY}}. The Director has reviewed these findings. Here is the gap analysis between what we currently do and what the research supports. Your task is to propose a revised sequence based on these findings." Update the onboarding best practice reference in `{{DEPT_DIR}}/research/onboarding/onboarding-best-practice.md`.

**Outputs:** Annual Onboarding Best Practice Research Report (8-14 pages) with pre-session, day-of, and post-session recommendations; gap analysis vs. current sequence; updated reference document.

**Hand to:** Director of Client Experience & Booking (full report); Client Onboarding Specialist (direct handoff with gap analysis brief); Post-Session Follow-Up Specialist (post-session onboarding findings).

**Failure mode:** Onboarding research often produces long lists of "best practice" elements without evidence on which produce the largest impact. Counter this by forcing a priority ranking: if the Client Onboarding Specialist can only implement 3 changes from this research in the next 30 days, which 3 are most likely to move the rebook rate? Every research output must answer this question explicitly.

### SOP 9.7 -- Quick-Research Response

**When to run:** A Director or department specialist submits a specific, bounded research question requiring a timely answer (not a multi-week deep-dive project).

**Frequency:** On-demand (typically 2-5 requests per week).

**Inputs:** Research question (verbatim, from the research request log), context (why the question is being asked; what decision it will inform), response deadline.

**Steps:**

1. **Clarify if needed.** If the question is vague ("What do our competitors do for onboarding?"), ask the requestor ONE clarifying question: "Which competitor specifically, and which onboarding touchpoint -- pre-session, first-session, or the 30-day post-session window?" If the question is specific and bounded ("What is the industry-average no-show rate for personal training in the US?"), proceed immediately without clarifying.

2. **Assess urgency.** Standard quick-research target: answer delivered within 24 hours. If the deadline is sooner (Director needs this for a presentation in 4 hours), prioritize above all other active research.

3. **Conduct targeted research:**
   - Search industry benchmark databases (Statista, scheduling platform reports, trade association publications) for the specific statistic or finding.
   - If the question is competitive ("Does Competitor X use SMS for appointment reminders?"), conduct a focused competitive audit using Tavily (Skill 21) to retrieve current public information from the competitor's website, booking page, and recent content.
   - If the question is behavioral ("Why do clients ghost after the first session?"), search Google Scholar for the most-cited and most-recent peer-reviewed findings; extract the direct answer and supporting evidence.
   - If primary data is unavailable, provide the best available secondary data with explicit caveats about applicability.

4. **Structure the response (1-3 pages based on question complexity):**
   - Direct answer first (lead with the finding, not the methodology).
   - Supporting data with sources cited (author/publication/date for each data point).
   - Confidence assessment: High / Medium / Low, with a one-sentence explanation (e.g., "Medium confidence -- single peer-reviewed study in a healthcare setting; direct applicability to {{COMPANY_INDUSTRY}} is plausible but not confirmed").
   - Limitations and caveats: what should the requestor know about the data's applicability to {{COMPANY_NAME}}'s specific context?
   - Recommended follow-up (if the question reveals a deeper research need): "This question surfaces a gap in our competitive intelligence on [topic]. If you want, I can queue a full SOP 9.1 audit of Competitor X for next month's research calendar."

5. **Deliver and log.** Send the response to the requestor. Log in the research request tracker: question, requestor, date requested, date delivered, decision informed (to be updated by the requestor within 14 days).

6. **Follow up.** Within 14 days, check whether the research informed a decision. Update the tracker entry. If it did not, ask the Director whether the question is still open or the decision was made another way -- this informs research prioritization.

**Outputs:** Quick-research response document (1-3 pages) with direct answer, data, sources, confidence assessment, and recommended follow-up if applicable.

**Hand to:** Requesting specialist or Director.

**Failure mode:** If a quick-research question cannot be answered with available data within 24 hours, do NOT fabricate a number or speculate without labeling it. Instead, deliver: "I could not find a reliable industry benchmark for [specific statistic] from a primary source within the time available. The closest available data point is [X] from [source], which may not apply directly because [reason]. I recommend [either: running a test to generate our own data / commissioning a full research project / asking a practitioner network / accepting uncertainty and making the decision with the available context]. I can have a full answer in [X] days if you want to queue this as a research project." Delivering an honest "here is what I can confirm and here is the gap" is vastly more useful than a confident fabrication.

## 10. Quality Gates

Before any research output ships from this role, it must clear all applicable gates:

### Gate 1 -- Self-Check (run before every output)

- [ ] Every data point cites a specific source (author or publication + date + URL or database reference). ZERO uncited statistics.
- [ ] All competitive intelligence is labeled with a confidence level: Confirmed (directly observed), Estimated (inferred from observable signals with stated methodology), or Inferred (logical inference from indirect evidence -- labeled as such and not presented as fact).
- [ ] Research methodology is documented in the report: what was searched, which databases or sources were used, what date range was covered, what was explicitly NOT researched (scope limits).
- [ ] All recommendations are actionable and specific: they name a concrete change (to a sequence, technology, metric target, or workflow) that the Director or a specialist can implement.
- [ ] Reports >5 pages include an executive summary that stands alone as a decision-support document.
- [ ] Research question or trigger is stated at the top of the report.
- [ ] No speculative claims presented as confirmed facts. Opinions and interpretations are labeled "This researcher's assessment:" or "Based on inference from [sources]:".
- [ ] The report has been spell-checked and is free of placeholder text or unresolved tokens.

### Gate 2 -- Department QC Review (for all outputs labeled "major" or "high-stakes")

The QC Specialist in the Client Experience & Booking department reviews for: source quality and recency (are the cited sources authoritative and current?), logical consistency (do the conclusions follow from the evidence?), absence of confirmation bias (did the research consider disconfirming evidence?), actionability of recommendations, and calibration of confidence labels.

### Gate 3 -- Devil's Advocate Review (for outputs that recommend a major technology adoption, a sequence overhaul, or contradict a current practice the Director has invested in)

The Devil's Advocate evaluates: research design bias, source selection bias, interpretation overreach (do the conclusions go beyond what the evidence supports?), and the cost and risk of being wrong (if the recommendation is implemented and the research is flawed, what is the operational downside?).

### Gate 4 -- Director Approval (for outputs that directly recommend technology procurement >${{TECH_APPROVAL_THRESHOLD}}/year, or a fundamental change to the booking or onboarding experience)

The Director confirms the research aligns with the company's strategic direction and the recommendations are prioritized correctly before implementation begins.

### Example B -- Quick-Research Response (SOP 9.7)

## 14. Bad Output Examples (Anti-Patterns)

### Anti-Pattern A -- The Uncited Benchmark

> "Industry research shows that service businesses achieve a 75% first-session rebook rate when they send a post-session follow-up within 24 hours."

**Why this fails:** no source is cited, no date is given, no sample population is described, and no confidence level is assigned. This statistic cannot be verified, may be fabricated or misremembered from an unrelated context, and if the Director acts on it and the result does not match, the researcher's credibility is destroyed. The research function's entire value rests on source integrity -- a single uncited benchmark undoes it.

**How to fix:** cite the specific report (publication, author, date, sample size) or, if a reliable source cannot be found, report: "I was unable to find a published industry benchmark for first-session rebook rate with a reliable source. The best available proxy is [X] from [source]. If this is a decision-critical data point, I recommend generating our own benchmark from {{CRM_PLATFORM_NAME}} data over the last 90 days."

### Anti-Pattern B -- Competitive Intelligence Presented as Confirmed Fact

> "Competitor X uses a 7-touch confirmation sequence: email at booking, SMS 48 hours out, SMS 24 hours out, email 24 hours out, SMS 2 hours out, phone call 1 hour out, and email same-day post-session."

**Why this fails:** this level of detail about a competitor's internal sequence cannot be confirmed from public observation -- it reads as if the researcher has direct access to the competitor's CRM. Presenting this as fact signals either fabrication or a problematic inference labeled as confirmation. It will cause the Director to trust competitive intelligence that may be entirely invented.

**How to fix:** label every element with its source and confidence level: "Confirmed (directly observed): email confirmation at booking [observed via test booking on intake form]. Inferred (from public Google reviews): post-session email with session notes and rebook link [3 of 11 recent reviews describe this; labeled as inferred]. Unconfirmed: intermediate reminder timing and channels [not observable from public sources; Calendly integration is visible on their booking page -- typical Calendly configurations include 24h and 2h reminders, but their specific settings are not public information]."

## 15. Common Mistakes (Pre-Empted)

| # | Mistake | Root Cause | Prevention |
|---|---------|------------|------------|
| 1 | Citing statistics from a blog post that ultimately traces to a fabricated or methodologically unsound original study | Convenience of secondary sources; headline statistics are easy to find on marketing blogs without tracing to an original study | Trace every statistic to its original published source before citing it; if the original source cannot be found, do not use the statistic; apply the three-source rule for critical benchmark numbers |
| 2 | Treating "what works for the most successful competitors" as universally applicable to {{COMPANY_NAME}} | Correlation bias; "they're successful and they do X, therefore X drives success" | Competitive intelligence identifies what competitors do, not necessarily why they succeed; every competitive finding must be filtered through "does this fit {{COMPANY_NAME}}'s service type, price point, and client demographic?" before becoming a recommendation |
| 3 | Over-delivering depth on low-urgency questions and under-delivering on time-sensitive ones | Researcher identity (thoroughness as a value in itself) vs. operator identity (timeliness is value) | Every research request in the queue is tagged with decision urgency and deadline; high-urgency requests always interrupt active projects; the SOP 9.7 quick-research protocol is the override for time-sensitive needs |
| 4 | Producing trend forecasts based on a single source or a single quarter of signals | Availability bias; the most recent or loudest signal feels like a trend | Trends require multi-source, multi-period confirmation before Category A or B classification; a signal appearing in only one report or one quarter is Category C at best |
| 5 | Conducting technology evaluations without fetching real API documentation for integration claims | Vendor marketing materials overstate integration capability; "integrates with [CRM]" can mean anything from a native bidirectional sync to a Zapier workaround that breaks on plan upgrades | SOP 9.2 mandates Context7 MCP API documentation retrieval for any integration claim that is load-bearing in the recommendation; if the API documentation cannot be retrieved, the integration claim is labeled "Vendor-stated, unverified at API level" |

## 16. Research Sources (Where to Look for Best Practice)

**Tier 1 -- Always consult first for this domain:**

- **Appointy, Jane App, Vagaro, Calendly, Acuity Scheduling** -- scheduling platform benchmark reports (published annually or semi-annually); the most direct source for appointment no-show rates, confirmation sequence performance, and scheduling behavior data segmented by industry vertical.

## 17. Edge Cases for This Role

### Edge Case 17.1 -- Research Request Requires Customer Data the CRM Department Has Not Released

- **Trigger:** The Director requests a benchmarking study that requires pulling cohort-level LTV, rebook rate by booking source, or retention data segmented by onboarding variant -- data that lives in {{CRM_PLATFORM_NAME}} but has not been exported or shared with this role.
- **Action:** (1) Submit a formal data request to the CRM department with a specific data specification: exact fields required, time period, level of aggregation (no PII needed for benchmarking), and the research question the data will answer. (2) Set a 48-hour response expectation. (3) If data is not delivered within 48 hours, escalate to the Director to unblock. (4) Do NOT attempt to access {{CRM_PLATFORM_NAME}} data directly if access has not been provisioned for this role. (5) If the data cannot be obtained, complete the benchmarking study with industry benchmarks only and label the "company vs. benchmark" section as "Pending internal data -- industry context only." Never fabricate or estimate internal performance data; it is available and will be obtained.
- **Escalate to:** Director of Client Experience & Booking (data access blocker), then Master Orchestrator if CRM department does not respond.

### Edge Case 17.2 -- Research Finds {{COMPANY_NAME}}'s Current Booking Sequence Violates a Consumer Communication Regulation

- **Trigger:** Regulatory research (SOP 9.5 trend scan or a quick-research question about compliance) reveals that a current practice -- for example, sending SMS appointment reminders to clients who provided only an email address at booking, or continuing to send marketing follow-up to clients who have not consented to marketing communications -- may violate TCPA (Telephone Consumer Protection Act for US SMS), CASL (Canadian Anti-Spam Legislation), or a similar regulation applicable to {{COMPANY_NAME}}'s jurisdiction and client base.
- **Action:** (1) Do NOT wait for the next scheduled report. Contact the Director of Client Experience & Booking immediately with a one-paragraph urgent brief: the specific regulatory concern, the specific practice that may be affected, the source and date of the regulatory guidance, and the recommended immediate interim action (e.g., pause the SMS sequence pending legal review). (2) Simultaneously route to the Legal & Compliance department for review. (3) Document the finding in the research repository with a "COMPLIANCE FLAG" tag. (4) Do NOT make unilateral changes to any live sequence -- surface the finding and let the Director and Legal & Compliance determine the response.
- **Escalate to:** Director of Client Experience & Booking + Legal & Compliance department simultaneously and immediately.

### Edge Case 17.3 -- Competitive Audit Reveals a Competitor Has Deployed Technology {{COMPANY_NAME}} Cannot Feasibly Match in the Short Term

- **Trigger:** The monthly SOP 9.1 competitive audit finds that a major Tier 1 competitor has deployed a meaningful client experience technology (e.g., a fully AI-driven personalized onboarding sequence, a real-time session progress tracker, or a sophisticated predictive rebook recommendation system) that is technically feasible but would require significant development investment or a technology adoption decision outside the department's authority.
- **Action:** (1) Document the competitive finding in the audit report with the standard evidence labeling. (2) Add a clearly marked section in the report: "Competitive capability gap requiring strategic decision." In it, describe the competitor's capability, the estimated implementation investment (pull from publicly available pricing or practitioner community estimates where available), and the potential impact on competitive differentiation if not addressed. (3) Present the finding to the Director as a strategic decision item, not a research-only finding -- it requires a decision about resource allocation or technology investment that is above the department's authority and belongs to the Master Orchestrator and human owner. (4) Do NOT recommend the competitor's specific technology stack unless a full SOP 9.2 evaluation has been completed. The audit finding triggers an evaluation; it does not conclude one.
- **Escalate to:** Director of Client Experience & Booking (for strategic framing); Master Orchestrator (for resource allocation decision).
