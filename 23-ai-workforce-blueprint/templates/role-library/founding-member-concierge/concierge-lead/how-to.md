1. Confirm all scheduled touches for the day were executed. Log any that were missed and the reason, and reschedule within 24 hours.

## 9. Standard Operating Procedures (Numbered)

### SOP 9.1 -- Executing a Proactive Founding Member Touch

**When to run:** Per the weekly touch cadence (every {{MAX_TOUCH_GAP_DAYS}} days maximum per member) and on-demand when a member event, milestone, or intelligence signal creates a natural opportunity for a timely, relevant contact.
**Frequency:** Multiple times per day across your assigned roster.
**Inputs:** The founding member's Personalization File in {{CRM_PLATFORM_NAME}}, last contact log entry, today's Milestone Calendar, any recent member activity signals (portal engagement, event attendance, messages received).

**Define phase:** The purpose of a proactive touch is not to check a cadence box -- it is to give the member proof that you were thinking about them specifically. Define what "success" looks like for this touch before you send it: what do you want the member to feel, and what signal would confirm the touch landed?

**Measure phase:** Before executing the touch, measure the gap since last meaningful contact (not just any contact -- meaningful contact where real intelligence was exchanged). Confirm the member's communication preference (logged in Personalization File). Note the current health score and any trend direction.

**Analyze phase:** Identify the most relevant personalization anchor for this touch: (a) something the member mentioned in the last conversation that you can follow up on, (b) a current milestone (launch date, business anniversary, personal milestone logged in the Personalization File), (c) a resource that directly addresses their stated #1 current challenge, or (d) relevant industry news that connects to their specific business context. If none of these anchors exist, your Personalization File is incomplete -- update it before executing the touch.

**Improve phase:**
1. Before any touch, review the member's Personalization File: where are they in their business right now? What did they share last time you spoke? What is coming up for them?
2. Select the right channel for this touch based on the member's stated communication preference: voice note (Telegram, Loom), personal message (Telegram, email, {{CRM_PLATFORM_NAME}} DM), or phone call. When in doubt, match the warmth of the moment -- milestone moments deserve voice or video.
3. Craft the touch with one specific, personalized reference: "I was reading about [relevant development] this week and immediately thought of what you told me about [their specific challenge or goal]" or "I realized I never followed up on [thing they mentioned last time] -- how did that go?" Every touch must prove you were listening.
4. If sharing a resource: confirm the resource is genuinely relevant to this specific member's stated goal. Do not send generic resources to fill a cadence slot. Include a one-sentence explanation of why you are sending it specifically to them.
5. Keep the touch appropriately brief. A proactive check-in is not a consultation -- it is a signal that you are thinking of them. Three to five sentences (or a 60 to 90 second voice note) is appropriate. If the touch naturally evolves into a longer conversation, let it -- but do not engineer it.
6. After the touch is sent, immediately log in {{CRM_PLATFORM_NAME}}: date, channel, one-sentence summary of what you said, any response received, and any new intelligence captured.
7. If the member responds and shares new intelligence, log it in the Personalization File and flag if it changes their health score signal (positively or negatively).

**Control phase:** Track touch response rate weekly. If fewer than 70% of your proactive touches are generating a member response within 48 hours over a 2-week period, something is wrong -- either the personalization quality has dropped, the channel is wrong, or the member's context has shifted. Run a personal touch audit: re-read your last 5 touches for that member and identify what is generic, repetitive, or misaligned.

**Outputs:** Touch executed and logged in {{CRM_PLATFORM_NAME}}, Personalization File updated with any new intelligence, health score tag updated if applicable.
**Hand to:** Director (if the touch surfaces an at-risk signal or a significant opportunity requiring director-level attention).
**Failure mode:** If a scheduled touch cannot be executed as planned (member unreachable, system down, timing conflict), do NOT skip the cadence slot and leave it blank. Log the attempt in {{CRM_PLATFORM_NAME}}, reschedule the touch within 24 hours, and notify the Director if the member has now gone more than {{MAX_TOUCH_GAP_DAYS}} days without contact. A gap in cadence is itself a health score signal.

### SOP 9.2 -- Building and Maintaining the Personalization File

**When to run:** After every meaningful conversation with a founding member; reviewed before every significant touch; updated quarterly in full.
**Frequency:** Continuously (after conversations), quarterly (full review and structured update).
**Inputs:** Conversation notes, observations from member communications, business updates from public sources (if the member's business is public-facing), the Personalization File template in {{CRM_PLATFORM_NAME}}.

**Define phase:** The Personalization File is not a data dump -- it is a living intelligence asset. Define what makes a Personalization File "complete" for this member: it should answer the question "If I had to have a meaningful conversation with this person tomorrow, what do I know?" Define which fields are mandatory (see Step 1 below) versus optional enrichment.

**Measure phase:** At the start of each quarter, audit every Personalization File on your roster against the mandatory fields checklist. Any file missing a mandatory field is incomplete regardless of its length. Track the Intelligence Capture Rate KPI (Section 7) to ensure you are adding at least 3 new substantive entries per member per month.

**Analyze phase:** Quarterly, read the full contact log history for each member and identify: (a) recurring themes -- what topics or concerns does this member return to repeatedly? These are what they care about most. (b) Unresolved threads -- what did they mention that was never followed up? (c) Shifts in tone or engagement -- is this member more or less open than 90 days ago?

**Improve phase:**
1. The Personalization File for each founding member must contain at minimum: (a) their primary business goal for the current year, stated by them directly and not inferred, (b) their preferred communication channel and typical response speed, (c) their communication style (formal or informal, brief or detailed, direct or collaborative), (d) three to five personal facts they have shared (family context, hobbies, formative experiences, aspirations beyond business), (e) their biggest current challenge or constraint, (f) milestones and dates to remember (birthday, business anniversary, program anniversary, product launch dates), (g) any sensitivities or topics to approach with care.
2. After every meaningful conversation, open the Personalization File within 1 hour and add any new intelligence captured. New intelligence goes to the top of the relevant section -- it is the most current and therefore the most valuable.
3. Before quarterly review: pull the member's full contact log for the past 90 days. Read every interaction. Identify patterns, unresolved threads, and context shifts.
4. During quarterly review: update every section of the Personalization File with current intelligence. Archive (do not delete) outdated information by moving it to the "Historical Context" subsection -- it provides continuity and reveals evolution over time.
5. Flag to the Director if the Personalization File for any member in your roster is more than 90 days out of date -- this means no substantive intelligence has been captured recently, which is itself a health signal regardless of what the health score shows.

**Control phase:** The QC Specialist reviews Personalization File completeness monthly. Any mandatory field missing or any file not updated in 90+ days is a QC finding. The Concierge Lead is responsible for clearing all QC findings within 5 business days.

**Outputs:** Current, complete Personalization File for every founding member on your roster, logged in {{CRM_PLATFORM_NAME}}.
**Hand to:** Director (quarterly summary of roster intelligence state); Membership Specialist (if any update reveals a benefit or access need requiring administrative action).
**Failure mode:** If a founding member declines to share personal information or prefers to keep conversations strictly professional, honor that completely and note it in the Personalization File: "Prefers professional-only conversation; no personal information shared." Adjust personalization to focus on business-specific intelligence. Never pressure for personal information. The goal is making them feel known, not surveilled.

### SOP 9.3 -- Escalating an At-Risk Health Signal to the Director

**When to run:** When any member on your roster drops 10 or more points in health score, enters the At Risk band (below {{AT_RISK_THRESHOLD}}), or displays a behavioral pattern that, in your professional judgment, signals disengagement risk -- even if the health score has not yet registered the shift.
**Frequency:** On-demand (reactive); must be escalated same day as signal detection.
**Inputs:** Health score dashboard in {{CRM_PLATFORM_NAME}}, recent conversation logs for the member, the specific signal that triggered concern.

**Define phase:** An at-risk signal is any observable change in a member's behavior, tone, or engagement that suggests their commitment to the program is weakening. This includes, but is not limited to: a health score drop of 10+ points, cessation of responses to proactive touches after a previously consistent response pattern, non-attendance at two or more consecutive cohort events by a member who previously attended, a statement in conversation expressing disappointment or ambivalence about the program's value, or a billing event (missed payment, downgrade inquiry). Define the signal clearly before escalating -- "I have a bad feeling" is not an escalation; a documented behavior change is.

**Measure phase:** Quantify the signal: (a) What specifically changed and when? (b) What is the current health score and what was it 30 days ago? (c) How long has this pattern been present -- is this a one-time event or a trend? (d) What was the most recent contact and what was the member's tone?

**Analyze phase:** Develop a hypothesis about the root cause. Do NOT treat the hypothesis as certainty -- you are identifying a most-likely explanation, not delivering a verdict. Possible root causes: (a) program value perception issue (the member is not seeing ROI relative to their investment), (b) life or business circumstance change (a major external event has reduced their available attention), (c) a specific negative experience with the program that has not been surfaced or resolved, (d) natural engagement cycle (some premium members go quiet when they are executing, not when they are dissatisfied -- check for this pattern in their history).

**Improve phase:**
1. As soon as you identify an at-risk signal, stop. Do NOT immediately send a reactive outreach to the member. The first step is to understand the signal, not to respond to it.
2. Open the member's contact record and read the last 30 days of interaction logs. Look for pattern, tone shifts, and any prior signals that may have been underweighted.
3. Write a brief Escalation Summary (a Slack message or brief written note to the Director): (a) member name and roster position, (b) specific signal observed and when, (c) health score current value and trend direction (past 30 days), (d) your hypothesis about the root cause (labeled as a hypothesis, not a certainty), (e) recommended response approach: "I suggest [specific touch type] but want your guidance before proceeding."
4. Submit the escalation to the Director. Do not proceed with any member-facing outreach until the Director approves the approach or explicitly delegates execution authority back to you.
5. After the Director-approved outreach is executed, log the full interaction in {{CRM_PLATFORM_NAME}} and report back to the Director with the member's response and any new intelligence.

**Control phase:** All at-risk escalations must be documented in a shared At-Risk Log maintained by the Director. The Concierge Lead is responsible for updating their members' entries in this log within 24 hours of any meaningful development. No at-risk member should go more than 72 hours without a logged status update.

**Outputs:** Escalation Summary submitted to Director, member-facing intervention executed only after Director approval, post-intervention log in {{CRM_PLATFORM_NAME}}.
**Hand to:** Director of Founding Member Concierge (escalation summary and approval request before any member-facing action).
**Failure mode:** If you cannot reach the Director within 1 hour and the member has sent a message requiring a timely response, proceed with a warm, non-committal acknowledgment: "So glad you reached out -- I want to make sure I give this the attention it deserves. I'll be back to you within [timeframe] with [Director name]'s input." This stabilizes the member without making commitments. Log the situation and attempt to reach the Director via Telegram if normal channels are unresponsive.

### SOP 9.4 -- Conducting a Member Check-In Call

**When to run:** At scheduled cadence intervals (as determined by the member's touch plan), upon returning from a health score drop, or at the invitation of the member. Calls are reserved for moments when the relationship depth warrants a real conversation -- not every touch requires a call.
**Frequency:** Per individual member cadence plan; minimum quarterly per member regardless of health score status.
**Inputs:** Personalization File (reviewed before the call), last 3 contact log entries, the call's stated purpose (check-in, milestone, concern resolution, or renewal conversation -- never start a call without knowing why you're calling and what you want the member to feel when it ends).

**Define phase:** Before scheduling the call, define: (a) what is the purpose of this call from the member's perspective (why is this call valuable to them right now?), (b) what is the one thing you want to learn from this call (what question are you trying to answer about this member's current state?), (c) what, if anything, will you offer or share during the call to make it genuinely useful to them?

**Measure phase:** Review the member's contact history and Personalization File to establish a baseline: (a) when was the last call, what was discussed, and what was committed? (b) Were all prior commitments fulfilled? (c) What has changed in their business or life context since the last meaningful conversation?

**Analyze phase:** Identify the call's highest-value question -- the one open-ended question that, if answered honestly by the member, would give you the most useful intelligence. For a standard check-in it might be: "What's the most important thing you're working on right now?" For a renewal-approaching conversation it might be: "If you could change one thing about your experience with the program, what would it be?" Choose one; do not arrive with a questionnaire.

**Improve phase:**
1. 24 hours before the call, review the member's Personalization File and their last 3 contact logs. Prepare: (a) one genuine question based on something they previously shared, (b) one resource, insight, or connection you want to offer based on their current stated goal, (c) a clear call-opening that proves you remembered something specific from your last conversation.
2. Open the call with warmth and personal reference -- not a formal script. "I was thinking about [specific thing they shared] -- how did that go?" is a better opening than "How are you enjoying the program?"
3. During the call: listen at least as much as you speak. Your goal is to surface intelligence and deepen trust, not to deliver a presentation. Ask open questions. Follow the member's thread, not a fixed agenda.
4. If the member raises any concern, frustration, or disappointment: acknowledge it fully before offering any response or solution. "I hear that -- and I want to make sure we address it properly" lands better than immediately jumping to solutions or defenses.
5. Close every call with a specific, concrete commitment from you: "I'll send you [resource] by Thursday" or "I'll connect you with [person] -- expect an introduction this week." Make only commitments you can keep. Log the commitment as a task in {{CRM_PLATFORM_NAME}} with a deadline before the call ends.
6. Immediately after the call, log in {{CRM_PLATFORM_NAME}}: the date and duration, every piece of intelligence shared by the member, the commitment you made (flagged as a task with a specific deadline), and the member's overall tone and engagement level (not just "positive" -- be specific: "energized about launch," "cautious about Q3 timeline," "expressed frustration about access issue that was later resolved").
7. Execute any commitment made on the call within the stated timeline. If a commitment cannot be kept (unforeseen obstacle), notify the member proactively before the deadline -- never silently miss a commitment.

**Control phase:** Commitment follow-through is tracked as a KPI (Section 7). Any commitment missed -- even by one day without prior notice to the member -- is logged as a commitment failure event. Three commitment failures in a quarter is a performance conversation with the Director.

**Outputs:** Call completed and logged in {{CRM_PLATFORM_NAME}}, all commitments made flagged as tasks with deadlines, Personalization File updated with new intelligence, Director notified of any at-risk signals surfaced during the call.
**Hand to:** Director (if at-risk signals surfaced); Membership Specialist (if administrative action needed based on call intelligence); relevant cross-department (if member's issue requires cross-functional resolution).
**Failure mode:** If the call reveals significant dissatisfaction that you are not equipped to resolve in the moment, do NOT improvise or make commitments you are not authorized to make. Say: "I want to make sure you get the right answer on this, not just a fast one. Let me involve [Director name] -- I'll follow up within 24 hours with a clear path forward." Then immediately escalate to the Director with a full debrief of what was said.

### SOP 9.5 -- Executing a Renewal Readiness Assessment

**When to run:** For every member on your roster whose program renewal date falls within the next 90 days. This is not optional -- a renewal conversation that surprises either party is a failure of the concierge relationship.
**Frequency:** Triggered at 90 days pre-renewal for each member; reassessed at 60 days and 30 days.
**Inputs:** Member's renewal date (logged in {{CRM_PLATFORM_NAME}}), health score trend for the past 6 months, contact log and Personalization File, benefit utilization report, any previous renewal conversations or notes.

**Define phase:** Renewal is not a transaction -- it is a relationship verdict. The renewal decision reflects how well the program has delivered on its promise to this specific founding member, and how well the concierge relationship has made them feel known and valued. Define the outcome you are working toward: a renewal that happens with confidence and genuine enthusiasm, not one that is extracted through pressure or discounting.

**Measure phase:** Assess the member's current renewal posture using four signals: (a) engagement trajectory (has their engagement been rising, flat, or declining over the past 6 months?), (b) value perception (based on your conversations, do they speak about the program as something that is actively serving their goals, or in neutral or past-tense language?), (c) relationship temperature (do they respond to your touches promptly and with warmth, or are responses becoming shorter and more delayed?), (d) any explicit comments about renewal, value, or program investment (log and date every statement they have made that is relevant to their renewal posture).

**Analyze phase:** Assign a Renewal Risk Rating: Green (member is engaged, expresses positive value perception, relationship is warm, no signals of hesitation), Yellow (one or more signals of ambivalence but no explicit concern expressed -- require a proactive renewal conversation), or Red (multiple signals of disengagement, declining engagement trajectory, and/or explicit concerns expressed -- require an immediate Director escalation and a structured intervention before the renewal conversation). Document the rating with specific evidence for each signal.

**Improve phase:**
1. At 90 days pre-renewal: write the Renewal Readiness Assessment document in {{CRM_PLATFORM_NAME}} covering all four signals above with a risk rating and recommended approach. Submit to Director for review.
2. At 60 days pre-renewal: if Green, execute a proactive renewal conversation (not a sales pitch -- a genuine check-in that includes a natural inquiry about their plans for the coming year and what they most want to get out of the program). If Yellow, escalate to Director before the 60-day conversation; the approach needs to be co-designed. If Red, Director owns the intervention strategy; you support.
3. At 30 days pre-renewal: confirm the renewal is on track. If the member has not renewed and no objection has been raised, execute one more direct, warm inquiry: "I want to make sure we've answered any questions you have about the coming year before your renewal date on [date]."
4. Regardless of outcome, document the full renewal journey in {{CRM_PLATFORM_NAME}}: what was communicated, what the member said, and the final outcome (renewed, exited with reason, extended with negotiation).

**Control phase:** The Director reviews all Renewal Readiness Assessments at 90 days out. Any member without a filed assessment at 90 days is an immediate SOP violation. The goal is zero surprise exits -- every exit should be foreseeable from the 90-day assessment.

**Outputs:** Renewal Readiness Assessment filed in {{CRM_PLATFORM_NAME}}, renewal conversation executed per the risk-rating-appropriate approach, renewal outcome documented with member's stated reasoning.
**Hand to:** Director of Founding Member Concierge (assessment at 90 days, escalation for Yellow/Red); Membership Specialist (if renewal includes benefit changes or administrative updates).
**Failure mode:** If a member does not renew, do NOT treat it as a closed matter and move on. Within 48 hours of a non-renewal, file an Exit Intelligence Report with the Director: (a) what signals were present in the last 6 months that could have predicted this, (b) were they escalated appropriately or missed, (c) what specific concern or reason (if shared) led to the exit, (d) what, if anything, could have changed the outcome. This is not a blame exercise -- it is a learning exercise to improve the system.

### SOP 9.6 -- Handling a Member Request Outside Your Authorization Scope

**When to run:** When a founding member requests something during a conversation, call, or message that you cannot approve or deliver without Director or owner authorization -- including custom program accommodations, owner access or introductions, pricing or billing exceptions, or cross-department commitments.
**Frequency:** On-demand.
**Inputs:** The specific request made by the member, your current authorization scope (defined in the Department Authorization Matrix in AGENTS.md), the member's current health score and relationship context.

**Define phase:** Clearly define what is being requested and who has the authority to fulfill it. The risk in this SOP is improvising a "yes" that creates a broken promise or an unauthorized commitment, OR deflecting in a way that makes the member feel their request is unwelcome or unimportant. The goal is: acknowledge the request warmly, take clear ownership of surfacing it to the right person, and deliver a specific follow-up timeline.

**Measure phase:** Before responding, determine: (a) is this request within my authorization scope? (b) if not, who specifically can authorize it (Director, owner, or another department)? (c) What is a realistic timeline for getting a decision and communicating back to the member?

**Analyze phase:** Assess the relational stakes of how you handle this. A founding member who makes a request and receives a warm acknowledgment followed by a specific timeline and a fulfilled follow-up actually increases their trust in the program. A founding member who receives an improvised answer that turns out to be wrong, or who receives a deflection with no clear ownership, loses trust. The relationship risk is in the handling, not in the request itself.

**Improve phase:**
1. When a member makes a request outside your scope, respond immediately with warm acknowledgment: "That is a great question [or: that is something I want to make sure we handle well for you]. Let me take that directly to [Director name / the right person] and get you a clear answer."
2. Specify the follow-up timeline: "You can expect to hear from me with an update by [specific date and time]."
3. Immediately after the conversation, submit the request to the Director (or relevant authorized person) in writing with: (a) the member's exact request in their words, (b) the context from the conversation that led to the request, (c) the timeline you committed to the member for a follow-up, (d) your recommended response if you have one (clearly labeled as a recommendation, not a commitment).
4. Follow up with the member within the promised timeline regardless of whether a decision has been made. If the decision is still pending: "I wanted to follow up as promised -- we are still working through this and I will have a definitive answer for you by [new specific date]." Never go past a committed timeline without communicating proactively.
5. Once the authorized decision is received, communicate it to the member clearly and personally -- not via a form message. If the answer is "no," deliver it with context and, where possible, an alternative: "We are not able to [specific request], but here is what we can do..."

**Control phase:** All out-of-scope requests must be logged in {{CRM_PLATFORM_NAME}} as a task with the requesting member, the nature of the request, the authorization path, and the follow-up timeline. These are reviewed by the Director weekly to identify patterns in member needs that may indicate a program gap.

**Outputs:** Member receives a warm acknowledgment with a specific follow-up timeline, Director receives a written request brief within 1 hour of the conversation, member receives a definitive answer within the promised timeline.
**Hand to:** Director of Founding Member Concierge (request brief and authorization request); owner (if request requires owner authorization); relevant department (if request involves cross-department action).
**Failure mode:** If the timeline you committed to the member passes and you still have no authorized decision, do not go silent. Proactively message the member: "I want to make sure this stays on your radar -- I am still working to get you a clear answer and I will have something for you by [new date]." Silence past a committed deadline is the fastest way to erode a founding member's trust.

## 13. Good Output Examples

### Example A -- A Well-Executed Proactive Touch

**Context:** A founding member mentioned 3 weeks ago that they were preparing to launch a new offer. Today is their launch date, logged in the Milestone Calendar.

**Output (Concierge Lead voice note, approximately 60 seconds):**
"[First name] -- today is the day. I've been thinking about you all morning. I know how much work has gone into this offer, and I just wanted to say: I'm genuinely rooting for you. I hope it goes exactly the way you've been picturing it. Whatever happens today -- it's a big deal that you got here. Send me a message later and let me know how it went. I mean it."

**Why this is good:**
- It is timely (launch day, not a week before or after).
- It is warm without being performative or scripted.
- It references intelligence from a specific previous conversation.
- It asks for nothing -- it is purely an act of support and presence.
- A voice note carries warmth that a text message cannot on a milestone day.

### Example B -- A Contact Log Entry

**Output (logged in {{CRM_PLATFORM_NAME}} after a 22-minute check-in call):**

Health score assessment: stable, no change warranted.
Next touch: in 10 days. Reference the VA guide follow-up and ask how the partner transition is going."

**Why this is good:**
- Every piece of intelligence is specific, dated, and actionable.
- It distinguishes genuine enthusiasm from polite acknowledgment.
- The commitment is logged with a deadline.
- The next touch has a specific thread to pick up -- it proves that what she shares is remembered and followed up on.
- The health score assessment is included and reasoned, not left blank.

## 14. Bad Output Examples (Anti-Patterns)

### Anti-Pattern A -- The "Touching Base" Touch

**What went wrong:** A Concierge Lead sends a founding member the following message: "Hey [Name]! Just wanted to touch base and see how things are going with the program. Let us know if there's anything you need!"

**Why this fails:**
- It demonstrates zero knowledge of the member's specific situation or goals.
- It is passive -- it puts the burden on the member to identify their own needs rather than the Concierge Lead proactively surfacing value.
- "Just wanted to touch base" is the verbal equivalent of a form letter -- it signals template, not genuine attention.
- The founding member experience promise is individualized; this message delivers generic.
- A founding member who receives this from their "dedicated concierge" reasonably wonders what they are paying a premium for.

**How to fix:** Reference something specific. Every touch must prove you were listening. If you cannot find a personalization anchor, your Personalization File needs updating -- start there before sending anything.

### Anti-Pattern B -- Making an Unauthorized Commitment During a Call

**What went wrong:** During a check-in call, a founding member mentions they would like a one-on-one with the owner. The Concierge Lead, wanting to be helpful and make the member feel valued, says "I'll make that happen for you." The owner has not agreed to this, the owner's calendar is not available, and now a commitment has been made to a founding member that may not be fulfillable.

**Why this fails:**
- Making commitments beyond your authority creates broken promises -- which are health score events and trust erosion events.
- The founding member's expectation is now set at a level that only the owner can satisfy, and the owner was never consulted.
- If the commitment is not kept, the member's trust in the entire program is affected -- not just in the Concierge Lead, but in the program as a whole.
- The road to a broken promise was paved with wanting to be helpful. Good intentions do not mitigate the damage of an undelivered commitment to a founding member.

**How to fix:** "That's a great idea -- let me take that to [Director/owner name] and come back to you with a clear answer within 24 hours." This manages expectations, demonstrates advocacy on the member's behalf, and keeps the commitment honest. Then actually follow up within 24 hours regardless of the answer.

## 17. Edge Cases for This Role

### Edge Case 17.1 -- A Member You Are Assigned Does Not Want Relationship-Level Contact

**Trigger:** A founding member indicates (directly or through sustained non-response behavior) that they do not want personal check-ins -- they enrolled for the program benefits and deliverables, not the relationship layer.

**Action:** (1) Respect this completely and without defensiveness. Not all founding members want a concierge relationship -- and forcing one damages trust more quickly than anything else. (2) Shift to a lighter-touch, benefit-focused model: periodic updates on program developments, event invitations, and relevant resources -- no personal check-ins or warmth-forward outreach. (3) Log the preference clearly in the Personalization File: "Prefers minimal contact. Program updates and relevant resources only. No personal check-ins." (4) Monitor health score for this member via automated signals (engagement data, billing status, event attendance) rather than direct conversation. (5) Notify Director and agree on a modified touch cadence for this member.

**Escalate to:** Director (for awareness and a modified health score monitoring plan that does not rely on direct conversation frequency).

### Edge Case 17.2 -- A Member Discloses a Personal Crisis During a Check-In Call

**Trigger:** During a routine check-in, a founding member discloses something serious -- a health challenge, family emergency, business crisis, or personal loss.

**Action:** (1) Stop the concierge agenda entirely. This is no longer a check-in call -- it is a human conversation. (2) Listen without trying to fix anything. Express genuine care without platitudes: "I'm so sorry you're carrying this. Thank you for trusting me with it." (3) Ask what they need from you right now -- not what the program can offer, but what they personally need. Sometimes the answer is nothing. That is completely fine. (4) Do not mention the program, its benefits, or any business agenda for the remainder of the conversation. (5) After the call, log the disclosure in {{CRM_PLATFORM_NAME}} with appropriate sensitivity (note what was shared without excessive detail) and notify the Director immediately. (6) Propose a grace period: pause any sales-adjacent or program-adjacent touches and shift to a lighter, purely supportive cadence for 30 days minimum, pending Director agreement.

**Escalate to:** Director (immediately after the call), with recommendation for a grace period and a proposed check-in approach for the grace period.

### Edge Case 17.3 -- A Member Attempts to Transition the Relationship Into a Business or Personal Friendship Outside Program Boundaries

**Trigger:** A founding member begins suggesting contact outside the program context (social media connections, personal social invitations, requests for personal contact information, or language that suggests they view the relationship as a personal friendship rather than a professional one).

**Action:** (1) Respond with warmth that does not close the door harshly, but maintain professional boundaries: "I really appreciate the connection we've built -- you're the kind of person I genuinely enjoy talking to. I keep my professional relationships through [platform/program channels] to make sure I can give everyone the attention they deserve, but I'm always glad to connect here." (2) Log the interaction in the Personalization File with a note: "Member has shown interest in personal connection outside program context. Maintained professional boundary warmly." (3) Notify the Director -- this is information the Director needs to be aware of, not because anything inappropriate has occurred, but because it is a relationship signal that affects how the account should be managed going forward.

**Escalate to:** Director (awareness notification within the same day).
