# PA-04-02 — Daily Top 3 Selection

**Role:** Task & Priority Manager
**Department:** Personal Assistant (04)

## Define

Daily Top 3 Selection turns a long task list into a short, honest plan for the day. Each morning the assistant selects the three tasks that, if completed, would make the day a success for the owner — and protects time for them.

**Purpose of this procedure**
- Convert an overwhelming backlog into a focused, achievable daily commitment.
- Ensure the most important work — not merely the easiest or loudest — gets the owner's prime attention.
- Give the owner a clear answer to 'what matters most today?' before the day's noise begins.

**When to run**
- Every morning, before the owner begins their working day.

**Frequency**
- Daily.

**Inputs**
- The full task list from the Task Capture system (PA-04-01).
- The day's calendar (so the plan fits the available time).
- Hard deadlines and any commitments due today or tomorrow.
- The owner's stated priorities and current goals.

**Outputs**
- A written Daily Top 3 delivered to the owner with a one-line rationale for each.
- Calendar time blocked for the Top 3 where focus is required.

**Hand-to (downstream)**
- The owner (for the day's execution) and the Deadline Tracking procedure (PA-04-03) for anything time-critical not in the Top 3.

**Definition of done**
- The owner has, before their day starts, three clearly stated priorities and protected time to do them.

## Measure

The Top 3 is measured by completion and by whether the right things were chosen — a day where all three were finished but none mattered is a selection failure, not a success.

**Signals this procedure tracks**
- How many of the Top 3 were actually completed.
- How often a 'true number one' priority was left off the list.
- How often the day was derailed by something that should have been foreseen.

**Key metrics**
- **Top 3 completion rate** — percentage of selected priorities completed same-day; target above 80%.
- **Right-three rate** — owner confirms the three were the right three; target near 100%.
- **Derailment count** — days knocked off plan by foreseeable interruptions; minimize.

**Where the measurement is recorded**
- End-of-day, the assistant notes which of the Top 3 closed and why any did not, in the role MEMORY.md.

## Analyze

Selection errors usually come from confusing urgency with importance, or from picking tasks that feel productive over tasks that move goals.

**Decision rules**
- If a task is both urgent and important, it is a Top 3 candidate.
- If a task is urgent but not important, delegate or batch it — it does not belong in the Top 3.
- If none of the three are hard, the selection criteria were wrong; re-select against the owner's real goals.

**Common failure modes and how to read them**
- **Easy-task bias:** Choosing the three easiest tasks to feel productive. The Top 3 is a priority decision, not a checklist trick.
- **Overcommitment:** Selecting tasks that cannot fit the available calendar time. Match the plan to the hours that exist.
- **Ignoring deadlines:** Leaving a same-day hard deadline off the Top 3. Hard deadlines win the slot.

## Improve

Selection improves by tightening the link between the Top 3 and the owner's actual goals, and by learning from days that went sideways.

**Step-by-step procedure**
1. Pull the full task list and the day's calendar.
2. Identify every hard deadline due today or tomorrow; these are mandatory candidates.
3. Against the owner's current goals, ask of each task: 'if only this got done today, would the day be a win?'
4. Select the three highest-leverage tasks — important first, then urgent — that realistically fit the available time.
5. Block calendar time for any of the three that needs focus, ahead of the day's meetings.
6. Write the Top 3 with a one-line 'why this matters' for each and deliver it to the owner before their day starts.
7. At end of day, record which closed and why any did not; feed that into tomorrow's selection.

**Escalation path**
- If hard deadlines repeatedly crowd out important-but-not-urgent work, flag a structural problem to the owner — the schedule, not the selection, needs to change.

**Optimization loop**
- Track which categories of task the owner consistently finishes vs. defers, and weight selection accordingly.
- Keep the rationale lines short and concrete so the owner trusts the list at a glance.

## Control

Control keeps this procedure stable and trustworthy over time. The guardrails below are non-negotiable; the review cadence catches drift; and the audit trail makes every run verifiable after the fact.

**Guardrails (never violate)**
- Never let the Top 3 exceed three; the constraint is the point.
- Never select a plan that cannot fit the day's real available time.
- Never bury a same-day hard deadline below softer work.

**Review cadence**
- Weekly: review completion rate and right-three rate; adjust selection criteria with the owner.

**Failure mode to actively prevent**
- Selecting the 3 easiest tasks to check off rather than the 3 most important ones.

**Audit trail**
- Each day's Top 3 and its outcome are recorded in MEMORY.md.
- Weekly the assistant reviews the trend and notes any criteria change.
