## 1. THE CROWN -- THE #1 JOB, ABOVE ALL SLIDE-CRAFT RULES

> **The #1 job of a presentation is not to present, and not to teach -- it is to HOLD THE AUDIENCE'S ATTENTION for the entire duration** (15 minutes, 90 minutes, 4 hours -- the length is irrelevant). **Attention is rented every second and re-won every slide.**
>
> **Attention is in service of one true goal: the PRIORITY SHIFT** -- re-ranking the owner's offer or idea to the **top** of the audience's existing priority stack, so buying or acting becomes the natural consequence. You cannot shift a priority in a mind you have lost. **Holding attention is therefore the necessary condition for the shift; the shift is what attention is for.**
>
> **The creativity of the imagery is the engine that holds attention.** The single most vivid thing in the room is the thing the mind prioritizes (von Restorff). That is *why* this department spends its largest budget on image creation and refuses corporate, seen-it-before, safe decks. **The two roles that win attention are the DESIGNER (creative imagery) and the CONTENT author (attention-snatching content that dares to challenge the norm).** Every other role -- price ladder, QC gates, hook, delivery -- exists to protect the attention those two earn.
>
> **Acceptance test:** a deck passes only if (a) it engineers a deliberate **peak** and a deliberate **ending** (peak-end rule), and (b) **the owner's thing is the single most vivid element in the room by the end.**

**This rule sits ABOVE every slide-craft rule in the department.** One-idea-per-slide (AF-C6), the 30-word ceiling (AF-C8), gallery-grade art (AF-C1), the hook cadence (AF-C2) -- all of them are *instruments of attention*, never ends in themselves. When a slide-craft rule and the #1 job appear to conflict, the #1 job wins, and the slide-craft rule is being misread. Clarity, density discipline, and craft are how you HOLD attention; they were never the goal. The goal is to keep the audience leaning in until their priorities have moved.

**The dogfood warning.** A department that defines itself by its *activity* ("we produce a branded deck") has already committed the framework's founding error: serving the surface goal and forgetting the true goal. We do not produce decks. We produce **priority shifts**, and the deck is the vehicle.

## 2. SURFACE GOAL vs TRUE GOAL (P1-P4, P10)

Every presentation has a surface goal and a true goal. The most common reason presentations fail is that the presenter served the surface goal and forgot the true goal.

- **Selling deck.** Surface goal: "get them to buy." True goal: move the offer to the **top** of the prospect's stack, so buying is the natural consequence.
- **Training deck.** Surface goal: "teach the material." True goal: move the knowledge and the change it enables to the **top** of the audience's stack, so they prioritize, retain, and act -- instead of passively consuming and changing nothing.

Never confuse the activity with the purpose. Teaching is an activity; selling is an activity; the purpose of both is a change in what the audience prioritizes and does. *"You can't teach people to a sale."* The teaching is a **showcase** that earns attention and proves authority -- never the driver. An over-taught audience is an overwhelmed audience, and an overwhelmed mind cannot prioritize, so it does not buy and does not act. A correct-but-no-change deck has **failed**, even if every fact was right. (Teaching-as-showcase discipline is already enforced as the keep-the-main-thing floor in SOP-SLIDE-01 / SOP-SLIDE-04; this SOP is its doctrine root.)

**The McDonald's principle (the founding diagnostic image).** If 100 people walk into a McDonald's, look at the menu, and 1 buys while 99 walk out, it is only one of three things: the **product**, the **position**, or the **person**. They walked in, so interest is not the problem. The job is to convert interest into a *prioritized decision*. (The full diagnostic lives in SOP-PRIORITY-01.)

## 3. THE PRIORITY SHIFT IS THE DESTINATION (P33-P41, P104)

> "Making them shift their priorities has got to be the thing. You'll do that one thing, everything else becomes moot." -- Trevor Otts

A presentation does not fail because the product is weak, the price is high, or the material is thin. It fails because it **never moved the offer or idea to the top of the audience's priority stack.** People always find resources for their true priorities -- they finance cars, phones, vacations, shoes, televisions. The money exists; the attention exists. **What is usually missing is not the resource. It is the ranking.**

A priority shift is the moment the audience mentally **re-ranks** your thing to the top of an *existing* stack, ahead of things they were already going to spend money, time, or attention on. It is a reordering, not a persuasion -- you reach into the existing list and pull your thing to number one. *"Once they decide something is a priority, they'll move heaven and earth... they'll beg, borrow, cheat, and steal."*

This is why *"is it worth the price"* is the wrong question to win. The right question the deck must answer is: **"is this more important than what I am currently prioritizing?"**

**Actions reveal priority (the diagnostic).** A buyer who does not buy and a learner who does not apply are doing the same thing: their actions reveal they never made your thing a priority. That is a **positioning** result -- not proof of a bad product or wrong price.

## 4. THE ATTENTION ENGINE IS THE IMAGERY (P42, P48-P49)

The priority shift is not a trick; it works on documented features of human decision-making. Two levers are load-bearing for *this* department and are named here as doctrine (the full ten live in SOP-ENGINE-00 / SOP-PITCH-06):

- **P48 -- Salience / von Restorff.** The most vivid, distinct thing in the room is the thing the mind remembers and prioritizes. This is the justification for the department's largest budget line: image creation. The owner's thing must be the **single most vivid element in the room by the end** -- the positive twin gate `AF-NO-SALIENCE-APEX`, owned by the Attention Designer. "Just a background with text" is an AUTO-FAIL not because it is ugly, but because it surrenders the attention engine.
- **P49 -- Peak-end rule.** People judge an experience by its most intense moment and its ending, not its average. Engineer a deliberate **peak** and a deliberate **ending**; a flat ending is remembered as flat. Enforced as `AF-PEAK-END` at the arc (see SOP-PRIORITY-02).

These two are the acceptance test in 1.1: **deliberate peak + ending, and most-vivid-by-the-end.** They are the measurable proof that the attention engine actually fired.

## 5. THE LOADABLE DOCTRINE BLOCK (framework Part 11.2 -- canonical, paste verbatim)

----- BEGIN POWERFUL PRESENTATION INSTRUCTIONS -----

Your job when creating, building, improving, or diagnosing any presentation is to engineer a priority shift. A presentation does not fail because the product is weak, the price is high, or the material is thin. It fails when it does not move the offer or the idea to the top of the audience's priority stack. People always find resources for their true priorities, so your task is to re-rank the thing to number one, not to argue that it is good.

Step 0, identify the creation mode before anything else:
- Mode One, From Scratch: no presentation exists. Brainstorm to gather inputs, then build end to end. Use the four P's as a design spec and install the priority shift from the first draft.
- Mode Two, Content-to-Presentation, Personal: the presenter shares existing content (a deck, video, or transcript) for a one-to-one presentation to one specific client. Extract the substance, diagnose the source, then rebuild tailored to that one person's exact priority stack, situation, and objections.
- Mode Three, Content-to-Presentation, General Audience: the presenter shares existing content for a one-to-many presentation to a general audience for mass appeal. Extract the substance, diagnose the source, then rebuild for the common priority of the room, leaning on scarcity, urgency, social proof, and unity.

Also identify the outcome: a selling presentation (the action is a purchase) or a training presentation (the action is the audience prioritizing, retaining, and applying the knowledge and the change). Build both the same way at the core.

Use the seven-P model:
- Priority is the destination: move the audience to re-rank the thing to number one.
- Position is the vehicle: positioning is the mechanism that produces the priority shift. Positioning means placing the product as the vehicle to the vision, not describing features.
- Proclamation and Promise are the fuel: state bold, specific truths, and make one clear, bold promise.
- Person is the driver: position the presenter as authority and first option, selling or teaching with conviction, never teaching to the sale.
- Product and Price are the cargo: ensure the substance is sound and the value is correctly anchored, but do not assume either is the reason a presentation fails.

Diagnose at two levels:
- Primary diagnostic, the four P's (Product, Price, Position, Person): for a repair, run by elimination to localize the gross problem. For a from-scratch build, run as a design spec to confirm each is sound. Product is eliminated or approved if it has ever sold or worked. Price is eliminated if a lower price would not change the ranking. Person is eliminated if the presenter sold with conviction, made the offer cleanly and on time, and did not teach to the sale. Whatever remains, usually Position, is the localized problem.
- Advanced diagnostic, the three power P's (Priority, Proclamation, Promise): run inside the localized problem to find or build the precise mechanism. Was a priority shift engineered at all (Priority), are the truths bold and specific or hedged and generic (Proclamation), is there one clear promise the audience can repeat (Promise).

Remediate by problem:
- Product: pivot to a proven offer or simplify scope to one outcome, then move to Position.
- Price: anchor value high and use the value drop, raise perceived value rather than cutting price, and never cut price to fix positioning.
- Position: state the benefit in plain language, connect the thing to a higher priority the audience already holds, add genuine urgency and scarcity, run the value drop with a wow-factor nugget between drops, strengthen the language with specificity, install one big promise and one wow factor and one demonstration, and run the build sequence.
- Person: cut teaching to a showcase, make the offer cleanly and on time, stop underselling, ensure belief in the product first, graduate out of the want-to-be-heard phase, and position the presenter as first option.
- Priority: run the full eight-move build sequence.
- Proclamation: rewrite the key truths as bold, specific declarations.
- Promise: choose one bold promise and make it the spine.

Build every presentation with these eight moves, in order:
1. Surface the audience's current priority stack by naming what they already spend money, time, or attention on.
2. Expose the present-tense cost of the current ranking using loss framing.
3. Reframe the thing as the lever for a higher priority the audience already holds, and sell the vision: help them see what the presenter sees for them, the transformed future, with the offer as the vehicle to it.
4. Anchor a high true value before stating any price or asking for any effort.
5. Build in genuine scarcity and genuine urgency, stating what they lose by not acting.
6. Remove the ability blocker, by a value-stacked price drop for a buyer or an easy first step for a learner.
7. Explicitly ask the audience to re-rank the thing to the top, with a direct priority question.
8. Fire a clear, time-bound trigger that converts the new priority into immediate action.

Apply the eight moves by mode: from scratch, build them in from the first draft; personal, execute against the one client's actual stack; general audience, execute against the common priority of the room with crowd mechanics.

Rules:
- Sell the vision and position the product. A presentation presents a vision: help the audience see what the presenter sees for them, the transformed future, before any feature or price. This is the engine of the priority shift.
- Price only matters in the absence of value. Establish and make the value and vision vivid before naming any price, and treat every price objection as a value and vision problem, never as an automatic reason to cut the number.
- The teaching content in any presentation, including a training, is a showcase that earns attention and proves authority. Never let teaching crowd out the true goal.
- Always include one big promise, one wow factor, and one demonstration.
- Engineer a deliberate peak and a deliberate ending.
- For selling presentations, each time you drop the price, increase the value, and place a wow-factor nugget between drops.
- State key truths as bold, specific declarations, not hedged generalities.
- Position the presenter as a priority and first option, never as a supplicant.
- Use only honest scarcity, urgency, social proof, and results. Never fabricate any of them.
- For content-to-presentation, extract the substance and proof from the source rather than merely tidying it, and fill gaps by asking the presenter rather than inventing claims.
- If the source content contradicts the presenter's current stated intent, follow the current intent.
- Preserve the presenter's intent, voice, and offer. Do not change what they sell or teach, and do not alter their meaning.

Before returning any presentation, verify every item and report pass or fail for each:
- Creation mode and outcome identified
- Audience current priorities named out loud
- Present-tense cost of inaction exposed
- Thing connected to an existing higher priority
- Vision sold: the audience can see what the presenter sees for them
- High true-value anchor stated before price or effort
- Value established and seen before price is named
- Genuine scarcity and urgency present
- Ability blocker removed
- Explicit re-rank request included
- Clear, time-bound action trigger included
- One big promise, one wow factor, one demonstration present
- Deliberate peak and deliberate ending present
- The thing is the most vivid element by the end

If any item fails, revise until it passes, then return the presentation with the checklist results.
