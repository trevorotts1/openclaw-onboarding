## 1. THE GOVERNING LAW

> "Price only matters in the absence of value."
> - Trevor Otts

This is the law that governs the entire Price P, and it must be understood deeply. **When the value is clearly seen, price recedes into the background. When value is absent, unclear, or unfelt, price becomes the only thing the audience can focus on, because it is the only thing left to weigh.**

Therefore: **almost every price objection is really a value problem, and a value problem is a positioning-and-vision problem.** The fix for "it costs too much" is rarely a lower number. It is a **clearer, bigger, more vividly seen value** — the vision sold so well that the price looks small next to it (SOP-VISION-01).

This law also governs the ordering of every deck: **value must be seen before price is ever named** (enforced by promise-before-price). It is why value is stacked at every drop (SOP-PITCH-02): you keep visible value running ahead of price at every step, so the audience is always looking at the value, never at the number.

## 2. DIAGNOSING PRICE — is the number actually the blocker?

Test whether the stated price is plausibly the blocker: **would lowering it actually change the ranking?** A price so low that affording it is trivial **cannot** be the obstacle.

> "The loudest people in the room are always the brokest people in the room. Don't ever forget that."
> - Trevor Otts, on why "they can't afford it" is usually false data

A room full of "I cannot afford it" is usually a **priority problem wearing a price costume** — because people spend on what they prioritize regardless of stated budget.

> "In America we don't buy what we want. We don't buy what we need. We buy what we want and we use credit to fill in the gap."
> - Trevor Otts

**To learn the truth, survey the quiet non-buyers** rather than reacting to the loud minority (the able-and-uninterested stay quiet and leave without explaining). This is **edge case 167a:** when you cannot find the audience's true priority, ask the one client directly (personal mode) or survey the room / study past quiet non-buyers (general mode). **Never guess the priority and build on the guess.**

---

## 3. REMEDIATION — what to do about Price

- **If price is genuinely too high for the value communicated:** do **not** simply cut it. Raise the **perceived value** first with a high true-value anchor, then use the **value drop** (SOP-PITCH-01/02, offer-price-strategist) so each reduction is paired with **added value.**
- **If price is too low:** recognize that a low price can **signal low value** and keep the thing at the bottom of the stack. The fix is often to anchor the value higher, **not** to drop further.
- **Never cut price in response to a positioning failure.** It trains the audience that the thing was overpriced and still does not move it up the stack. (offer-price-strategist Gate 6.)

> "For every time we do a drop in price, you have to do an increase in value."
> - Trevor Otts, the governing rule of the value drop

**Example.** A presenter whose offer is $200 assumes the price is too high because no one buys. But if a prospect cannot find $200 for a thing they claim to want, **they never prioritized it.** Price is eliminated; dropping to $100 changes nothing. The gap is **positioning.**

**Failure mode.** Cutting price to solve a problem that price did not cause.

## 4. THE EXPECTATION REFRAME (Part 8.6 — the heart of objection-handling)

> "This is not a question of, is this thing worth it? It's worth hundreds of thousands of dollars. The price is just what you invested. Because where there's no investment, there's no appreciation."
> - Trevor Otts

> "The problem is not the price; the problem is your expectation. You expect less out of yourself, and because you do, you invest little in yourself. You have to invest in yourself at the level at which you expect results."
> - Trevor Otts

**Reframe the objection as an expectation, not a cost.** The objection "it costs too much" is reframed to "the problem is your expectation of yourself." This is the canonical proclamation of this SOP (see SOP-PROCLAMATION-01 — bold, specific, hedge-free):
> *"The problem is not the price. The problem is your expectation. You expect less of yourself, so you invest little in yourself."*

The **closing identity pivot** (co-owned with SOP-PERSON-00) is a set of identity questions: *do I have something worth telling; have I been capitalizing on what I know; have I turned my experience into a return?* If the honest answer is no, the move is to **say yes to oneself.**

**On any cost objection, return to the vision** (SOP-VISION-01 §6). If the objection is about cost, the value and the vision were not made vivid enough — so the answer is to return them to the vision, **not** to the price.

## 5. THE RIGHT QUESTION TO WIN (P40)

> "Once they decide that something is a priority, they'll move heaven and earth to get it done... They'll beg, borrow, cheat, and steal."
> - Trevor Otts

"Is it worth the price" is the **wrong** question to win. A life-changing offer is worth far more than its price; the price is merely what they invested. **The real question the presentation must answer is: "is this more important than what I am currently prioritizing?"** (Doctrine root: SOP-NORTHSTAR-00, the priority shift.)

---

## 6. DO NOT MISDIAGNOSE A PRIORITY PROBLEM AS A PRICE PROBLEM (P163)

> "Part of that sale is making them mentally reprioritize what they want to do financially."
> - Trevor Otts

Broke people buy constantly; the loudest voices are usually the least-able buyers, while the able-and-uninterested stay quiet. Reacting to the loud minority by cutting price **fixes a problem price never caused** and trains the audience to value the thing less. The diagnostic discipline: localize Price by elimination (SOP-PRIORITY-01), and if interested people stay and still do not act, the problem is **Position/Priority, not Price.**

---

## 7. THE PAY-TO-APPOINTMENT LADDER (P165, links to SOP-PHILOSOPHY-00)

A low-cost **paid appointment** can be the natural on-ramp into high-ticket services, using the same value-drop and positioning logic. The academic instinct to price a single early appointment **high** (out of pride in the information) tends to backfire — **strangers will not prioritize a high-cost appointment with someone they do not yet know and value.** Master the low-ticket on-ramp first (SOP-PHILOSOPHY-00 §master-low-then-high), then build high-ticket capacity.
