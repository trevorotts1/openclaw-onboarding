## 1. THE RULE — Person has two faces

The Person P is **the presenter, and how the presenter positions themselves.** It has two faces:
1. **As a diagnostic** — is the presenter the reason the presentation is not working?
2. **As a construction element** — the deliberate self-positioning that gives the presenter standing to move an audience.

Person is **the hardest P to see from the inside**, because it requires honesty about one's own performance. Nothing in the presentation looks obviously wrong; the presenter simply was the problem. This SOP makes the diagnosis explicit and repeatable so it can be run honestly instead of avoided.

---

## 2. THE ACADEMIC DRIFT (P5) — the root failure mode of the knowledgeable presenter

A presenter who is academic by nature drifts toward the **activity** (teaching, demonstrating intelligence) and away from the **purpose** (the priority shift). People with the most knowledge have the **strongest** version of this instinct: they vomit everything they know over the audience and try to teach all the way to a sale.

> "You can't teach people to a sale."
> - Trevor Otts, quoting Linda Clemens

The result is the **opposite** of buying: an overwhelmed, brain-burned-out audience, and **an overwhelmed mind cannot prioritize.** Teaching to a sale is the single most common way a knowledgeable presenter sabotages their own true goal. The teaching is the **showcase, never the driver** (doctrine root: SOP-NORTHSTAR-00).

---

## 3. THE DIAGNOSTIC — "I always know when it's me"

> "I always know when it's me. When I try to teach to the sale. If I try to teach to the sale, we're gonna have no sale."
> - Trevor Otts, on the single clearest sign the presenter is the problem

> "Sometimes it's the person who dilly-dallied around on making the offer. They undersold. It's the confidence with which they delivered the product to the person."
> - Trevor Otts

**The Person diagnostic — ask these honestly:**
1. **Did I teach to the sale instead of selling?** Did I spend the presentation demonstrating knowledge rather than moving the audience to a decision? *This is the number-one tell.*
2. Did I **dilly-dally** on making the offer — delay it, or bury it?
3. Did I **undersell** — soften or apologize for the offer or the price?
4. Did I **clearly believe** in the thing, or did doubt leak through?
5. Did I want to **be heard** more than I wanted the outcome?

> "They want to be heard more than they want to sell. And it's okay, because when you are in initial stages, you do want to be heard, because you just need the confidence that comes along with being heard."
> - Trevor Otts, on the forgivable early-stage version of the Person problem

---

## 4. REMEDIATION — match the fix to the specific tell

| Tell | Fix |
|---|---|
| **Taught to the sale** | Cut the teaching down to a **showcase teaser**; move the energy to the priority shift and the offer. The teaching exists to prove you are the right person, not to educate. Reread SOP-NORTHSTAR-00. |
| **Dilly-dallied on the offer** | Make the offer **cleanly and on time**, with a structure that forces the offer to appear at the right moment. Use the build sequence (SOP-PRIORITY-02) so the close is not left to nerve. |
| **Undersold** | Anchor a **high true value** and let the value drop do the work (SOP-OBJECTION-01 / offer-price-strategist), so you never have to apologize for the price. |
| **Did not believe in it** | **Fix the belief before the next presentation.** A presenter cannot transfer conviction they do not have. If the doubt is about the product, resolve the product question first. |
| **Wanted to be heard** | Recognize this as a **confidence phase**; give yourself permission to pass through it, then deliberately graduate out of it by practicing **selling**, not just speaking. High-ticket comfort is a habit built through repetition, not a talent. |

## 5. SELF-POSITIONING — position yourself as a priority (P46, P99–P101)

Beyond fixing performance, the presenter must position themselves so they are **treated as a priority.** This is the commitment-and-consistency lever (P46) turned inward: claim the identity, and prioritizing yourself becomes consistent with it.

> "I need to work with people who see me as a priority. People who don't see me as a priority are not my ideal customer, my ideal friend, my ideal client, my ideal nothing."
> - Trevor Otts

> "I'm not your second option. I'm your first option."
> - Trevor Otts

**Self-positioning moves:**
- Hold the internal stance that you **work with people who prioritize you.** Let go of the posture of being grateful to be in a room with people who undervalue you but still want what you offer.
- Take the position of **first option, not second.**
- **Strengthen your own language** about what you want and do not want, with specificity — because a generic internal stance produces a generic external presence, and the audience reads it instantly and **mirrors it back as a lack of prioritization.**

---

## 6. THE CLOSING IDENTITY PIVOT (P139) — Person meets the objection

The closing pivot is a set of **identity questions** the presenter brings the audience to (and must hold for themselves first): *do I have something worth telling; have I been capitalizing on what I know; have I turned my experience into something that generates a return?* If the honest answer is no, the move is to **say yes to oneself.** This is where Person and objection-handling meet — the identity claim is what makes the re-rank stick. See SOP-OBJECTION-01 §expectation-reframe; the two SOPs co-own the close.

---

## 7. EXAMPLE + FAILURE MODE

**Example.** A presenter with deep subject expertise gives a brilliant, information-dense webinar and sells nothing. Product proven, price fair. Diagnosis: **Person — they taught to the sale.** The remediation is not more content; it is **less.** Cut the lecture to one demonstration, add a clear promise and a priority shift, make the offer cleanly with conviction → the next session converts.

**Failure mode.** Teaching instead of selling, hedging instead of declaring, or carrying an internal posture of being the **second option**, which the audience reads and mirrors back.

---

## 8. EDGE CASES THIS SOP OWNS

- **167f — the presenter wants to "just teach":** explain the true-goal principle (SOP-NORTHSTAR-00). If it is a genuine pure training with no action goal, **still engineer a priority shift toward applying the knowledge** — a training that changes nothing has failed even when every fact is correct.
- **167g — the presenter is the problem but cannot see it:** surface it **plainly but kindly**, naming the specific tell (most often teaching to the sale or a delayed offer), and offer the matched fix from §4. **Do not soften it into vagueness** — a generic note will not change the behavior. (Co-handled with devils-advocate-presentations and the presenter-coach Rehearsal Gate.)
- **P136 — training trigger:** in a training, the Person must deliver an explicit, **time-bound** instruction to take the first action immediately, while energy is high. Verified at the Rehearsal Gate.

## 9. PRODUCING-ROLE STEPS

**Presenter-Coach (Rehearsal Gate, SOP 9.4):**
1. Run the §3 diagnostic against the rehearsal: confirm the offer is made cleanly and on time, the deck is not taught-to-the-sale, the presenter is not underselling, and the time-bound trigger is delivered aloud.
2. On any tell, deliver the matched §4 fix by name (never a vague note).

**Presenters-Speech-Writer:**
1. Write the self-positioning (§5) into the spoken script — first-option language, the identity pivot — so conviction is scripted, not left to nerve.

**Attention-Content-Strategist:**
1. Record the Person read and the chosen self-positioning stance in `priority_shift_spec.json` so downstream roles build to it.
