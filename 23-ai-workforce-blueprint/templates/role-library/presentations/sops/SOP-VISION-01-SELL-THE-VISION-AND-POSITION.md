## 1. THE RULE (the job description of every presenter)

> "Presentations are about selling the vision."
> "Your job is to sell the vision and position the product."
> - Trevor Otts

A presentation is **not** an information session and **not** a feature tour. A presentation is the act of presenting a vision. The presenter's job has exactly **two halves, and both are mandatory**:

1. **Sell the vision** — transfer the picture of the audience's transformed life, business, or future that the presenter can already see and the audience cannot yet see for themselves.
2. **Position the product** — place the offer or idea as the **prioritized vehicle** that delivers that vision.

Do one without the other and the deck fails in a predictable way:
- **Vision without positioning** → the audience leaves inspired and buys nothing.
- **Positioning without vision** → you are pushing features at people who do not yet want where the product leads.

This SOP governs both halves. It is the engine that the North Star (SOP-NORTHSTAR-00) runs on: **a vividly seen vision is what makes the audience want the destination badly enough to re-rank the offer to number one.** Vision-selling is therefore the primary content mechanism by which attention is converted into the priority shift.

---

## 2. WHAT THE VISION IS

> "I must help them see what I see for them."
> - Trevor Otts, defining the job of the presenter

Said in full: **I, the presenter, must help them, the audience, see what I see for them.** The audience arrived unable to see the future the presenter sees for them — who they could become, what their story could build, what their life could look like on the other side of this decision. The presenter can see it. The entire presentation exists to move that picture from the presenter's mind into theirs until they can see it as clearly as the presenter does.

When they can see it, they want it. When they want it, **the priority shift happens almost on its own**, because a person will always prioritize a vision they can vividly see for themselves.

**The vision is the destination; the product is the vehicle.** The two are inseparable. This is why Part 0.2 of the framework comes before positioning in Part 5.3: you cannot position a product toward a destination the audience does not yet want. Positioning toward an unwanted destination is just feature-pushing.

---

## 3. THE LIGHTNING ROD (why positioning is the master lever)

Positioning is about being in exactly the right place at exactly the right time — the way a lightning rod must be.

- The **vision you have transferred** is the charge building in the air.
- Your **positioning** is the lightning rod.
- When the rod is in the right position at the right time, the vision and the offer connect, and the powerful moment — the decision — is created.
- Out of position, the same vision and the same offer produce **nothing**: the lightning is never caught, the electricity is never created, the moment never happens.

This is why **Position is the master lever** — the one P responsible for shifting priorities (Product, Price, and Person do not do that job). And it is why positioning must be engineered with precision, not left to instinct. See SOP-PRIORITY-01 §Position for the diagnostic; this SOP supplies the constructive doctrine.

## 4. THE VISION-AND-POSITION BUILD (Part 5.3 Moves 1–2, the constructive moves this SOP owns)

The full eight-move build sequence lives in SOP-PRIORITY-02. This SOP owns and deepens its **first two positioning moves** — the vision-selling half — which every deck must execute before any feature, benefit, price, or ask.

### Move 1 — Sell the vision first
Before any feature, benefit, or price, transfer the picture of the transformed future the audience cannot yet see for themselves.
- **Personal mode (1:1):** paint the vision of that one client's specific future.
- **General mode (1:many):** paint the shared future the whole room wants.
- This is the engine of the priority shift. It sells the *destination*; positioning then makes the offer the prioritized *vehicle* to it.
- **Placement:** the vision is opened early (the Hook opens on it — see hook-strategist) and is the thing the audience is *returned to* on any cost objection (§6).

### Move 2 — Speak clearly to the value and the benefit
The single most common positioning failure is a value communicated in a way that is **too complex to grasp.**

> "Zero Human Company is a really good product. But not enough people are buying it because the explanation of what it is is too complex. How it would benefit them is too complex."
> - Trevor Otts, diagnosing a positioning failure on his own product (the canonical complexity-failure example)

The fix: state, **in plain language a tired person could understand**, exactly what the thing is and exactly how it benefits them. If the audience has to *work* to understand the benefit, the benefit does not exist for them.

- This is enforced downstream by **AF-C8** (density ceiling) and **AF-C6** (one-big-idea): complexity on a slide is a defect, not a style.
- The law that governs the ordering: **value must be seen before price is ever named** (price only matters in the absence of value — see SOP-OBJECTION-01). Vision and value come first; the number comes after.

## 5. POSITIONING IS NOT DESCRIBING (the failure mode)

**Positioning is not describing the product or listing its features.** It is placing the product as the prioritized vehicle to the vision the audience now wants.

The signature of a positioning failure: the product is fine, the price is fine, the presenter showed up, interested people stayed, engaged, even asked for payment plans — **and still no one acts.** That pattern means they liked it but did not prioritize it. (Diagnosis-by-elimination lives in SOP-PRIORITY-01; this SOP's job is to prevent it by construction.)

**FAILURE MODE for this SOP:** speaking clearly to features and information while never speaking to priority — so the audience understands the thing perfectly and still ranks it last.

---

## 6. ON A COST OBJECTION, RETURN TO THE VISION (Part 8.6)

When the objection is about cost, **the value and the vision were not made vivid enough.** The answer is to return the audience to the vision, **never** to drop to the price.

> "This is not a question of, is this thing worth it? It's worth hundreds of thousands of dollars. The price is just what you invested."
> - Trevor Otts

Underneath every price objection sits the same law: **price only matters in the absence of value.** The full objection-handling doctrine lives in SOP-OBJECTION-01; this SOP records the rule that the *destination* of every cost objection is the vision, because the vision is what makes the price look small next to it.

---

## 7. WORKED EXAMPLE (the 90-minute membership)

A membership presentation runs a full ninety minutes. Attendees stay the whole time and ask for payment plans afterward, then nobody enrolls. Product proven, price low, presenter engaging. The diagnosis is **positioning.** The specific gaps: the presenter had stripped urgency out when simplifying the pricing, **never connected the membership to the attendees' deeper goal (no vision sold)**, and explained the value in insider language (complexity failure, Move 2). Rebuilt with a clear plain-language benefit statement, a higher-priority connection (the vision sold), real scarcity and a time-bound close, and a value-stacked layer drop, **the same presentation converts.**

## 8. PRODUCING-ROLE STEPS

**Attention-Content-Strategist (P0B-PRIORITY):**
1. In `priority_shift_spec.json`, record the `the_vision` field: the one-sentence picture of the transformed future, stated as "they can see [specific future] for themselves."
2. Record `higher_priority_hook` — the existing top-of-stack priority (legacy, family, freedom, purpose, identity) the vision attaches to.
3. Confirm the product is framed as the **vehicle to** that vision, not as a feature set.

**Slide Copywriter / Hook-Strategist:**
1. Open the deck on the vision (the Hook sells the destination, not the product).
2. Execute Move 2: state the value in plain language a tired person could understand; never insider language.
3. Keep the vision the spine the deck returns to on the cost beat.

**Attention Designer / Slide Image Creator:**
1. Render the vision as the single most vivid thing in the room — the imagery IS how the audience "sees what the presenter sees" (von Restorff; SOP-NORTHSTAR-00). A vision the audience cannot picture has not been sold.
