## Style / Definition
The Humanistic buyer decides through **people, relationships, and feelings.** Their real questions are never on the spec sheet: *Who's behind this? Who else is in it? Will I belong? Do they actually care?* In the 12-email sequence they own **Emails 7–9: E7 = PASTOR (Story), E8 = Star-Story-Solution, E9 = PAS.**

## Purpose & Intention
Humanistic buyers can't be argued into a purchase — they must be *welcomed* into one. Data bounces off them; stories land. Their three-email block is built entirely from people-shaped persuasion: a full narrative arc (E7), a relatable hero's case study (E8), and empathetic pain resonance (E9). Won properly, this buyer becomes your community's warmest member and your most natural referrer — they don't recommend products, they introduce *people*.

## How To Write It (voice & tactics)
1. **Write as one human to one human.** First person, warm, specific. This is the segment where corporate polish actively hurts — a slightly imperfect, personal voice outperforms a flawless brand voice.
2. **Lead with people, never products.** Every concept arrives wearing a face: a member's story, the founder's moment of doubt, the community's inside joke. The product is what the people are gathered around.
3. **Show you see THEM.** Name their situation with real empathy before offering anything. Feeling understood is this buyer's conversion event.
4. **Make belonging tangible.** Who they'll meet, how members talk to each other, what it feels like to arrive. "You'll fit here" is the promise under every winning line.
5. **Use social proof as connection, not scoreboard.** "Meet Dana, who started exactly where you are" beats "10,000 customers served."
6. **Invite, don't push.** CTAs framed as welcome: *join us, save your seat at the table, come meet everyone.* Pressure reads as proof you don't care.
7. **Honor the relationship in every send.** Reference the journey so far; never act like a stranger with a pitch.

### Subject-Line Guidance (Humanistic)
- **People and names in the subject:** *"{{contact.first_name}}, meet the member who almost quit"*
- **Warmth and story cues:** *the story behind…, what she told me…, a letter for you*
- **Personal address matters most here** — `{{contact.first_name}}` reads as genuine greeting, not merge-field trickery
- **Feeling-words over power-words:** home, together, finally, belong
- **Avoid aggressive urgency/scarcity** — it breaks the relational frame
- Standard rules apply: 2 A/B subject lines, 8–12 words, key info in first 40 chars, no pricing

## Use Cases
- Emails 7–9 of any 12-email buyer-type sequence
- Coaching, community, and membership offers
- Service businesses where the buyer is choosing a *person* to trust
- Founder-story campaigns and behind-the-scenes sends
- Win-back emails that repair the relationship before re-offering

## Detailed Example
**Framework:** PAS (Email 9, Humanistic slot)

**Subject Line A:** {{contact.first_name}}, the loneliest part nobody talks about
**Subject Line B:** You're not behind, {{contact.first_name}}. You're alone. Different problem.

**Preview Text:** The part of building alone that data never captures — and the door out.

**Email Body:**

{{contact.first_name}},

Can I name something? The hardest part of what you're building isn't the strategy. **It's carrying every decision alone** — no one to gut-check the big call, celebrate the quiet win, or tell you you're not crazy.

Left alone, that isolation compounds. Decisions get heavier. Wins feel smaller because no one saw them. Slowly, the work you loved starts feeling like solitary confinement with a login page.

It doesn't have to be like that — and honestly, it was never supposed to be.

**The Circle** is the room we built for exactly this: 200 builders at your stage, weekly small-group calls, and a place where "can I get eyes on this?" gets answered within the hour.

**[Come meet everyone — join The Circle →]**

Dana, who you met in the last email, says the community turned out to be the feature she didn't know she was buying. Most members say some version of that.

There's a seat at the table with your name on it, {{contact.first_name}}.

[Founder Name]

## Quality Checklist
- [ ] Voice is one-human-to-one-human — no corporate polish
- [ ] A person (member/founder/character) appears before the product does
- [ ] Reader's situation named with genuine empathy
- [ ] Belonging made tangible (who they'll meet, how it feels)
- [ ] Social proof framed as connection, not numbers
- [ ] CTA is an invitation (join/come/meet), not pressure
- [ ] Continuity honored — references the relationship/journey so far
- [ ] Subject lines carry names, warmth, or story cues; no aggressive scarcity
- [ ] Framework matches slot (E7 PASTOR-Story, E8 Star-Story-Solution, E9 PAS)
- [ ] Founder's actual name in the signature
