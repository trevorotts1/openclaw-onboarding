## Style / Definition
The cold-outreach variants adapt **Star-Chain-Hook** (proof-led) and **Star-Story-Solution** (story-led) for readers with **zero prior relationship**. Same skeletons as the parent frameworks (see their entries), recalibrated for the cold context: shorter, personalized in line one, and aimed at earning a **reply** — not a purchase.

## Purpose & Intention
Cold email is a different sport: you haven't earned attention, trust, or even recognition. These variants exist so the library's two most credibility-dense frameworks can operate in that hostile environment. The strategic shift: the parent frameworks close sales; the cold variants **open conversations.** Choose Star-Chain-Hook when your proof is unusually strong; choose Star-Story-Solution when a peer's story will lower defenses faster than credentials.

## How To Write It
1. **Compress everything.** Cold readers give you ~5 seconds. Both variants should land under 120 words — roughly half the warm-email length.
2. **The Star must be about THEM.** Warm emails can open with your big idea; cold emails must open with *their* world — a specific, researched observation about their business ("Saw you just expanded to a second location…"). Personalization IS the pattern interrupt.
3. **Star-Chain-Hook cold: shrink the chain to 2–3 links maximum,** each independently verifiable, at least one external (not your own claim). Unverifiable claims from a stranger read as spam.
4. **Star-Story-Solution cold: make the Star a near-exact peer** of the recipient — same industry, same size, same problem. Cold readers extend zero benefit of the doubt to loose analogies.
5. **Hook/Solution = micro-commitment, never purchase.** Ask for a reply, a yes/no, a 10-minute look. "Worth a look?" outperforms "Book a call" from a stranger.
6. **Zero decoration, one link maximum, no image blocks.** Deliverability and skimmability rule cold inboxes.
7. **Write the P.S. as a pressure release.** One line that makes opting out easy ("If this isn't relevant, tell me and I'll close the file") — paradoxically lifts reply rates.

## Use Cases
- First-touch B2B prospecting to researched targets
- Partnership and collaboration outreach
- Reviving completely cold segments (>12 months dormant — treat as strangers)
- Local-business outreach where one peer case study exists
- Any send where the recipient has never heard of the sender

## Detailed Example
**Framework:** Star-Chain-Hook (Cold-Outreach Variant)

**Subject Line A:** {{contact.first_name}} — a number about second locations
**Subject Line B:** Quick observation about your expansion, {{contact.first_name}}

**Preview Text:** Two verifiable facts and a yes/no question. 30 seconds, promise.

**Email Body:**

{{contact.first_name}},

Congrats on opening the second location — saw the announcement last week.

Here's the number that made me write: **industry data shows 60% of second locations underperform the first for their entire first year,** and the cause is almost always systems that lived in the owner's head at location one.

We've documented the fix across 40+ multi-location operators — the average one gets location two to parity in under five months.

Worth a 10-minute look at how? A simple "yes" here is the whole next step.

[Founder Name]

**P.S.** If the expansion's already running smoothly, tell me and I'll happily close the file.

*(Signature rule: replace [Founder Name] with the founder's actual name at generation time.)*

## Quality Checklist
- [ ] Under 120 words
- [ ] Line one contains a specific, researched observation about the recipient
- [ ] Chain ≤3 links (or Star is a near-exact peer), all verifiable
- [ ] Ask is a micro-commitment (reply/yes-no), never a purchase
- [ ] One link maximum; no images; nothing that trips spam filters
- [ ] P.S. gives an easy, graceful out
- [ ] Tone: useful colleague, not eager vendor
- [ ] Would YOU reply to this if it arrived from a stranger?
- [ ] Two A/B subject lines; preview <100 chars
- [ ] Founder's actual name in the signature
