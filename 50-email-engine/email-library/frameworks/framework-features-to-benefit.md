## Style / Definition
Features-to-Benefit links every product feature to a concrete customer benefit using the chain: **"This [FEATURE] means you'll [BENEFIT], which allows you to [DEEPER BENEFIT]."** Structure: Intro → feature→benefit pairs → summary → CTA. It converts a spec sheet into a stack of personal outcomes.

## Purpose & Intention
Buyers never purchase features — they purchase what features *do for them*. This framework wins when the offer is rich in deliverables but the reader hasn't yet felt why those deliverables matter. It is the antidote to the most common email failure: listing what's in the box instead of what's in it *for the reader*. Ideal for methodical buyers who want substance, and for Email 4 of the Landing-Page 10-email promo set (leveraging landing-page sections 8, 9 & 10).

## How To Write It
1. **Choose 3 features maximum.** More than three dilutes; pick the three that map to the reader's loudest desires, not the three you're proudest of.
2. **Run the "so what?" chain twice per feature.** Feature → "so what?" → functional benefit → "so what?" → emotional or identity-level payoff. Never stop at the first answer. ("Weekly live calls" → get unstuck fast → never lose a week of momentum to a question nobody answered.)
3. **Open with the reader's outcome, not the product.** One or two sentences that name the result they're chasing — the features become evidence for that promise.
4. **Order pairs by descending desire.** Lead with the feature tied to the benefit they want most; the reader may not read past pair one.
5. **Bold the feature, land on the benefit.** The bolded feature is the skimmer's anchor; the sentence must *end* on the deeper benefit so the payoff is the last thing felt.
6. **Summarize in benefits only.** The recap paragraph stacks the three payoffs together — no feature language allowed in the summary.
7. **One CTA, on its own line.** Frame the click as claiming the benefits, not "learning more."

## Use Cases
- Email 4 of the Landing-Page 10-email promo sequence (built from landing-page sections 8, 9 & 10)
- Methodical-buyer slot (Email 3) in the 12-email buyer-type sequence
- Product or program launches where the deliverable stack is a genuine differentiator
- Upsell emails proving the upgrade tier's components are worth it
- Re-engagement emails for subscribers who know the product exists but never connected it to their life

## Detailed Example
**Framework:** Features-to-Benefit

**Subject Line A:** {{contact.first_name}}, 3 things inside that do the heavy lifting
**Subject Line B:** The template vault alone is worth the click, {{contact.first_name}}

**Preview Text:** Each one was built to remove a specific bottleneck. Here's exactly how.

**Email Body:**

{{contact.first_name}},

You don't need more information. You need the specific pieces that remove the bottlenecks between you and consistent results — so let me show you exactly what's inside and why each piece matters.

**Weekly live coaching calls** mean you never sit on a problem for more than seven days, which allows you to keep momentum in the exact weeks most people quietly quit.

**The plug-and-play template vault** means you skip the blank page entirely, which allows you to ship in an afternoon what used to eat an entire month.

**The private member community** means someone two steps ahead of you is a message away, which allows you to borrow proof that this works every single time doubt shows up.

Put those together and here's what you actually get: momentum that doesn't stall, output without the agonizing, and belief on demand.

That's not a feature list. That's a different working life.

## Quality Checklist
- [ ] No more than 3 features featured
- [ ] Every feature completes the full chain: feature → benefit → deeper benefit
- [ ] Each pair sentence *ends* on the payoff, not the feature
- [ ] Opening names the reader's desired outcome before any feature appears
- [ ] Features ordered by descending reader desire
- [ ] Summary paragraph is 100% benefit language (zero feature words)
- [ ] Single CTA on its own line, framed as claiming the outcome
- [ ] 150–300 words; paragraphs ≤3 sentences; key points bolded
- [ ] Two A/B subject lines (8–12 words, hook in first 40 chars, `{{contact.first_name}}` where possible); preview text <100 chars, doesn't repeat subject
- [ ] Founder's actual name in the signature
