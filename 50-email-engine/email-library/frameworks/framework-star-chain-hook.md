## Style / Definition
Star-Chain-Hook is a three-part credibility engine: **Star (a big, attention-grabbing idea) → Chain (a linked run of facts, sources, reasons, and benefits that build credibility) → Hook (the CTA).** The **Chain is the key element** — a sequence of proof points, each strong enough to hold, that drags the reader from curiosity to conviction.

## Purpose & Intention
Some readers are attracted by boldness but only convinced by evidence. Star-Chain-Hook serves exactly them: the Star earns the open-minded read, the Chain earns belief link by link, and the Hook converts the accumulated weight into action. It's the Email 2 slot for spontaneous buyers (bold enough to hold their attention, substantive enough to upgrade their gut-yes into confidence) and a top performer for cold and skeptical audiences.

## How To Write It
1. **Make the Star genuinely big — and true.** A claim, statistic, or idea the reader hasn't heard that reframes their situation ("The average founder loses a full workday every week to tools that were supposed to save time."). If the Star wouldn't stop *you* mid-scroll, it's not a Star.
2. **Build the Chain from mixed link types.** Alternate: hard fact → external source → logical reason → concrete benefit. Homogeneous chains (all stats, or all benefits) feel like either a lecture or a pitch; alternation feels like a case.
3. **Each link one line where possible, each able to stand alone.** A chain is only as strong as its weakest link — cut any link you can't back up. Three to five strong links beats eight soft ones.
4. **Sequence links from external to personal.** Start with the industry-wide fact, end with the benefit in the reader's own week. The chain should travel from "the world" to "your Monday."
5. **Let the Hook cash the chain.** By the CTA, action should feel like the logical conclusion of the evidence: "Given all that — here's the next step." One clear hook, own line.
6. **Don't decorate.** This framework wins on density. Every sentence either grabs, proves, or hooks.

## Use Cases
- Email 2 (spontaneous block) in the 12-email buyer-type sequence
- Cold outreach where credibility must be built from zero (see cold-outreach variants entry)
- B2B and analytical-adjacent audiences who distrust hype
- Product claims that sound too good without proof
- Second-touch emails after a bold first email raised eyebrows

## Detailed Example

**Subject Line A:** {{contact.first_name}}, you're losing a full workday every week

Every link above holds. Which leaves one honest next step:

## Quality Checklist
- [ ] Star is big, specific, and true — would stop the writer mid-scroll
- [ ] Chain has 3–5 links, mixing facts / sources / reasons / benefits
- [ ] Every link is verifiable — nothing you couldn't defend if challenged
- [ ] Links travel from external (world) to personal (reader's week)
- [ ] Each link visually distinct (own line, bolded label)
- [ ] Hook reads as the logical conclusion of the chain
- [ ] Zero decorative sentences — every line grabs, proves, or hooks
- [ ] 150–300 words; paragraphs ≤3 sentences
- [ ] Two A/B subject lines (hook in first 40 chars); preview <100 chars
- [ ] Founder's actual name in the signature
