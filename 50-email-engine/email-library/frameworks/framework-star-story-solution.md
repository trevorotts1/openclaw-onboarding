## Style / Definition
Star-Story-Solution is character-driven persuasion: **Star (introduce a main character) → Story (the character faces the reader's exact problem) → Solution (how the character wins — with your product as the means).** It runs on **narrative transportation**: readers absorbed in a story adopt its conclusions as their own.

## Purpose & Intention
When a reader watches someone-like-them struggle and win, the sale happens by identification instead of argument — "if it worked for her, it can work for me" is a conclusion the reader *reaches*, which is a hundred times stronger than a claim they're *told*. It differs from PASTOR-Story in scale: this is a tight, single-character case-study arc, not a full six-part architecture. It holds the Email 8 slot (humanistic block) of the 12-email sequence and adapts superbly to cold outreach.

## How To Write It
1. **Cast a Star at the reader's level — or one step behind.** The reader must think "that's me" (or "that was me last year"). A Star too far ahead inspires distance, not identification. Give them one humanizing specific: a name, a detail, a habit.
2. **Give the Star the reader's exact problem, including the failed attempts.** What the character already tried (and why it didn't work) silently dismantles the reader's "I've tried things before" objection.
3. **Include the doubt.** One line of the Star's skepticism before the turn ("she almost didn't sign up") — pre-agreeing with the reader's own hesitation makes the win believable.
4. **Let the Solution arrive through the Star's action, not your narration.** "She ran the first play in the system" beats "our system provides." The character stays the actor; the product stays the instrument.
5. **Quantify the win, humanize the meaning.** One number (revenue, hours, clients) plus one human moment (what she did with it). Numbers convince; moments convert.
6. **Bridge to the reader in one sentence, then CTA.** "Her starting point was your current one." Then the invitation to begin the same arc.

## Use Cases
- Email 8 (humanistic block) in the 12-email buyer-type sequence
- Case-study and member-spotlight campaigns
- Cold outreach where a peer's story lowers defenses faster than credentials (see cold-outreach variants entry)
- Objection-handling emails — cast a Star who held the exact objection
- Testimonial amplification: turning one great client result into a full email

## Detailed Example
**Framework:** Star-Story-Solution

**Subject Line A:** {{contact.first_name}}, meet the founder who almost said no
**Subject Line B:** Her third failed launch changed everything, {{contact.first_name}}

**Preview Text:** Same starting point as yours. Very different ending. 60-second read.

**Email Body:**

{{contact.first_name}},

Marcus ran a small consulting shop with a habit you might recognize: every January he'd plan the year that would finally be different, and every December he'd wonder what happened.

**He'd tried the courses. The planners. The 5am club.** Each one worked for about three weeks — because none of them touched the actual problem, which was that his business had no repeatable way to create demand.

When he found the Demand Engine program, his first reaction was the same as yours might be right now: *"I've bought things like this before."*

He joined anyway — mostly because the first module was a diagnostic, not a pep talk.

That diagnostic found it in week one: he was reinventing his outreach from scratch every month. He installed the one repeatable sequence the program is built around, ran it without changing anything else, and **booked 14 qualified calls in the next 30 days — more than the previous five months combined.**

The number mattered less than the Friday he closed his laptop at 3pm because the pipeline was already full.

Marcus's starting point was your current one, {{contact.first_name}}. The arc is available.

**[Start with the same diagnostic Marcus ran →]**

[Founder Name]

## Quality Checklist
- [ ] Star is at the reader's level (or one step behind) with one humanizing specific
- [ ] Story includes the Star's failed prior attempts
- [ ] Star's own doubt/skepticism voiced before the turn
- [ ] Solution arrives through the Star's action — product stays the instrument
- [ ] Win is quantified (one number) AND humanized (one moment)
- [ ] One-sentence bridge connects Star's start to reader's present
- [ ] Reader could summarize the story in one line after a single read
- [ ] 150–300 words; paragraphs ≤3 sentences; key beats bolded
- [ ] Two A/B subject lines; preview <100 chars, no subject repeat
- [ ] Founder's actual name in the signature
