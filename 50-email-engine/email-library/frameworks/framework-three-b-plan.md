## Style / Definition
The 3-B Plan is ruthless compression: **Brevity, Bluntness, Basic.** Short, blunt, simple — get to the point and the incentive fast. Under ~150 words, no warm-up, no cleverness tax. Built for busy, spontaneous buyers who decide in seconds.

## Purpose & Intention
Attention is the scarcest asset in the inbox, and some readers punish anything that wastes it. The 3-B Plan converts by *respect*: it treats the reader like someone with somewhere to be. It opens the entire 12-email buyer-type sequence (Email 1, spontaneous) precisely because a short, generous first email earns the right to send eleven more. It is also the mandated exception in the Landing-Page promo set — Email 7 runs 3-B and is deliberately shorter than the 150–300-word standard.

## How To Write It
1. **Lead with the point — first line, no exceptions.** The offer or incentive belongs in sentence one. If your first sentence is context, delete it and promote sentence two.
2. **One idea, total.** One offer, one benefit, one CTA. The moment a second idea appears, you're writing a different framework.
3. **Blunt ≠ cold.** Keep one beat of warmth or wit ("No essay today — just this"). Bluntness is about pace, not personality.
4. **Basic words, short sentences.** Nothing a 12-year-old couldn't read aloud. Cut every adjective that isn't doing sales work.
5. **Make the incentive concrete and immediate.** "40% off until midnight" beats "special savings for a limited time." Specificity is what spontaneous buyers act on.
6. **CTA within the first 60 words.** Then stop. The discipline to end early IS the framework.
7. **Word-count gate: under 150 — aim for under 100.** Count them. This rule is structural, not stylistic.

## Use Cases
- Email 1 — the opener — of the 12-email buyer-type sequence (spontaneous)
- Email 7 of the Landing-Page 10-email promo sequence (the mandated short email)
- Flash sales, deadline-day reminders, last-call sends
- Abandoned-cart first touch (within 1–3 hours, when speed beats persuasion)
- Pattern interrupt inside a long sequence — a one-breath email after several long ones

## Detailed Example
**Framework:** The 3-B Plan

**Subject Line A:** {{contact.first_name}} — 40% off. Midnight. That's the email.
**Subject Line B:** No essay today, {{contact.first_name}}. Just this.

**Preview Text:** One line, one offer, one deadline. You'll be done in 15 seconds.

**Email Body:**

{{contact.first_name}},

Straight to it: **the full Toolkit is 40% off until midnight tonight.**

Every template, every checklist, every system — the same package members use to win back 8+ hours a week.

**[Grab it at 40% off →]**

At 12:01am the price goes back up and stays there.

That's the whole email. Told you it'd be quick.

[Founder Name]

*(Signature rule: replace [Founder Name] with the founder's actual name at generation time.)*

*(Word count: 66 — well under the 150 ceiling.)*

## Quality Checklist
- [ ] Under 150 words — counted, not estimated (under 100 preferred)
- [ ] The point/incentive appears in the first sentence
- [ ] Exactly one idea, one offer, one CTA
- [ ] CTA appears within the first 60 words
- [ ] Incentive is concrete (number + deadline), not vague
- [ ] One beat of warmth or wit — blunt, not cold
- [ ] No sentence a busy reader would skip
- [ ] Reads clean on a phone screen without scrolling past the CTA
- [ ] Two A/B subject lines, as short and blunt as the body; preview <100 chars
- [ ] Founder's actual name in the signature
