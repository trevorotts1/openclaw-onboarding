## Style / Definition
The Abandoned Cart objective recovers a purchase the buyer already started, using five triggers: **loss aversion, cognitive-dissonance resolution, the Zeigarnik effect (unfinished tasks demand completion), trust/risk reduction, and reciprocity.** Hard timing rule: **the first email sends within 1–3 hours** of abandonment.

## Purpose & Intention
An abandoned cart is the warmest cold moment in marketing: desire was proven, then interrupted. The buyer is living a small contradiction — "I wanted this / I didn't buy it" — and cognitive dissonance means they *want* that contradiction resolved. Your email decides which way: completing the purchase, or (if you wait too long) deciding they never really wanted it. That's why the 1–3-hour window is doctrine — you're re-entering an open decision, not restarting a closed one.

## How To Write It (trigger craft)
1. **Honor the 1–3 hour window.** Email 1 lands while emotional context is alive. Every hour after three, you're persuading instead of reminding.
2. **Frame as completion, not promotion (Zeigarnik).** "You're one step from done" — never "come back and shop." The task is 90% complete; offer the last 10%.
3. **Make loss aversion concrete.** Show the specific item(s) left behind — name, image, quantity. "Your cart" is abstract; "the Toolkit you selected" is a possession about to slip away.
4. **Resolve the dissonance in their favor.** Affirm the original judgment: "you picked this for a reason." Help them re-access why they wanted it.
5. **Hunt the real objection (trust/risk).** Most abandonment is hesitation, not rejection — price flinch, security doubt, unanswered question. Answer it: guarantee, support link, FAQ line.
6. **Deploy reciprocity before discounts.** A helpful answer, a bonus, free shipping — give first. Reflex-discounting trains your list to abandon carts on purpose.
7. **Sequence, don't repeat:** E1 (1–3h) = pure completion reminder → E2 (~24h) = objection handling + trust → E3 (~48–72h) = incentive or honest last call.

### Per-Buyer-Type Tactics (Abandoned Cart)
- **Spontaneous:** fastest send in the window; friction-free "finish in one click"; countdown framing. Trigger emphasis: **Zeigarnik + loss aversion.**
- **Methodical:** answer the unspoken objection; guarantee, security signals, FAQ link; no pressure. Trigger emphasis: **trust/risk reduction.**
- **Humanistic:** "did something come up?" human check-in; offer real help from a real person. Trigger emphasis: **reciprocity + trust.**
- **Competitive:** what they lose by waiting — price protection expiring, stock claimed by others. Trigger emphasis: **loss aversion** (status-coded).

## Use Cases
- E-commerce checkout abandonment (classic)
- Course/program enrollments started but not completed
- Application or booking forms left mid-flow
- Webinar registrations begun but unsubmitted
- Any measurable "started yes, didn't finish" moment

If a question stopped you, just reply — a real person answers within the hour. And everything's covered by the 30-day guarantee, so there's nothing to get wrong.

[Founder Name]

## Quality Checklist
- [ ] Email 1 timed within 1–3 hours of abandonment
- [ ] Framed as completing an almost-done task, not re-shopping
- [ ] The specific abandoned item named/shown
- [ ] Original buying judgment affirmed ("you picked this for a reason")
- [ ] Likely objection addressed (guarantee/support/FAQ)
- [ ] Reciprocity or help offered before any discount
- [ ] Discount (if any) reserved for the final sequence email
- [ ] Sequence roles distinct: reminder → objection → last call
- [ ] Tactics tuned to buyer type (see table above)
- [ ] Two A/B subject lines; preview <100 chars; founder's actual name signed
