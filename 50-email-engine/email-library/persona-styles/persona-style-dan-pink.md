## Style / Definition
The Dan Pink style is **social science with a raised eyebrow**: research-backed claims that **challenge conventional wisdom**, organized around the intrinsic-motivation triad of **Autonomy, Mastery, and Purpose.** Its rhetorical signature: "everything you've been told about X is not just slightly wrong — the science points the other way."

**⛔ Cardinal rule (all 12 persona styles):** NEVER mention the persona's name, never quote them, never reference their books/talks. Patterns only; content 100% original.

## Purpose & Intention
This style converts **smart skeptics** — readers who deflect motivational language but cannot resist a well-built counterintuitive argument. The mechanism is intellectual pleasure: the email delivers an "aha" that reorganizes something the reader thought they understood, and the offer arrives as the practical application of the insight. Because it sells through intrinsic motivators (self-direction, getting-better, mattering) rather than carrots and sticks, it's ideal for audiences numbed by bonus-stacking and countdown timers.

## How To Write It
1. **Open with the conventional wisdom — stated fairly.** "The standard advice for growing a practice is: work harder on lead generation." Steelman it; a strawman kills the reveal.
2. **Turn it over with evidence.** "But when researchers actually tracked what separates growing practices from stuck ones, effort barely registered. The differentiator was structural." Pattern-language and findings-language, honestly calibrated.
3. **Explain the mechanism through the triad.** Why do external pushes (rewards, pressure, hacks) fail? Because lasting performance runs on **autonomy** (self-directed work), **mastery** (visible improvement), and **purpose** (work that matters). Map the reader's stall to a missing element.
4. **Let the offer be the application.** "The Advanced Program is, structurally, those three elements installed": self-paced architecture (autonomy), skill-progression design (mastery), work tied to outcome (purpose).
5. **Respect the reader's intelligence procedurally:** cite the kind of evidence, admit boundaries ("this holds for complex work; routine work behaves differently"), invite verification.
6. **Keep wit dry and light.** One wry aside per email ("we've been bribing ourselves to do work we chose — and wondering why it stopped working").
7. **De-carrot the CTA.** No bonus avalanche. The pitch is the compelling nature of the work itself. Honor their previous purchase as evidence they're intrinsically driven (Upsell-2 rule).

## Use Cases
- High-ticket / Upsell-2 emails to analytical, B2B, or expert audiences
- Repositioning an offer after discount-fatigue has set in
- Selling mastery-based programs (skills, certifications, practice-building)
- Thought-leadership sequences that must persuade without hype
- Methodical buyers who need a mechanism before a motivation

## Detailed Example
**Framework:** ACCA carried in this persona style · **Objective:** Upsell

**Subject Line A:** {{contact.first_name}}, the standard growth advice fails on contact
**Subject Line B:** Why pushing harder stopped working, {{contact.first_name}} — the evidence

**Preview Text:** Conventional wisdom, meet the data. The data wins in three paragraphs.

**Email Body:**

{{contact.first_name}},

The standard advice for getting to the next level goes like this: set bigger targets, attach bigger rewards, apply more pressure. It sounds rigorous. It's practically corporate scripture.

There's just one problem: **when researchers actually study sustained high performance in complex work, that model doesn't merely underdeliver — it backfires.** External pushes work for simple, mechanical tasks. For work requiring judgment and creativity — your work — they reliably narrow focus and *reduce* performance.

We've been bribing ourselves to do work we chose, then wondering why it stopped working.

What the evidence keeps surfacing instead is a triad: **autonomy** (directing your own work), **mastery** (measurably getting better at something that matters), and **purpose** (a reason bigger than the invoice). When performance stalls — as yours may have — one of the three has usually gone missing, and no bonus fixes a missing element.

The Advanced Program is that triad, installed: you set the direction and cadence (autonomy), the skill-progression is measured and visible (mastery), and the entire build ties to outcomes you actually care about (purpose).

No bonus stack in this email — you already proved the drive is internal when you joined the first program. Just the application of a better model:

**[See how the Advanced Program is built →]**

Test the claim against your own experience. That's the only evidence that should convince you anyway.

[Founder Name]
