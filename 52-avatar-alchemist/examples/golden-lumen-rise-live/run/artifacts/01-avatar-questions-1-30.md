# Avatar Intelligence — 30-Question Profile (Lumen Rise Collective)

Ideal avatar: women founders in visibility and authority coaching for women founders who feel unseen.

## Demographic and Psychographic Profile

### Question 1: Name and archetype

Her name, for the purposes of this profile, is Diane Whitfield — though she answers just as readily to a hundred other names across the country. The archetype that captures her is **The Overlooked Authority**: a practitioner whose competence has outpaced her visibility, whose expertise is genuine and whose profile is faint. This archetype is defined by a specific gap, not a deficiency. She is not the striving beginner trying to earn her first credential, and she is not the loud self-promoter chasing attention she has not earned. She is the person who has already earned the standing and has never claimed it.

Within this archetype live two recognizable sub-types. The first is the Reluctant Expert, who is privately certain of her value but treats marketing as something slightly beneath the work itself. The second is the Quiet Builder, who has grown almost entirely by referral and word of mouth, and now feels the ceiling of that model — because referrals arrive on someone else's schedule, not hers. Both share the same signature: excellent at the craft, invisible at the threshold. The naming matters because Diane has spent years being described by what she is not — "not a self-promoter," "not pushy," "not out there." This archetype describes her by what she is: an authority the market has simply failed to notice yet.

### Question 2: Marital status and family

Diane is married, most often to a long-tenured partner with a stable career of his own — a school administrator, an engineer, a regional sales director — someone whose income steadies the household enough that her under-earning has never triggered a true crisis, which is part of why it has gone unaddressed for so long. The marriage is generally solid and quietly supportive; her spouse believes in her work in a general way but does not fully grasp why a woman this good is not better known, and occasionally asks a well-meaning question ("why don't you just raise your rates?") that lands harder than he intends.

She is raising school-age children, frequently two, somewhere in the range of six to fourteen. Her days are shaped around drop-off, pickup, homework, and the logistical tetris of two working parents. This family reality is not incidental to her business — it is load-bearing. It is why she guards her calendar, why she resents work that sprawls without paying more, and why "a calendar that fills itself" is not a slogan to her but a genuine longing. A subset of this avatar is divorced and co-parenting, which sharpens the income anxiety considerably; for her, under-pricing is not a quiet inefficiency but a direct threat to stability.

### Question 3: Location and lifestyle

Diane lives in the United States, most typically in a comfortable suburb of a mid-to-large metro — think the leafy edges of Atlanta, Charlotte, Dallas, Minneapolis, Denver, or Columbus — or in a well-off smaller city like Asheville, Boise, or Ann Arbor. She owns her home. She is not coastal-elite and not rural; she is squarely the professional suburban class, in a neighborhood with good schools and a farmers' market she means to visit more often than she does.

Her lifestyle is full but not lavish. She works from a converted spare bedroom or a corner of the finished basement, on Zoom for a good chunk of the day, in the gap between the morning school run and the afternoon one. She drives a paid-off SUV or a mid-tier crossover. Her routine has a rhythm: coffee and email before anyone else is up, client sessions clustered mid-morning to mid-afternoon, then a hard pivot into carpool, dinner, and homework. She exercises in fits and starts — a Peloton she is loyal to in January, a walking habit she keeps in the spring. She would describe herself as busy rather than fulfilled, and she notices, on the drive home from a school event, that the other parents have no idea what she actually does for a living.

### Question 4: Occupation and income

Diane is a founder and owner-operator of a service-based practice — a business coach, a leadership consultant, a fractional operator, a brand or marketing strategist, a health or career coach. She is the practice; there is no meaningful team behind her, perhaps a part-time virtual assistant and a contractor she uses for design. She delivers the work herself, one-to-one or in small groups, and her calendar is the actual constraint on her income.

Her business revenue sits between $90,000 and $250,000 a year, and this is the number that tells the whole story. By revenue she is a legitimate, established business, not a hobbyist. But she underpays herself, often taking home far less than a salaried peer with her expertise would command, because she chronically under-prices. She charges $150 an hour for work worth $400. She quotes a package at $3,000 that should be $9,000, then discounts it when the client hesitates. She has not raised her rates in two years because raising them feels like a claim she has not earned the right to make. The gap between her revenue and her take-home is not an accounting problem — it is a visibility and authority problem wearing an accounting costume.

### Question 5: Education and credentials

Diane is well-educated and, if anything, over-credentialed for how invisible she is. She holds at least a bachelor's degree, very commonly a master's — an MBA, an MSW, an M.Ed., or a graduate degree in psychology, communications, or public health. She frequently carries a professional certification directly tied to her practice: an ICF coaching credential (ACC or PCC), a PMP, a SHRM certification, a nutrition or health-coaching certification, or a niche methodology certification she paid real money and real weekends to earn.

Crucially, she keeps collecting credentials as a substitute for claiming authority. Another certification feels safer than another price increase, because a credential is externally granted while authority must be self-declared. She has a shelf — literal or digital — of certificates, and a running list of the next program she is considering. Part of the work Lumen Rise does is help her see that she crossed the threshold of "qualified" years ago, and that the next certification will not make her legible. Her positioning will.

### Question 6: Favorite quote

The line that lands hardest for Diane is Marianne Williamson's, the one she has half-remembered for years and kept in a note on her phone: *"Your playing small does not serve the world. There is nothing enlightened about shrinking so that other people won't feel insecure around you."*

She loves it because it reframes her core objection. She has always privately worried that stepping forward would be self-serving — a little vain, a little much. This quote tells her the opposite: that her smallness is not humility, and that it does not actually help anyone. She has also underlined Maya Angelou's *"People will never forget how you made them feel,"* because it names exactly the thing her clients already say about her and no stranger ever gets to experience. The quotes she returns to are all, at bottom, permission slips — arguments for visibility that let her keep her dignity intact.

### Question 7: Books, magazines, and blogs

Her bookshelf tells you who she is. The book she recommends most is Brené Brown's *Daring Greatly*, closely trailed by Susan Cain's *Quiet: The Power of Introverts in a World That Can't Stop Talking* — a title that describes her precisely — and Donald Miller's *Building a StoryBrand*, which she bought because she suspects, correctly, that her messaging is the weak link. She reads a business or self-development book roughly monthly and dog-ears more than she finishes.

For a magazine, she keeps up with *Fast Company* and skims *Forbes* online, drawn to founder profiles and the recurring fantasy of being the one profiled. She reads *Real Simple* for the part of her life that is not work. For blogs and newsletters, she is subscribed to James Clear's newsletter, reads Seth Godin's blog most mornings (his short posts on being remarkable feel aimed at her), and follows Amy Porterfield and Jenna Kutcher in the online-business space. She consumes far more advice than she implements, because most of it tells her to do more rather than to be legible.

### Question 8: Conferences and communities

Diane invests in rooms, though she often stands at the edge of them. She attends at least one industry conference a year — a coaching or consulting summit, a niche practitioner conference, or a regional women-in-business event — and she typically comes home with a full notebook and no follow-up, because networking-as-self-promotion is exactly the muscle she has never built.

She has paid to be in a mastermind or two, usually a mid-tier group program in the $2,000 to $10,000 range, where she is quietly one of the more capable people in the room and rarely the most visible. She belongs to several online communities: a Facebook group or Circle community tied to a course she bought, a local women's business association or chamber group, and possibly a professional body like the ICF or a trade association for her specialty. She values these communities for belonging and craft, but they have not moved her visibility, because everyone in them is also trying to be seen. What she has never had is a room built specifically to make her legible to the market outside it — which is the room Lumen Rise is.

### Question 9: Ten needs and problems

1. She needs a clear, sayable positioning statement — a way to describe what she does that a stranger can repeat accurately without her in the room.
2. She has a pricing problem: she under-charges relative to her results and does not know how to raise rates without losing the clients she has.
3. She needs a repeatable way to be visible that does not feel like bragging or performing, because performance is exactly what stops her.
4. She has a referral-dependency problem: her pipeline relies on other people's timing, so her income is unpredictable month to month.
5. She needs message templates and language she can reuse, because writing every post and email from scratch is why she posts so rarely.
6. She has a differentiation problem: to an outsider she looks interchangeable with a dozen other coaches, even though her actual work is not.
7. She needs a calendar that fills predictably rather than in feast-or-famine cycles tied to her sporadic marketing.
8. She has a time problem: her business must fit inside school hours, so anything that demands constant content churn is a non-starter.
9. She needs proof and social evidence organized in a way strangers can see, because her best testimonials currently live in private inboxes.
10. She has an identity problem: she does not yet think of herself as an authority, so she cannot yet ask the market to.

### Question 10: Ten goals and motivations

1. To have a fully-booked practice within 90 days, with a waitlist rather than a scramble.
2. To raise her prices to match the value she already delivers, and to pay herself what a peer with her expertise would earn.
3. To be genuinely known in her field — for her name to arrive in a room before she does.
4. To become referable by strangers, not only by past clients, so her reputation compounds without her constant effort.
5. To build a marketing rhythm that fits inside school hours and does not consume her evenings.
6. To finally stop discounting, and to hear a "yes" to full price without flinching.
7. To be the person profiled and interviewed in her niche, rather than the one taking notes in the audience.
8. To convert her private wall of testimonials into public, visible proof of her standing.
9. To feel a quiet, settled authority — certain of her worth without needing to shout it.
10. To secure her family's financial footing so her income reflects her competence, not her discomfort with being seen.

### Question 11: Deepest fears

Diane's deepest fear is that she will remain competent and unknown for the rest of her career — that she will do excellent work in obscurity until she quietly closes the practice, having never once been counted among the people who mattered in her field. She is afraid that visibility will cost her the very dignity that makes her trustworthy, and worried that if she steps forward she will finally be seen as the thing she has always dreaded being: too much.

Her specific fears include:

1. The fear of being perceived as a braggart — that promoting herself will read as arrogance and quietly cost her the respect of peers and family.
2. The fear of exposure and judgment — that if she raises her visibility and her rates, someone will decide she is a fraud who has overreached.
3. The fear of irrelevance — that younger, louder, more online competitors are lapping her while she stays politely quiet, and that her window is closing.
4. The fear of regret — that she will look back at fifty-five and realize her invisibility was a choice she kept making, and that she wasted a decade being the best-kept secret in a field that would have hired her gladly had it only known her name.

### Question 12: Truest desires

What Diane most deeply desires is to be legible and remembered — to be known as clearly and generously by strangers as she already is by the clients who love her. She wants the ease of a practice that fills itself, and she craves the specific, private relief of being recognized as the authority she has quietly been all along, without ever having to become someone louder or less herself to get there.

Her truest desires include:

1. The desire to be sought out — for ideal clients to arrive already convinced, so she never has to chase or convince.
2. The desire for quiet authority — to hold real standing in her field and carry it calmly, certain without shouting.
3. The desire to be paid in full and without apology, so her income finally reflects her competence.
4. The desire to be remembered — to leave a room and have her name repeated accurately, so her reputation travels ahead of her and does the work she has been doing alone.

### Question 13: Core objections

Diane arrives skeptical, and her doubt is reasonable — she has bought visibility advice before and it told her to hustle harder, which she rejects on principle and on capacity. Her hesitation is not about whether she needs help; it is about whether help exists that will not require her to become a performer. Her core objections include:

1. "This will turn me into a self-promoter, and that's not who I am." She doubts that visibility and dignity can coexist, and fears the whole thing is just permission to brag.
2. "I don't have time to become a content machine." She hesitates because every program she has seen assumes she can post daily, and her calendar is already governed by school pickup.
3. "I've tried this before and nothing stuck." She is skeptical of another course, another framework, another mastermind, because she has a shelf of them and remains overlooked.
4. "Maybe I'm not actually good enough to be that visible." Beneath the practical objections sits the quiet doubt that if she steps into the light, she will be found wanting — the fear dressed up as prudence.

## Synthesis

Everything in this profile resolves to a single throughline: **Diane does not need to be louder. She needs to be legible.** The market has not overlooked her because her work is weak or her belief is thin — it has overlooked her because her reputation does not travel, her positioning cannot be repeated, and she has quietly decided that being seen is the same as bragging. Those are not character flaws. They are solvable problems of language, structure, and permission.

This reframes the entire relationship. She is not a striver who needs to be built up from nothing; she is an established authority who needs to be made findable. Every fear she carries — the fear of arrogance, of exposure, of being too much — is really a fear that visibility and dignity cannot coexist. The Visible Founder Accelerator exists to prove they can: to give her positioning frameworks she can say out loud without flinching, message templates that make her easy to refer, and a rhythm that fits inside school hours rather than swallowing her evenings. The promise of a fully-booked, visible practice in 90 days is not a volume play. It is legibility applied — a calendar that fills itself because the market can finally see, name, and repeat what she does.

The emotional arc the brand must honor moves from recognition to relief to resolve. First she must feel seen: *yes, that overlooked, best-kept secret — that's me.* Then she must feel the relief that the fix is not more hustle, more credentials, or a new personality, but a way of being known that leaves her dignity intact. Then she resolves to claim the authority she already earned. Lumen Rise wins her not by promising to change who she is, but by promising the world will finally know her as she already is — competent, worth finding, and remembered. She was never the problem. Her invisibility was. And invisibility, unlike talent, can be engineered away.
