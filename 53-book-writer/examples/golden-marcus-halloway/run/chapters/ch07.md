# Chapter 7 — Feedback Without Fear

Feedback is the nervous system of a team. When it works, signals move fast, and the body corrects before it stumbles into a wall. When it fails, people walk straight into walls they could have seen coming. Every new manager knows feedback matters. Almost none of them were ever taught to give it in a way another human can actually use. So they do one of two things. They swallow the problem until it grows too big to swallow, then let it out sideways in a tense review. Or they soften the message so much that nothing survives the trip. The engineer walks away thinking the one-on-one went great, and nothing changes.

There is a better way. It begins with one uncomfortable truth.

Fear kills the signal.

## The cost of a scared team

Picture a code review from your old life as the star engineer. Someone left a comment on your pull request that read, "This whole approach is wrong." No explanation. No path forward. Just a verdict. Remember how your stomach dropped. Remember how, for the next hour, you were not thinking about better code. You were thinking about how to defend yourself. You reread the comment eight times looking for the insult hidden inside it.

That is what fear does. It hijacks the brain. When a person feels judged, the thinking part of them goes quiet and the protecting part takes over. They stop listening for the lesson and start listening for the threat. You can deliver the most accurate feedback in the world, and if it arrives wrapped in fear, the person cannot hold it. They are too busy holding themselves.

So the first job of feedback is not to be right. It is to keep the other person's mind online.

A scared team hides its mistakes. That is the real danger, and it is expensive. A team that fears you will not tell you the deploy looks shaky. They will not admit they do not understand the ticket. They will not raise a hand in the meeting to say the timeline is fiction. They will nod, go quiet, and let you find out the hard way. The information you most need travels on the exact channels that fear shuts down.

You cannot lead what you cannot see. And you will not see anything if people are afraid to show you.

## Feedback is a gift, not a verdict

Change the frame in your own head first. Feedback is not a grade you stamp on a person. It is information you hand them so they can steer. A grade is about the past and about them. Information is about the future and about the work. One closes a door. The other opens one.

Here is a small test you can run on yourself. Before you give feedback, ask, "Am I trying to be right, or trying to be useful?" If the honest answer is that you want to prove a point, wait. Cool down. Come back when your goal is the other person getting better, not you feeling vindicated. The smartest person in the room can win the argument and lose the engineer. You do not want that trade anymore.

## The Situation-Behavior-Impact frame

Vague feedback is cruel, even when it is kind. "You need to be more of a team player" gives a person nothing to hold. More of a team player how? Starting when? They will nod and change nothing, because there is nothing to change. You handed them a mood, not a move.

So use a frame. The one that has never failed me is called Situation-Behavior-Impact. Three parts, in order.

First, the **situation.** Name the exact moment. "In yesterday's stand-up, when we were reviewing the payments ticket." Not "lately." Not "sometimes." A specific place in time the person can walk back to in their memory.

Second, the **behavior.** Describe only what you saw or heard, like a camera would. "You cut Priya off twice before she finished her point." Not "you were rude." Rude is your judgment. Cutting someone off twice is a fact. Facts are hard to argue with. Judgments start fights.

Third, the **impact.** Say what it caused. "Priya stopped talking for the rest of the meeting, and I think we lost her read on the risk." This is the part people skip, and it is the part that lands. Behavior alone can feel like nitpicking. Impact shows the person why it matters, in terms they care about.

Put together, it sounds like this. "In yesterday's stand-up, when we reviewed the payments ticket, you cut Priya off twice before she finished. She went quiet after that, and I think we lost her read on the risk." Then stop. Let it sit.

Notice what the frame does. It never once says the person is bad. It sticks to a moment, an action, and a result. There is nothing to defend against, because you did not attack. You described. The engineer can hear it because their mind stayed online.

Run the same frame for good news, and it becomes rocket fuel. "In the incident call last night, you posted a running timeline in the channel every ten minutes. It kept six people from asking the same question and let me stay out of your way. That was leadership." Specific praise tells a person exactly what to do again.

## Praise in public, correct in private

The oldest rule in the book is still the best one. Praise in public. Correct in private. Both specific.

Public praise does two jobs at once. It rewards the one person, and it teaches everyone else what "good" looks like on your team. When you say in the team channel, "Sasha caught the race condition before it hit production by writing a test for the thing everyone assumed was fine," you are not just thanking Sasha. You are telling the whole team that careful testing gets seen here. Culture is built out loud, one specific compliment at a time.

Private correction protects the one thing you cannot afford to spend: a person's standing in front of their peers. Correct someone in a meeting and you may fix the bug, but you have taught the whole room that this is a place where you can be shamed. Everyone gets a little quieter. Everyone takes a little less risk. You traded a small win for a large tax.

So take it offline. A quick message. "Got a minute after this? Want to walk through the deploy with you." No audience. No stakes but the work itself.

Two warnings. Do not save up corrections for the big quarterly review. Feedback has a shelf life measured in days, not months. A surprise in a formal review is a failure of nerve that happened weeks earlier. And do not sandwich hard feedback between two compliments to soften it. People learn the pattern fast. Soon every kind word from you becomes a warning shot, and your praise goes worthless. Say the good thing because it is true. Say the hard thing because it is useful. Keep them honest and keep them apart.

## Receiving feedback is the whole game

Here is the part almost every new manager gets wrong. You think giving feedback is the skill. It is not. Receiving it is.

Your team is watching how you take it far more closely than how you give it. The first time an engineer tells you the truth and you get defensive, you have taught the entire team a lesson: telling you the truth is dangerous. They will not make that mistake twice. The channel goes dark. And you will have done it to yourself while believing you run an open door.

So model it. When someone gives you feedback, even clumsy feedback, even feedback that stings, do four things in order.

Say thank you. Not "thank you, but." Just thank you. You are rewarding the courage it took, before you react to the content.

Ask one real question. "Can you give me an example?" You are showing that you want to understand, not to win. The question also gives you something concrete to work with instead of a vague bruise.

Sit with it before you respond. You are allowed to say, "Let me think about that." You do not owe anyone an instant verdict on your own behavior. What you owe them is that you took it seriously.

Then close the loop. A week later, come back. "You told me I interrupt in stand-up. I have been counting. You were right. I am working on it." That single sentence does more for trust than a hundred speeches about being approachable. It proves the channel is real. It proves feedback goes somewhere when you give it to this manager.

Do that once, in public earshot, and watch what happens. People start telling you things. The deploy that looks shaky. The ticket nobody understands. The timeline that is fiction. The information you most need starts flowing again, because you made it safe to send.

## Make it small and make it often

The manager who saves feedback for big moments has already lost. Feedback should be as normal as saying good morning. Small, frequent, low-stakes. A quick "nice fix" in the review. A two-minute "hey, that landed sharp in the meeting, here is what I would tweak." When feedback is a constant light hum in the background, no single instance carries dread. It is just how the team talks.

Contrast that with the manager who is silent for six weeks and then schedules an ominous thirty-minute block. Everyone knows what silence-plus-calendar-invite means. Dread does the damage before you say a word.

Think of feedback like a lamp you keep lit low all day rather than a spotlight you snap on to interrogate someone. The steady, warm light lets people see the work and each other. The spotlight only makes them flinch. Quiet, constant, and specific beats loud, rare, and vague every single time.

## Run the whole conversation once, on paper

The first few times, the frame will feel stiff in your mouth. That is normal. Every new skill feels like a costume before it feels like clothing. So do not improvise the hard ones. Write the conversation out before you have it, the way you would rehearse a tricky demo. Three sentences is enough.

Say you need to talk to Priya, who has been shipping fast but skipping tests, and a small bug slipped to a customer last week. Here is the whole thing on paper. Situation: "In Thursday's release, the discount code fix." Behavior: "It went out without a test, and it broke checkout for about twenty minutes." Impact: "Support took nine tickets, and I had to pull you off the new work to hotfix it, which cost you the afternoon." Then the turn that matters: "What would it take to make testing the default on changes like this?"

Read it back and notice what it does not contain. No "you always." No "you need to care more." No adjective standing in for a fact. Every line is a thing that happened or a number you can point to. You are not building a case against a person. You are handing them a clear picture and asking them to help fix it. When the words are ready before the feelings arrive, you get to be useful instead of just being right.

There is one more move to rehearse: the pause. After you say the impact, stop talking. New managers rush to fill the silence, softening the point until it dissolves. Let the quiet do its work. The person needs a breath to move from defending to thinking, and your silence gives it to them. Count to five in your head if you have to. The most important part of the sentence is the part after it.

## Ask before you land it

There is a small move that makes hard feedback twice as easy to hear. Ask first. Before you deliver it, say, "Can I give you a bit of feedback on the demo?" and then wait for a yes. It takes three seconds, and it changes everything about what comes next. You are no longer ambushing someone. You are knocking before you walk in. The other person gets a breath to brace, to shift from doing into listening, and that small bit of consent turns your words from an attack into a favor they agreed to.

It does quiet work on you, too. If you have to ask for permission, you have to believe the feedback is worth asking for. Half the nitpicks that would have leaked out sideways never survive that little test. You pause, you weigh it, and you keep only the ones that matter. Theo told me once that the asking was the part that made him trust it. He knew that if I bothered to knock, the thing on the other side of the door was real.

Ask first. Then say the true thing.

## Written or spoken, choose on purpose

Not every piece of feedback needs a face. Small, in-the-moment nudges live perfectly well in a comment on the work. "Nice catch on the edge case." "This function is doing three jobs; want to split it?" Fast, low-stakes, and easy to act on. The work is right there, so the feedback lands on the work.

But the heavier the feedback, the more it needs your voice and your eyes. Tone does not survive text. A message that reads as calm in your head can read as cold on someone's phone at nine at night, and now they are stewing until morning with no way to ask what you meant. The rule is simple. If it could sting, say it out loud, where they can hear that you are on their side. If it is a small course correction, write it down, where they can act on it without a meeting. Match the channel to the weight, and half your feedback problems disappear before they start. High standards and psychological safety are not opposites. They are partners. The safest teams are also the most honest, because honesty is only possible where it is not punished. You can expect excellent work and make it safe to fall short on the way there. In fact, that is the only way anyone ever reaches excellent work: by being allowed to be bad at something first, in front of a manager who tells them the truth without making them afraid.

You are not lowering the bar. You are removing the fear that keeps people from reaching it.

This week, give one piece of feedback using Situation, Behavior, Impact — then ask one person how you can get better, and thank them for the answer.
