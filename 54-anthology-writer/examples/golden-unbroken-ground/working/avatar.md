## Niche + primary goal
Working people — mechanics, line cooks, shop owners, tradespeople — who tied
their whole identity to a business, a title, or a building, and are quietly
terrified of who they would be without it. The primary goal: give them language
for the grief of losing the thing, and a way to see that the thing was never
the same as the self.

## Questions 1–30 (identity, pain, transformation)
1. **Who is the reader?** A 40-to-60-year-old who built or inherited something
   physical and lost it — a shop, a route, a station, a farm.
2. **What keeps them up at night?** The fear that without the building they are
   nobody; that the town only knew them as "the Bell shop."
3. **What have they already tried?** Working harder, refinancing, holding on
   with both hands long after the numbers stopped working.
4. **What do they secretly believe about themselves?** That they are only as
   good as the thing they can point to.
5. **What transformation do they want?** To stand in a room, unattached to any
   title, and still know their own worth.
... (questions 6–30 carried in full from the aa-01 handoff: the reader's
   language, objections, the moment the padlock arrived, the blue Igloo cooler
   they carried out, the first morning with nowhere to open.)

## Questions 31–32 (research-informed context)
31. **Market/context finding:** Small-business closure grief is widely
    under-named; readers reach for "burnout" language because "identity loss"
    isn't offered to them. (Research pass — degrade-plus-flag if no search tool.)
32. **Competitive/voice gap:** No one is writing this in the plain, dignified,
    Studs-Terkel-oral-history register these readers actually trust.

## Rewritten avatar (aa-03 synthesis)
Marcus Bell's reader is a person who mistook the roof over the work for the work
itself. They do not need to be told to "pivot." They need to be shown, in
concrete detail — a certified-mail padlock, a cooler carried to a truck, a first
Monday with nowhere to unlock — that the skill, the steadiness, and the way they
treat people walked out of the building with them. The chapter must earn that
turn without slogans.
