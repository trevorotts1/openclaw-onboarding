Unnecessary
A first chapter, in the author's own voice

The first roof was not mine. I need you to understand that from the very beginning, because it turns out to be the whole story in miniature, and I did not understand it myself for years.

It belonged to a hardware store on the corner of a block that everyone else had quietly written off. The owner let me use it because the roof leaked anyway, and he figured a few crates of soil could not make it leak any worse. I climbed the fire escape with a bag of secondhand seed and a conviction I had not yet earned, and I told myself I was going to feed the neighborhood. Just me. One person, one roof, one impossible promise made to nobody but the wind.

I want to tell you about the night I nearly quit, because everything that matters came after it.

It was the end of my second summer. I had done everything right, or everything the books swore was right. I had crammed that roof with more beds than it should ever have held. I had worked until my hands cracked and split at the knuckle. And I sat up there in the dark at the end of the season, surrounded by the best harvest I had ever grown, and I did the arithmetic, and I understood with a sick certainty that it was not close to enough. Not for the block. Not for the families I passed every single morning. I had given a roof everything I had, and the roof had given everything back, and it still could not feed the people I had promised to feed.

I thought, that night, that the lesson was that I had failed. It took me a long time, and one nervous teenager two buildings over, to understand that the lesson was actually that I had misunderstood the entire assignment.

Because here is what I could not see from inside my own exhaustion. I had made myself the farm. Every seed, every harvest, every drop of water had to pass through my two hands, and so the size of the whole enterprise was capped forever at the size of one tired person. I had built myself a beautiful ceiling and then spent two years slamming my head against it, and I had called the bruise ambition.

What saved the neighborhood was not that I tried harder. It was that I finally, reluctantly, and with more grief than pride, gave the work away.

This book is about what happened when I stopped trying to be the one who fed everyone and started trying to be the one who taught everyone to feed themselves. It is about the strange sorrow of becoming unnecessary on purpose, and the even stranger joy that was waiting on the far side of that sorrow. It is about rooftops I have never once set foot on that are green tonight because of a single Sunday morning I almost slept straight through.

But before any of that, I have to take you back up the fire escape of that leaking hardware store, to the exact moment the teenager from two buildings over leaned over the parapet and asked me the question that took everything I believed about my own work and quietly turned it upside down.

I said yes to them for the wrong reason. I said yes out of guilt, on a Saturday I could not spare, and it turned out to be the most important yes of my entire life.

Let me tell you what they asked me.
