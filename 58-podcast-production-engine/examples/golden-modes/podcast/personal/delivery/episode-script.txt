[warm] I want to begin today with something I have never said out loud on this show. There was a winter, not so long ago, when I came very close to shutting off the kiln for good, locking the studio for the last time, and walking away from all of it without telling a single person why.

[reflective] From the outside, nothing looked wrong. The shelves were full. The lights were warm. If you had walked past the front window on a cold evening, you would have seen exactly what a small pottery studio is supposed to look like, rows of bowls the color of river stone, a woman bent over a wheel, steam on the glass. What you would not have seen is that I had stopped answering my emails, that I had been letting the phone ring out for weeks, and that most mornings I sat in the car in the alley behind the shop for a long time before I could make myself walk in.

[somber] I think I owe you the honest version of why, because the tidy version helped no one, least of all me. It was not one loud disaster. It was a slow, quiet erosion. A big wholesale order fell through in the autumn, and then the account I had leaned on for years closed without much warning, and somewhere in there my father got sick two states away and I started spending my nights driving instead of sleeping. None of it was cinematic. It was just a lot of small weights set down on the same shelf, one after another, until the shelf I had built my whole life on began, very slowly, to bow.

[honest] And here is the part I am almost ashamed to admit. The thing that frightened me most was not the money, though the money was real and frightening. It was that I had stopped feeling anything when I sat down at the wheel. The one place that had always given me back to myself had gone silent. I would center the clay, the way I have ten thousand times, and feel nothing at all, and that emptiness scared me more than any empty bank account ever could.

[gentle] I told no one. That was the mistake, and I want to name it plainly, because I think a lot of you are doing the very same thing right now behind your own warm front windows. I performed fine. I posted pretty pictures. I let everyone believe the light in the window meant the light was on inside me too. And the loneliest thing a person can do is stand in a room full of the work of their own hands and feel like a stranger to all of it.

[tender] The turn, when it finally came, was almost too small to notice. A woman named Ruth, who had been buying a single mug from me on the same week every year for as long as I had been open, came in one grey afternoon. She did not want to buy anything. She had heard, the way people in a small place always hear, that things were hard. She did not give me advice. She did not tell me it would be alright. She just sat down on the stool by the wheel, the one my students use, and she said, would you throw one while I sit here, I have always wanted to watch and never once asked.

[earnest] So I did. Not for an order. Not for the shelf. Not for anyone on the internet. I threw one bowl for one person who simply wanted to watch, and somewhere in the middle of it, with her quiet in the room and my hands finally busy again, the smallest thread of feeling came back. Not a flood. A thread. But I had been so empty for so long that a single thread felt like the tide coming in.

[reflective] I have thought a great deal since then about why that worked when nothing else had. And I think it is because for months I had been trying to save the studio, when what I actually needed was to be reminded why it was worth saving. Those are not the same task, and I had them backwards. I kept trying to fix the business as though it were a machine that had stalled, when the truth is it was never a machine. It was a room where people came to be quiet together and make something with their hands, and I had let the numbers convince me that the room was the least important part.

[resolute] So I made a decision that made no financial sense at all, and it saved me. I stopped, for a while, trying to grow the studio, and I started, instead, trying to deserve it. I called the students I had gone silent on. I said the true thing to the people who had noticed the light was off. I let a few of them see me not fine, which is the hardest thing I know how to do. And slowly, because I let them, they came back, and they brought the room back to life around me long before the ledger ever recovered.

[thoughtful] I want to be careful here, because I am not selling you a rescue. The money did not come roaring back the next morning. Some of those cold weeks stayed cold for a long time. What changed first was not the balance. What changed first was that I was no longer carrying it alone, and it turns out that a weight shared across a room is a completely different weight than the same load carried by one person in an alley at dawn.

[measured] If you are somewhere in your own quiet winter right now, I do not have a clever trick for you. I only have the one thing that worked, and I will hand it to you plainly. Let one person see the room with the light off. Just one. Not the whole world, not a confession to the crowd, just one person who has earned the truth, sitting on the stool while you do the thing you forgot you loved. The shame tells you that hiding is protecting you. It is not. The hiding is the whole illness. Being seen, by even one steady person, is the beginning of the cure.

[sincere] I kept the studio. I want you to know that, in case you were worried, because I would have been. The kiln is on tonight, and it will be on next week, and Ruth still comes in for her one mug on her one week every year, except now she stays longer, and now I let her.

[hopeful] So here is what I will leave you with, from the far side of a season I did not think I would come back from. The work of your hands is not the thing worth saving. The room is. The people in it are. You are. And if the light has gone out somewhere inside you while it still burns bright in your front window, please, before you lock the door, let one person sit down on the stool and watch you make one small thing. It may be only a single thread of feeling. But a single thread, when you have been empty long enough, is how the tide begins to come back in.

[warm] Thank you for staying with me through a harder one today. If it reached you, do not send me a message. Do the braver thing. Go let someone sit on your stool. I will be here next week, with the light on, in every way that matters.
