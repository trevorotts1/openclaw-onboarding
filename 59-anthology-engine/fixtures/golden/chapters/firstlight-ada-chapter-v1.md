# The Room Before the Room

The first office I ever rented had a lock that only turned if you lifted the
door on its hinge and leaned into it. Every morning for two years I did that
small, ridiculous dance, and every morning it reminded me that the thing I was
building did not yet hold its own weight. I had to hold it. That is the part no
one tells a first-time founder: for a long stretch, you are the load-bearing
wall.

I mentor early founders now, and the first question I ask is never about the
product. It is about the room before the room, the ten quiet minutes before a
pitch, before a hard conversation with a co-founder, before the payroll run that
you are not sure will clear. What do you do in that room? Because that is where
the company is actually decided. The deck is theater. The room before the room
is the operating system.

For me the answer took years to find. I used to fill those minutes with noise,
rehearsing lines, checking a dashboard that had not changed in an hour. What
finally steadied me was smaller and stranger: I would write down the single true
sentence I was afraid to say out loud. Not the strategy. The fear. "I do not
know if this will work." "I hired the wrong person and I have known it for a
month." Naming it did not make it false. It made it usable.

Discipline, I have come to believe, is not the absence of fear. It is the habit
of turning fear into a sentence you can act on. The founders who last are not the
brave ones. They are the ones who learned to lift the door on its hinge, lean in,
and let the lock turn one more day.
