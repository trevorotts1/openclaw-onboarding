# The Room Before the Room

The lock on my first office only turned if you lifted the door on its hinge and
leaned your shoulder into it. For two years I did that small, absurd dance every
morning, and every morning it told me the truth I was not ready to hear: the
thing I was building did not yet hold its own weight. I held it. That is the part
no one warns a first-time founder about. For a long stretch, you are the
load-bearing wall, and the wall does not get to rest.

I mentor early founders now, and I never open with the product. I open with the
room before the room, the ten quiet minutes before the pitch, before the hard
conversation with a co-founder, before a payroll run you are not certain will
clear. What do you do in those minutes? Because that is where the company is
actually decided. The deck is theater. The room before the room is the operating
system.

My own answer took years and one bad Tuesday to find. I had rehearsed a funding
call until the words went flat, and when the line connected I heard myself lying
brightly about a number I knew was soft. I lost the round. That night I started a
different practice. Before anything that frightened me, I wrote down the single
true sentence I was most afraid to say. Not the strategy. The fear. "I do not
know if this will work." "I hired the wrong person and I have known it for a
month." Naming the fear did not make it false. It made it something I could pick
up and carry on purpose.

Discipline is not the absence of fear. It is the habit of turning fear into a
sentence you can act on. The founders who last are rarely the bravest in the
room. They are the ones who learned to lift the door on its hinge, lean in, and
let the lock turn one more day.
