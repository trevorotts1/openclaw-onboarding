# The Apprenticeship of Saying No

I said yes to my first fifty clients. All of them. The desperate ones, the
disorganized ones, the one who paid in three currencies and none of them on time.
I told myself this was hustle. It was actually fear wearing hustle's jacket. A
young shop that cannot say no is not generous; it is undefended.

The lesson arrived as most lessons do, disguised as a good month. We had more
work than we could hold, and because I had never practiced refusal, I could not
choose. So we did everything at seventy percent, and seventy percent is the exact
temperature at which reputations quietly cool. A client I admired left without a
complaint, which is the worst kind of leaving, because there is nothing to fix,
only something to have prevented.

That week I wrote a single page I still keep taped inside a cabinet door. It lists
the three kinds of work we are built to do beautifully, and it gives me
permission, in my own handwriting, to decline the rest. The first time I read it
aloud to a prospect and said, "We are not the right shop for this, and here is who
is," something strange happened. He thanked me. He sent two referrals. Refusal,
done with care, is a form of respect.

I teach my crew now that the boundary is the product. The work inside the boundary
gets our whole attention because the work outside it never gets our half. Saying
no is not the thing you do instead of growing. For a small team, it is the
apprenticeship that growth is built on.
