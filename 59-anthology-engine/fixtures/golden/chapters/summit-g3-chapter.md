# Inventory of a Slow Recovery

The warehouse flooded on a Sunday, which felt, at the time, like the universe
adding punctuation to a sentence I had not finished reading. Nine months of stock,
most of it uninsured in the way that only optimistic small businesses manage to
be uninsured. I stood in four inches of water and did the math that tells you the
company is over. Then I did it again, because the first answer had been so clear
it frightened me.

What saved us was not resilience, a word I have come to distrust because it asks
the wounded to perform for an audience. What saved us was a list. Not a vision, not
a pep talk. A list of the next fourteen concrete things, in order, that a tired
person could do without deciding anything large. Pump the water. Call the three
customers whose orders we could still partly fill. Photograph everything for the
claim we would probably lose. Pay the two employees who showed up before I paid
myself.

Recovery, I learned, is not a mood you summon. It is an inventory you work. On the
worst days I could not feel hopeful, and I stopped requiring myself to. I only
required myself to reach item nine before I let myself sit down. Some mornings item
nine was the whole horizon.

We reopened fourteen weeks later, smaller and stubborner. I keep the flood list in
a drawer, laminated now, less as a trophy than as a translation: proof that when
feeling fails, structure can carry you the rest of the way to shore.
